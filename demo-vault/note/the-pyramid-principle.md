# The Pyramid Principle

At McKinsey, one of the lessons I learned was the importance of structured thinking and communication (View Highlight)

One of the tools that we used at McKinsey was the Pyramid Principle, a methodology for structured communication. (View Highlight)

The key take-aways from the Pyramid Principle at McKinsey were:

Start with the answer first.
Group and summarize your supporting arguments.
Logically order your supporting ideas. (View Highlight)

Start with the answer first. (View Highlight)

To communicate in a structured way with a busy executive, you should start with the answer to the executive’s question first, and then list your supporting arguments. (View Highlight)

This “top-down” structure is counter-intuitive for many of us, especially those with a scientific or engineering background used to writing technical papers. For many people, it’s natural to build up to a conclusion by first reciting all of the facts (View Highlight)

When an executive asked a question — “What should we do?” — you were to start your response with, “You should do X,” very crisply and directly. Only then, after you have answered the question, should you present your supporting reasons. (View Highlight)

Why (View Highlight)

First, you want to maximize your time with your audience (View Highlight)

In some cases, the executive may already mentally be at the conclusion you want them to reach (View Highlight)

Second, many executives often think in a “top-down” manner. They want to focus on the big picture—in this case the “answer”— and don’t want to get bogged down by details (View Highlight)

Finally, you are more persuasive when you are direct. By answering the executive’s question first, you sound more assertive and confident (View Highlight)

Group and summarize your supporting arguments. (View Highlight)

The Pyramid Principle advocates that “ideas in writing should always form a pyramid under a single thought. (View Highlight)

The single thought is the answer to the executive’s question. Underneath the single thought, you are supposed to group and summarize the next level of supporting ideas and arguments. Then, for each supporting idea or argument, break that further into more ideas or arguments until you have formed a pyramid (View Highlight)

It just so happens that the magic number of ideas in a group is three (View Highlight)

Logically order your supporting ideas. (View Highlight)

There are a few different ways of logically ordering ideas that belong in the same group: (View Highlight)

Time order: if there is a sequence of events that form a cause-effect relationship, you should present the ideas in time order.
Structural order: break a singular thought into its parts, ensuring that you have covered all of the major supporting ideas.
Degree order: present supporting ideas in rank order of importance, most to least important. (View Highlight)

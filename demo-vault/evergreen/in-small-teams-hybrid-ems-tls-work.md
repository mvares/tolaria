# In small teams, hybrid EMs + TLs work

The main responsibilities of engineering managers (more below 👇), revolve around 1) planning, 2) aligning stakeholders, and 3) taking care of the health and growth of the team.

[https://refactoring.fm/p/how-far-without-managers](https://refactoring.fm/p/how-far-without-managers)

In small teams, this might not equate to a lot of work, because:

- **Planning is straightforward** — it doesn’t require endless cascading goals through years and quarters, and you don’t need to juggle with resource allocation.

- **Alignment is easy** — your whole team works together on a single product, and external stakeholders are few. Piece of cake!

Hiring, onboarding, and taking care of people is still hard work, but hey, it’s basically everything you have to do!

So when your team grows to the point where you need to establish management behaviour for the first time, it is natural to look at people who already exhibited leadership traits—tech leads or senior devs—and ask them to take care of 1:1s, planning duties, and so on.

For these hybrid managers, however, tech work is still a big chunk of their days:

- They do 1:1s, but also weigh in design decisions.

- They help with planning but also do PRs and write critical code.

Now, it is important to understand that, for many, this isn’t a quick rite of passage. Most companies are not *hyper-growth VC-fueled* startups. Many remain at this stage *forever*. 

So these companies look at their team and wonder: “*how could we hire non-technical managers? Impossible!*” — and they are right.

Do you need full time engineering managers? 🤔

Let's sort this out.

The main responsibilities of engineering managers, revolve around:

1. planning

1. aligning stakeholders

1. taking care of the health and growth of the team.

In small teams, this might not equate to a lot of work, because:

1. Planning is straightforward — goals are simple and so is resource allocation.

1. Alignment is easy — the whole team works together on a single product, and stakeholders are few.

Hiring and taking care of people is still hard work, but hey, it’s almost all you have to do!

So when your team grows to the point where you need to establish management behavior for the first time, it is natural to look at people who already exhibited leadership traits—tech leads or senior devs—and ask them to take care of 1:1s, planning duties, and so on.

For these hybrid managers, however, tech work is still a big chunk of their days:

1. They do 1:1s, but also weigh in on design decisions.

1. They help with planning but also do PRs and write critical code.

Now, it is important to understand that, for many, this isn’t a quick rite of passage.

Most companies are not hyper-growth VC-fueled startups. Many remain at this stage forever. And that's... fine.

As you grow more, though, the management side gets heavier, and managers easily fill their days with non-technical work.

# The three questions of Platform Engineering teams

In the first year of forming a platform team, you’re probably not going to make a lot of major advances on the [CNCF platform engineering maturity model](https://tag-app-delivery.cncf.io/whitepapers/platform-eng-maturity-model/).

Instead, you’ll want to think about success through the establishment of a platform mindset on your team, and feedback from your customers and stakeholders.

How do you know if you have set yourself up right? You can ask yourself these questions:

![image](../assets/1a6bdf02815c80fd.png)

#### 1) Are you writing code, not just scripts, templates, or documentation?

If you’ve put together a balanced team, the team should be writing software.

You have identified a place where you can create leveraged value through your own custom software layer, and you’ve started building.

You may have taken over ownership of an existing platform or proto-platform from another part of the organization, or you may have been given ownership of a platform that was owned by volunteers in the past.

#### 2) Are you operating any critical systems?

Similarly, it’s not enough to be writing some code.

There should be systems that you actually own and operate. That could be just taking first line responsibility for a critical cloud system, or operating open source, but a common failure of new platform teams is that they don’t take on operational ownership of anything, which undermines their value to the rest of the company.

The platform mindset **blends software engineering and operations**. A team with no operational responsibilities may be very attractive to software engineers, but you won’t know if you’ve got a sustainable platform until you can show that you know how to operate foundational software infrastructure.

#### 3) Are you talking to your customers and stakeholders?

If you want to have the years it will take to build out mature platforms, you’re going to need the goodwill and patience of the rest of the company.

Some of that comes from operating things for them, which is why operations are so critical. Some of it might come in providing one-off support to fix problems in their systems, but this should be viewed as a small part of your customer engagement, not your primary customer interface.

Most of the goodwill should come from **listening to your customers**, creating product roadmaps that reflect their concerns, and showing those customers and their bosses that you are delivering reliably against those roadmaps.

This is product management, yes, and also stakeholder management: demonstrating that you’re creating broad value with the budget and headcount allocated to your team. I cannot stress how important it is that you share your goals and provide regular progress updates. Teams that neglect to do this run the risk of having their entire budget questioned with the idea that they’re just playing around and doing “*engineering for engineering’s sake.*”

Your application team customers are a market where you are trying to achieve product-market fit, in competition with their ideas about what they could do themselves and what they could get by managing their own vendor offerings or open source. The product part of your platform mindset is a survival instinct as much as it is about building the right thing.

Build both quick wins and strategic needs, operate something critical, talk to your customers, and advertise your work.

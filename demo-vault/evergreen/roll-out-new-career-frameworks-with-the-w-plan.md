# Roll out new career frameworks with the W plan

There are many company-wide systems that are challenging to put in place — think OKRs, engineering principles, or career frameworks — because they need to strike the right balance between being participated by the team (bottom-up) and following the vision of the leadership (top-down).

Both are needed:

- **Top-down direction** — delivers consistency and fast decision-making.

- **Bottom-up contribution** — makes things grounded with your team’s reality, creates consensus, and leads to better decisions in the end.

To achieve this, I am a fan of the [**W Framework**](https://review.firstround.com/the-secret-to-a-great-planning-process-lessons-from-airbnb-and-eventbrite) for this, by Lenny Rachitsky and Nels Gilbreth.

It is made up of four steps:

1. **Context** — leadership shares a high-level strategy with Teams

1. **Plans **— teams respond with proposed plans

1. **Integration** — leadership integrates into a single plan, and shares with Teams

1. **Buy-in** — teams make final tweaks, confirm buy-in, and get rolling

This is a generic strategy that you can apply to pretty much anything that requires wide participation.

![image](../assets/24bd1183d28d4630.png)

What does it look like in practice? Here is an example that I adapted to create and rollout a career framework:

1. **Context** — leadership provides high level direction: fundamental levels and tracks based on current team composition and future strategy.

1. **Plans** — teams (e.g. your senior leaders) come back with the qualities and responsibilities they would like to see for the various levels.

1. **Integration** — leadership challenges, integrates these elements and shares back with teams.

1. **Buy-in** — Final tweaks, Q&A + people get assigned to the levels.

When a company grows, it comes a time you have to introduce a career framework.

Rolling out the framework is a tricky moment because people are very personally and directly affected by it.

In my experience, a good rollout starts with a good creation process.

This is true for pretty much anything that is company-wide: OKRs, career frameworks, remote policies, etc.

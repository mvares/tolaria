# Freedom vs Rules is a fractal

I thought a lot about what to write in this conclusion, and I keep coming back to Hastings’ idea of the two options to structure an organization: **Rules & Process** vs **Freedom & Responsibility**.

Hastings makes the case for innovative companies to structure themselves around **F&R**, and makes a (now) famous analogy: the CEO is like the leader of a jazz band—the musicians (employees) need freedom and flexibility, but without the right conditions, the beautiful music turns into a loud mess.

I love this take. I believe it is *crucial* to have this conversation and promote F&R because, undoubtedly, we have been skewing towards R&P for decades, while innovation and technology need something else.  

#### Freedom vs rules is a fractal

However, I also believe that F&R vs R&P is a **fractal argument** that needs to be evaluated at many levels of granularity. It not only calls for different choices in different companies — it calls for different choices in different *areas* of the same company.

I am a musician myself, and the jazz band analogy definitely struck a chord (pun intended). In a jazz band you would leave your soloists free to create beautiful lines, but you would orchestrate drums and bass with rules & process. That’s because — to use Hastings words — drums are more about *safety and consistency*,* *while soloing is more about innovation.

Likewise, even on an elite team, you will always benefit from processes around those parts that benefit from safety & consistency. In engineering, think your release pipeline, or your QA process.

When I interviewed Kathryn Koehler, Director of Engineering at Netflix, she told me they do yearly and quarterly planning across the company. That’s process! Below that, teams design initiatives autonomously to achieve goals. That’s freedom!

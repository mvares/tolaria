# Freedom & Responsibility vs Rules & Process

Reed Hastings argues that modern companies have two main options for how to structure their organizations: rules-and-process, or freedom-and-responsibility.

1. **Rules and process** — is how most organizations work, and was born directly out of the industrial revolution. It prizes *order* over individuals’ freedoms*, *and it’s best for situations where maintaining consistency and safety is paramount.

1. **Freedom and responsibility** — prizes *flexibility**** ***over consistency. It allows people to move and adapt quickly, which is best for businesses that rely on innovation.

However, while both have their merits, for Hastings the success of most modern companies depends on being relevant and innovative, rather than safe and consistent, so they should pick F&R.

F&R, though, is a destination. To get there, you need to take care of three pillars:

- 🏅 **Talent Density**

- 📣 **Candor**

- **🔓 Removing Controls**

In the book, these three ideas unfold throughout three main chapters, which, however, are not organized the way you think.

Instead of covering one idea per chapter, each chapter goes through *all* of them, each time in a more advanced way. They work like *beginner*, *intermediate*, and *expert *levels of F&R.

The reason for this choice, I believe, is that the book wants to be both a *manual* about good culture (according to Netflix) and a recollection of the *Netflix evolution* through various stages 👇

So, the chapters read like evolutionary steps, and for good reason.

Today it is easy to take Netflix for granted as a modern tech giant, but the reality is: Netflix is older than Google. It was founded in 1997, and for more than *ten years* it only worked as a DVD rental service. 

It *reinvented* itself in 2007, as a streaming platform, and the rest is history (trivia: it has still been shipping DVDs [until last year](https://www.today.com/popculture/news/netflix-stop-shipping-dvds-rcna80319)).

So, the culture changed and grew together with its host, and the book structure (and all the stories and examples) wants to reflect that.

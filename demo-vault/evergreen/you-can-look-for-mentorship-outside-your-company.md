# You can look for mentorship outside your company

[Untitled](https://www.notion.so/cdcd6c8d298d406081860629e713513e). If you are not able to develop such relationships within your company, you can look for them outside.

Here are a few places you might look at:

- **Your network **— this is obvious and should be your first bet. Think at people in your network you would be happy to get mentored by, and reach out to them. You may ask directly if it’s ok for them to have periodic calls, to keep each other updated and get their advice. You will be surprised by how often people are happy to do that.

- **Communities **— engaging in communities is a great way to get “distributed” mentoring and get to know new peers you could build relationships with. This is exactly what we are going for with the [**Refactoring community**](https://refactoring.fm/p/community). If you haven’t already, you should [fill the survey](https://airtable.com/shrb2I6TW2QyDqtEZ) and apply to join! Other related communities are: [**Engineering Managers**](https://engmanagers.github.io/), [**Rand's Slack**](https://randsinrepose.com/welcome-to-rands-leadership-slack/), and [**Dev Interrupted**](http://devinterrupted.com/).** **

- **Networking apps **— apps such as [**Shapr**](https://shapr.co/) and [**Lunchclub**](https://lunchclub.com/)** **help you meet professionals who are relevant for your career. They create serendipitous encounters and work pretty well.

- **Professional coaching **—  there exist platforms where you can get professional coaching and mentoring. I do some coaching myself on [**BestPracticer**](https://bestpracticer.com/), which is very good. Other options include [**CTO Craft**](https://ctocraft.com/cto-coaching/)** **and private mentors (more in the resources section).

If you are not able to develop such relationships within your company, you can look for them outside.

# Choose boring tech

I have a bias for choosing **boring technology** — because it is predictable and it fails in *known* ways.

A lot of “exciting” tech turns out to be so only because of novelty and because we still haven’t figured out its limitations (looking at you, frontend frameworks?!).

![Known unknowns vs [unknown unknowns](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/There_are_known_knowns) — tech edition](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F79bf263e-62e4-42e5-9795-9e740f0c79fa_1312x842.png)

I am also a fan of the [*innovation tokens*](https://mcfunley.com/choose-boring-technology#:~:text=gets%20about%20three-,innovation%20tokens,-.%20You%20can%20spend) model, by [Mcfunley](https://twitter.com/mcfunley). 

It basically says: you can’t innovate on everything, so limit yourself to what is worthy. If your product / business is already unproven, better to couple it with proven technology.

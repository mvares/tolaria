# Talent Density at Netflix

The first pillar of Netflix culture is **talent density**, which means: *only hire and retain the best*.

> *A great workplace is stunning colleagues.
*— Reed Hastings

This rule is backed by a three-part belief:

1. An agile company needs innovators

1. Innovators are high performers

1. High performers thrive among other high performers

The last part is crucial. An A+ team makes everybody perform at their best, and helps with retention too. But it is also incredibly hard to achieve. Netflix developed very practical recipes about it, especially when it comes to **retention** and **firing**.

#### 🔒 Retention — Pay top dollar

Hiring great talent is hard, so once you bring it on board you need to do everything in your powers to retain it.

To do so, Netflix pays top-of-market salaries, and even encourages employees to regularly *window-shop* to figure out their true market value. If they receive a superior offer, Netflix is happy to match it.

Netflix *pegs* salaries to market value, to the point where it ditches traditional performance bonuses and raise schemes. If your market value increases by 20% in one year, you get a 20% raise. If you are doing an excellent job but your value stays put, you receive no raise at all.

In the book it is unclear how managers figure out *top market value* for their employees, outside of employees telling them after shopping around, but Netflix has indeed a reputation for paying top dollar, so I am not going to nit-pick.

#### 🚪 Firing — The Keeper Test

Netflix notoriously fires fast, and gave its approach a name: **The Keeper Test**.

Each manager is supposed to periodically run their directs through the keeper test. For each of them, the manager needs to ask herself whether, faced with the possibility of losing them, she would fight to retain them. If the answer is no, the person needs to be let go.

Hastings admits this approach is ruthless and has the potential to create fear and anxiety. People care about job security, after all.

Netflix counters this in two ways:

- **Sticking to A+ talent **— top performers are less concerned with job security because they know they can always find a new job in little time.

- **Removing the shame of being let go** — Netflix is upfront about creating very *transactional* relationships with employees. Hastings is fond of saying the company is like a professional sports team, rather than a family. In a family everyone backs one another no matters what, while in a sports team turnover is natural, and there is no shame in changing jerseys.

#### 📌 Luca’s take

I have found the **talent density** ideas to be the most controversial in the book. Controversial is probably not the right word here — it is just advice that not all companies can apply.

That’s because hiring well is, by definition, a zero-sum game. Just like *paying top dollar* is.

Netflix’s core belief here is that, if anything, top talent is *still* underrated*, *especially in tech, so you should kind of arbitrage that. In a nutshell: top engineers, paid top of the market, are still a bargain. 

You may or may not agree with it, but still, this is the cornerstone of Netflix culture, and *a lot* of the advice in the book, in terms of organizational principles, evolved by having this in mind.

# How to pick good project ideas

This isn’t strictly about startups: even at side-project scale, if the goal is to make something that makes sense as a business, you should ask yourself whether this idea is good, and whether it is good *for you*.

There isn’t wide consensus on how to answer this question. If you search online you will find plenty of hot takes about idea vs execution, picking the right market, and more. My personal litmus test is simple, and is based on two things:

1. **You are *****expert***** in the problem**

1. **You know how to *****build *****it**

Let’s look at both.

#### You are *expert* in the problem 🔍

Startups, and products in general, are solutions to problems, and you should pick a problem you are an absolute expert in.

The easiest way to do so is, of course, to pick one you personally have. You take something that really bothers you, whose solution you would pay for, and create it yourself.

But that’s not the only way. You may have worked in some space for years and found a gap — something that can be done better. You may not be the one who feels the pain, but you know well the people who do, and why.

#### You know how to *build *it* *🧱

You bring something to the table that helps to create the solution. This is usually about one of two things:

- 🔨 **Tech** — you know how to build the actual product, or a big part of it. You know your way around tech and design. 

- 📣 **Distribution** — you have some advantage at finding your ideal customers, vs the average Joe. Maybe you have worked with them, or you have an audience, or you are a big SEO expert and that problem is perfect for SEO.

#### Market vs execution risk ⚖️

The two factors above have different weights based on the kind of risk you face the most. All startups face two kind of risks:

- **Market risk** — you build something that nobody’s want.

- **Execution risk** — you are not able to build the solution.

There the more an idea, or a problem, is obvious, the less the market risk, and the more the execution risk that you face. If your idea is to create AGI (OpenAI), or to summon cabs in 2 minutes from your phone (Uber), the question is not whether people will want it, but rather if you are able to build it.

In fact we have to assume that all obvious — but unsolved — ideas are hard, otherwise somebody would have solved them already.

Conversely, there exist plenty of niche or weird ideas, for which execution is kind of trivial, but it is unclear whether you are looking at real problems, or simply nobody cares about it.

Market risk is defused by your *expertise *about the problem, while execution risk by your *build skills*.

So, all of this sums up in having a strong answer to the *why me* question. **Why am I the right person to do this?**

The best answer should be something like: *I am an expert in this problem and I know how to create something that solves it*.

Should you build that startup / project idea you have had since forever? 🤔

I have always started many projects, from small ones to big, VC-backed startups. Most of them failed, and, in hindsight, they were usually not good ideas to begin with.

So, over time, I developed a simple litmus test to figure out if I should move forward with an idea.

Even for side projects, if you want to create a business, you should ask yourself two questions:

1️⃣ Is this idea good?

2️⃣ Is it good for me?

In turn, these boil down to two key factors:

1) 🔍 𝗬𝗼𝘂 𝗮𝗿𝗲 𝗲𝘅𝗽𝗲𝗿𝘁 𝗶𝗻 𝘁𝗵𝗲 𝗽𝗿𝗼𝗯𝗹𝗲𝗺 — this doesn't just mean you've merely encountered the problem. True expertise comes from personally experiencing the pain, or knowing extremely well people who do.

2) 🧱 𝗬𝗼𝘂 𝗸𝗻𝗼𝘄 𝗵𝗼𝘄 𝘁𝗼 𝗯𝘂𝗶𝗹𝗱 𝗶𝘁 — this is about some unique advantage you can bring to the table. It should be one of two things:

↳ 𝗧𝗲𝗰𝗵 — you can build the product or a significant part of it

↳ 𝗗𝗶𝘀𝘁𝗿𝗶𝗯𝘂𝘁𝗶𝗼𝗻 — you know how to reach your ideal customers

These factors help balance the two key risks in every startup:

1) 🛒 𝗠𝗮𝗿𝗸𝗲𝘁 𝗥𝗶𝘀𝗸 — the danger of building something nobody wants. Mitigated by your deep understanding of the problem.

2) 🔨 𝗘𝘅𝗲𝗰𝘂𝘁𝗶𝗼𝗻 𝗥𝗶𝘀𝗸 — the challenge of actually building and delivering the solution. Reduced by your tech chops or distribution advantage.

A key insight here is that the more obvious an idea, the higher the execution risk. After all, if it was easy + obvious, someone would have done it already. Think Uber or OpenAI — the demand is clear, but building it is the challenge.

Conversely, the more niche or unconventional the idea, the more the risk shifts on the market side: is this even a real problem?.

So, ultimately, this all comes down to answering one question:

𝗪𝗵𝘆 𝗮𝗺 𝗜 𝘁𝗵𝗲 𝗿𝗶𝗴𝗵𝘁 𝗽𝗲𝗿𝘀𝗼𝗻 𝘁𝗼 𝗱𝗼 𝘁𝗵𝗶𝘀?

The ideal answer should be: "I'm an expert in this problem AND I know how to create a solution for it."

If you can confidently say this, you might be onto something.

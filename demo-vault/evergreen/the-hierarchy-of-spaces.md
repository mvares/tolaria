# The hierarchy of spaces

Speaking of productivity vs carving out space for yourself, Jean told me about her personal experience, which she also covered in a [two-part series](https://jeanhsu.substack.com/p/space-to-do-whatever) in her newsletter.

She remembers a time, years ago, when she felt extremely exhausted and didn’t want to engage in creative things anymore. She always thought of herself as a creative person, so she wondered: *maybe I am not creative anymore*?

Over time she realized that nothing had changed — her need for creativity and self-expression would still emerge, but there were other ***needs*** and **spaces** that needed to be fulfilled before she could go to the next level.

So she came up with a **hierarchy of spaces**, similar to Maslow’s hierarchy of needs.

1. **Space to not be exhausted** — this is about your basic needs, like good sleep and eating well. Even basic needs can be challenging with young kids and demanding work, but if you don’t take care of those, you can’t move up to the next level.

1. **Space to do whatever** — this is about giving yourself the permission to spend some *unproductive* time. Like watching Netflix, or doing absolutely nothing.

1. **Space to enjoy** — only once you have some spaciousness to do whatever, you can engage in activities you like and enjoy doing. These might be games, hobbies, and anything that makes you happy.

1. **Space to be creative** — this is about activities that let you express yourself in a way that is uniquely *you*. Jean believes that true creativity can only emerge once you have satisfied your other spaces.

Personally, I have found that the lower you are in this hierarchy, the harder it is to rationalize the need for such a space. E.g. it is easier for me to justify time spent on activities I genuinely enjoy — e.g. sim racing, or playing guitar — rather than e.g. watching Netflix, which looks more like **space to do whatever**.

But even with stuff I do for pleasure I sometimes have the temptation of finding a **productive** angle, like setting targets or goals.

One time, about a year ago, I set a quarterly goal of improving my ranking on [iRacing](https://iracing.com/), kind of like a personal OKR, with precise targets and everything. Fortunately, soon I looked at it and thought “*what am I doing?!*” and scrapped it.

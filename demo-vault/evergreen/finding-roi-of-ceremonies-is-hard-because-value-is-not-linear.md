# Finding ROI of ceremonies is hard because value is not linear

As managers, we always say we should cut meetings whose value is low, that is, lower than the opportunity cost of attendees' time.

In my experience, though, this is tricky to judge. 

The ROI of ceremonies is often not linear:

- Daily standups may feel like a waste of time until you casually intercept a big blocker.

- Retrospectives may seem shallow until a project goes bad and it prompts a real discussion.

Some processes are simply designed to run *at a loss *most of the time, and then have a few instances that repay the whole cost.

I feel that, as humans, we are bad at assessing these. When value distribution is uneven we need *longer* feedback loops — and we don’t like that.

This is one of the cruxes of moving from IC to management. As an engineer, most feedback loops are extremely short: you write code, it works, deploy to prod, check how it goes. As a manager, good luck with that! When you design how your team works—as a system—you need many cycles to figure out how things are doing. And a full cycle goes from minutes/hours (coding) to weeks (management).

I wish a good start of the week to all managers out there! ❤️

---

No one wants to waste their time on useless meetings or ceremonies, so, for each of them, it is useful to try to figure out their return on investment. As Louis Bennett from Reforge says:

> If a team’s ceremonies create more value than the opportunity cost of its attendees’ time, then the ceremonies are arguably not overhead. And… the converse is also true.

However, in my experience, this can be hard to assess, because in most ceremonies the *cost component* is constant, while *value* is not.

Example: I may find that daily standups are 80% of the time not worth the hassle. But roughly **once a week** you may have one where you discover a crucial roadblock, and that value possibly repays the cost of all the other standups.

Same goes with retros, 1:1s and other processes that look unnecessary until they don't.

As humans we are bad at assessing situations where the value distribution is uneven. We only get better with experience.

# Being a Generalist in 2025

### 📑 Materials

---

[https://refactoring.fm/p/generalists](https://refactoring.fm/p/generalists)

### ✍️ Post

---

About four years ago, I published [an article about generalists](https://refactoring.fm/p/generalists) which was part advice part self-therapy. 

In fact, having been a founder for most of my career, I have a love/hate relationship with my *skills*:

- I love doing—and learning—many different things.

- I dread not being capable of top-notch execution on pretty much *anything* I do.

In the industry we are fond of saying we should *balance* our specialist and generalist sides by building a T-shape, that is: solid foundations across the board + one strong specialty.

We also often hear that is good to build the horizontal leg during the first years of your career, before specializing into something. This is good advice! But it also sounds kind of like: go on and play with things for a few years, but then get your sh*t together and figure out what you want to do as a grown up.

Many people do indeed, but some don’t, and end up with *shapes *that look more like a rake, with various vertical legs of odd, different lengths, protruding from the horizontal base.

![image](../assets/27dbdf02815c80ce.png)

This is old news and, by now, we shouldn’t overly worry about it. There is plenty of data out there about how generalists eventually do very well in their careers, and above all I am fond of [Range](https://www.goodreads.com/book/show/41795733-range), by David Epstein, which basically reads like an ode to generalists.

However, this reassurance never felt completely satisfactory to me.

My rampant impostor syndrome has often demanded *more*, which is why I wrote the previous article in the first place — to address how generalists actually *fit* software organizations, and how they should think about themselves.

Fast forward four years, I think it’s time for an updated take, largely because of AI. It is no secret that lately I have been writing a lot about this, and one angle I am particularly interested by is how AI is changing what skills are relevant, and how teams are going to change because of it. Of course it’s still early to say, but some trends are already in place, and I think it’s worth talking about them.

So here is the agenda for today:

- ❓ **What is a generalist? **— as always, definitions first.

- 🎨 **Product engineers **— the rising stars of generalists.

- 🎽 **Engineering managers **— a classic destination which still 100% stands today.

- 🏗️ **Staff+ engineers **— growing your generalist career as an IC, a surprisingly viable option.

- 🏛️ **Applying your domain knowledge **— attacking the problem from another angle.

Let’s dive in!

---

### ❓ What is a generalist?

Generalists come from all walks of life, and it’s hard to find a proper definition of *what makes* for a generalist. I asked some friends about *why* they thought they were generalists, and here are some of the most common answers:

- *You are equally (or similarly) proficient in various non-overlapping skills.*

- *When looking at openings, you can apply for roles that belong to different career tracks.*

- *During your work, you regularly perform tasks that usually fall into the domain of different specific roles.*

The problem is these things exist on a spectrum. Full-stack engineers may feel like generalists themselves, but they are arguably less so than a good friend of mine who started their career as a pre-school teacher, turned into customer success operator, QA engineer, and eventually engineering manager. 

There is also this common notion that people end up generalists largely because of accidental twists and turns in their careers. It may be the case for some, but I don’t think it’s how it happens for *most.*

We follow our inclinations more than we realize, and, as Steve Jobs said, often times you can only connect the dots *backwards*, and see that your journey makes sense in a way that you didn’t realize yourself. So, I have known a lot of *generalists*, and here is an observation: the vast majority of them is motivated by the *what*, rather than the *how*. They are **motivated by the impact** and the value they create, rather than building stuff for the sake of it.

For the sake of clarity: I have also known a lot of people motivated by the *how*—by **doing things well**—and they are super valuable. They just happen to turn more often into specialists.

Another interesting angle goes back to Daniel Pink’s model about motivation — which I have probably quoted on the newsletter more than any other mental model. Pink believes our motivation depends on three factors: **Autonomy**, **Mastery**, and **Purpose**. In real-word situations, these are often at odds, especially autonomy and mastery. Being more autonomous requires being able to do more things, which naturally creates a tension with the level of mastery you are able to achieve in each domain.

Now, autonomy and mastery might be at odds, but **impact***** *****and mastery** are not, or should not be. In fact, in healthy teams impact comes first, while mastery is subordinate to it.

This is true at all levels, and even in your career as a whole. With the exception (maybe) of the few initial years of their career, people get largely promoted because of the impact they produce, rather than the skills they possess. And on your resume, what you helped accomplish always speaks for your skills *more* than anything else you can put in there.

Now, I feel a big difference between generalists and specialists is that this mindset comes easy-ish to the former, while the latter need to come to terms with it. In fact, if we value ourselves, as professionals, by the level of quality, complexity, and sophistication of the software systems we create, we might be disappointed e.g. to take a design shortcut to improve the time to market, at the expense of some tech debt down the line.

Now, how does all of this apply to modern tech teams? Let’s look at various roles and angles.

---

### 🎨 Product engineering

If we had to compile a chart of the biggest software engineering trends of this year, AI would surely top it, and I would put product engineering as second.

I wrote several times about it, so I am just linking to past articles:

- [How to Build a Product Engineering Culture](https://refactoring.fm/p/how-to-build-a-product-engineering)

- [How to Become a Product Engineer](https://refactoring.fm/p/how-to-become-a-product-engineer?utm_source=publication-search)

In a nutshell, teams that work in *product engineering mode* employ full-stack engineers with product sensibility, and empower them to own and ship features from top to bottom.

For early stage startups this is nothing new — they have worked like this for a long time, and not only for productivity: the economics of splitting engineers in frontend, backend, etc, don’t make a lot of sense when your headcount is small. 

What’s different now is that more and more mid-to-large orgs are experimenting with assembling teams that work this way. AI also accelerated the trend, by making it easier to work across multiple layers even when you are not 100% proficient in them.

So, there’s no question product engineers are *generalists*, but does this mean we are ditching specialists here?! Not at all! But specialists are evolving, too.

While in the past you would put together e.g. frontend and backend specialists to create a new feature, these experts are now more valuable to **improve their respective platforms **so that product engineers can keep working at max velocity.

In other words, specialists *pave the way* to the generalists, so the latter can run faster.

![The product engineering workflow is a bit like curling. Product engineers are the stone, that has to move as fast as possible, while specialists act as *sweepers*, creating the platform and tools to enable product engineers go fast.](../assets/27dbdf02815c808c.jpeg)

Think about it: your best frontend engineer’s time is likely better spent on improving your component system, or reducing bundling times for everybody, rather than creating yet another form.

This way, specialists can do what they do best: focus on quality and act as *taste-makers*, empowering the productivity of the rest of the team.

This makes sense on all levels:

- ⚡ **Velocity **— little coordination is needed.

- 🔬 **Quality **— good platforms are built and maintained this way.

- 🎯 **Personal aspirations **— both generalists and specialists focus on rewarding work.

- 📈 **Scaling **— you scale generalists for throughput and specialists for quality. 

Also, this is not happening for coding only, but at all levels of product development. I believe a healthy mental model for professionals across all the stack is to focus on continuously *reducing* the scope of our work. We can ask ourselves:

- *What is hard about my job?*

- *How can I make it less hard?*

- *How can I create systems / platforms / procedures that simplify the work I do?*

- *How can I make some parts of my work easier and more well-understood, so I can delegate them to generalists (and in part to AI?)*

And, in turn, we *re-expand *this scope by taking on more strategic work, which replaces what we have delegated and automated.

### 🎽 Engineering Managers

While product engineering is relatively new, there is another domain, instead, where generalists have thrived for *decades*: **management**.

Engineering managers are the ultimate generalists, who need to fill their team’s gaps wherever needed. This may include: good judgment to facilitate design conversations, systems thinking to improve processes and workflow, and people’s skills to perform your classic management duties.

Not all EM roles work this way, with some letting you stay closer to your specialist skills (e.g. being an EM of a specific function), but still, transitioning to an EM role **grows your duties in** **breadth**, rather than in** **depth. If your mastery was already often secondary as an IC, here it 100% takes the back seat, as your personal outcome becomes the outcome of your team.

EMs come from many walks of life, but in my experience the transition is easier if you come from a generalist experience.

### 🏗️ Staff+ Engineers

Good generalists can *also* grow their careers as ICs, under the right conditions. Engineers who can cut through the whole stack and think at the system as a whole are best positioned to spot big, cross-team opportunities for improvement.

These can materialize as refactoring initiatives, big buy vs build decisions, or complex organizational issues. Staff+ engineers are usually associated with deep expertise, which is often right, but these are also flexible roles that take the shape of what’s needed in your specific org.

Will Larson wrote a [fantastic article](https://staffeng.com/guides/staff-archetypes/) about Staff engineer archetypes, which includes at least a couple of generalist-ish ones. In particular, I have seen many *Right-hands* during my career: 

> *Folks in this role attend their leader’s staff meetings and work to scale that leader’s impact by removing important problems from their plate. Problems addressed at this level are never purely technical and instead involve the intersection of the business, technology, people, culture, and process. Right Hands often dive into a fire, edit the approach, delegate execution to the most appropriate team, and then pop over to the next fire elsewhere in the organization.*

### 🏛️ Bonus: deep domain knowledge

Finally, an underrated way to grow your career as a generalist is by focusing on industries with deep domain knowledge. 

If you had a diverse set of roles but mostly in e.g. finance, healthcare, or crypto, you can often have a shot at specific adjacent roles in that same industry, compensating your lack of experience in the role itself with your experience in the space, which may take years to acquire otherwise.

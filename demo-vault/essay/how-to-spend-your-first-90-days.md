### ✍️ Post

Joining a new company is hard.

In September 2020, after 8 years as co-founder and CTO of Wanderio, I quit to join Translated.

I went from a place that I had shaped myself, where I knew all the ins and outs, to one that was completely foreign.

Such transitions are even harder than they used to be, because of **remote work**. In fact, for as many upsides we can find in remote, building relationships with your new-found teammates is not one of them.

To make this easier, it is good practice to create a plan for your initial time at the company, setting goals and expectations. This is usually done for the first 90 days, and you may set checkpoints every 30 days, so you get what is commonly called a **30-60-90 plan**.

In my experience, your manager may or may not work with you on such a plan. Some managers basically create it for you, while others stay hands-off and expect you to take more initiative.

This is not just personal preference — the reality is many companies don’t have good onboarding processes. You might find that everyone is busy with their job and your manager is not going to help you in a meaningful way.

That said:

- **As a new hire** — it is important that you create such a plan in any case.

- **As a manager** — you should sit down with your report and create the plan together.

This article provides a simple framework for creating a 30-60-90 plan and setting yourself (or your new report) up for success. We will cover:

- **🤹‍♂️ The Learning / Performing / Personal model **— the three main types of activities** **that you are going to spend your time on.

- **🗺️ How to structure the plan** — in themes, goals, and measures.

- **🗓️ What to do in the various stages **— from day 0 to 30, from 30 to 60, and from 60 to 90.

- **✏️ Templates** **and examples** — that you can use to get started.

- **📚 Readings and resources** — as usual, to learn more.

Let’s dive in!

### 🤹‍♂️ Learning / Performing / Personal

A useful framework to reflect on how to spend your time as a new hire is to organize activities into three broad categories:

- **📚 Learning **— absorbing anything about the business, product, process, etc.

- **⚡ Performing **— executing things that have a positive impact.

- **🏃‍♂️ Personal **— creating relationships, building trust, and getting yourself comfortable with the culture.

Other frameworks just draw the learning vs performing comparison. I consider *personal* as a third category on its own because I believe you need to be intentional about it — especially in remote environments.

How you spend your time between the three categories also varies with time.

If at the beginning you usually enjoy a **honeymoon*** *phase where you are given more freedom to learn, while this balance rapidly shifts in favor of **execution** by the third month or so.

Expectations rapidly shift from learning to execution over the first 90 days

![image](../assets/3bd0d56c23d4470f.png)

So, let’s see how you can create such a plan 👇

### 🗺️ How to structure the plan

Creating the 30-60-90 plan is an opportunity to make the new hire comfortable with the method the company uses to set goals.

The best format for the plan is the one you are already using for other team and individual goals. Do you use OKRs? Go with OKRs. Do you do something more informal? Let’s keep it informal.

For each 30-days period, here is a simple way to organize activities:

- **Theme **— define an over-arching theme for the period. Over the stages this might shift from e.g. *learning*, to *performing your role independently*, to *coming up with a solution* to a proposed problem.

- **Goals** — write 2-3 bullet points about what you want to achieve.

- **Measures** — for each goal, write down how you measure success.

Overall, this is very similar to OKRs, with a theme on top. If you are a manager, you may use this hierarchy to ensure alignment and give gradual autonomy, by:

- Providing the theme yourself

- Collaborating with the new hire on goals

- Letting them come up with the measures

### 1️⃣ What to do in days 1 → 30

The first 30 days are for learning and getting yourself comfortable in the company.

You can focus mostly on the *learning* and *personal* areas.

About learning, absorb as much as possible of the **global context**. That means the business, product, culture, teams, processes. Figure out what really matters.

Here are a few things you may want to focus on:

#### 🏋️‍♂️ Training

As a manager of a new hire, you should guide this by providing meaningful training. [Kishan Sampat](https://www.linkedin.com/in/kishan-sampat/), Engineering Manager at Dimagi, told us how they do it:

> In the first week, we work with the new hire to have them complete HR trainings such as training on harassment and discrimination prevention, data security and privacy training, etc.Additionally, we also focus on communicating their benefits and get them familiarized with how internal communication works as well as what the company culture is like.The goal here is to enable the new hire to feel as comfortable as possible to navigate the new changes that they may be going through, as well as create an environment for them to be able to reach out to the right people when they have a question or need help.

About your *personal*** **activities, invest time in creating relationships and building trust.** **You can do this in various ways 👇

#### ☕ 1:1 with teammates

Have 1:1s with all the people in your team, and with key stakeholders in the company. Prepare some questions to make this both a moment of bonding and learning. Some good ones are:

- *What should I know about the product / company / people?*

- *What is the biggest challenge that you face?*

- *What can I do to make your life easier?*

- *Who else should I talk to?*

Kudos to [Linda](https://twitter.com/thelindazhang) for coming up with these questions 🙏

#### 🧊 Ice breakers

As a manager, facilitate conversations by creating ice breakers moments, like team chats, personal presentations or fun projects.

More insights by Kishan:

> At the end of the second / third week, we also present an opportunity for the new hire to give a small presentation (what we call a "brownbag") about themselves to their team and business unit.Additionally, we also encourage some new hires (depending on their role) to build a "fun-application" using our product. This is a way for them to be familiarized with the product features by building something of their choosing.

#### 🔀 Align with your manager

The relationship with your manager is the most important one you have within the company. Spend some time figuring out how to work well together.

A few topics might be:

- *What does success look like in the first 90 days?*

- *What is the best way to communicate / work with you?*

- *Anything you like / dislike or anything I should know about you?*

As a manager, it is helpful to write your own “*guide to me” *document and share it with your new reports. Rands has a great one 👇

- 📑 [**How to Rands**](https://randsinrepose.com/archives/how-to-rands/)

### 2️⃣ What to do in days 30 → 60

These are the days where on one side you are still learning, but on the other side you are already figuring out how to contribute.

In particular, your learning should shift from the **global** context to the **local** one — the one of your team. Figure out how your team works, how they measure success, and how you can help them.

Set yourself up for a win by identifying something 1) **impactful**, 2) **low-risk**, and 3) **not controversial**, that you can get done within the rest of the 90 days. Here is some advice on how to choose these projects:

- **🐄 Avoid the sacred cows **— all companies have some strong beliefs, justified or not, that you better not question early on. You want to find something that doesn’t piss anybody off.

- **🔍 Check with your manager **— your manager might already have something in mind for you to take on. For managers: yes, you should.

- **🔌 Fill in the gaps **— find something nobody owns. That is the best way to create value without stepping on someone else’s shoes.

Here are a couple of other things you may try:

#### 👻 Shadow teammates

Spend some time side-by-side with relevant teammates — might be other devs, or stakeholders of the product — to understand how they work and what their pains are.

This is both a learning time and an opportunity to find problems to address.

#### 👺 Take on the unsolvable

[Camille Lahoud](https://www.linkedin.com/in/camille-lahoud-11864618/), CTO at Base Alpha, shared this great tip with the community:

> I like to […] pick an “unsolvable” task, something that has been pending for a while or posing a challenge to the team; these tasks will teach you the most about the limitations / edge cases of the project, and the pain points that the team is facing.

### 3️⃣ What to do in days 60 → 90

In the last 30 days the honeymoon is over, so you want to get some wins under your belt.

In particular, you want to deliver on the commitments that you set. People watch for your **say / do ratio** and you want to build credibility.

#### 🏆 Quick win

Wins take many shapes:

- **Small feature **— deliver something meaningful for the product.

- **Process improvement **— automate some parts of a process (= save someone else’s time) or make a process more effective.

- **Take over something **— take ownership of a small piece of the product / process, freeing someone else from managing it.

Delivering wins is not the only thing you should pay attention to. Here are other areas you should cover:

#### 🗳️ Feedback from manager

After 90 days, it is time to get some structured feedback from your manager.

You should get this regularly on a frequent basis, but in case you haven’t so far, you should raise your hand and ask for it directly.

Look for specific, actionable feedback that you can use to improve what you do. I wrote more about feedback in a previous article:

- 🌀 [**How to Give Feedback**](https://refactoring.fm/p/how-to-give-feedback-?s=w)

Feedback is the starting point for what comes next 👇

#### 🗺️ Roadmap for next months

Work with your manager on what you will be working on in the next few months. Define what success looks like, for you and for your team.

#### 🌱 Vision for your growth

Align expectations on your career growth path.

You now know enough about the company to figure out some direction you want to go yourself: check in with your manager’s ideas, and adjust your respective goals.

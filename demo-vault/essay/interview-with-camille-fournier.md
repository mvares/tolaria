### 1) 🏢 Founder mode vs manager mode

We kicked off the chat by discussing Paul Graham's recent essay on "[Founder Mode](https://paulgraham.com/foundermode.html)".

Camille points out that the essay presents an overly simplistic view of "*founder mode*" versus "*manager mode*". Real-world management is far more nuanced, with even large corporations often operating outside the stereotypical "manager mode" described.

Camille believes a lot of this talk is driven by the widespread desire, among technical leaders, to find a foolproof *algorithm* to run and scale companies. She is skeptical about it 👇

> *Leadership is not that. And so I absolutely do agree that, you know, if you think that there is an algorithm whereby you hire good people and you treat their teams like black boxes and you, the founder, I don't know actually what the founder is doing in this case... I think you're absolutely right that that's not going to end well for you.*

So, Camille warns *both* against the extremes of detached management, and excessive micromanagement. Her advice is to strike a balance: stay engaged while trusting your teams, focusing on results rather than constant oversight.

Of course this is hard to achieve, but there are no shortcuts.

### 2) 👨‍💼 Good management for founders ([09:16](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aeYzFLzs4jo&t=556s))

Camille discusses the evolution of management practices and scaling leadership challenges:

#### Find your “area of genius” **🌟**

To stay engaged in daily work and not fall into detached manager mode, Camille advises founders and leaders to identify their “area of genius”:

> *I think as a founder, you know, you've got to figure out what your thing is. Like, what is the genius that you have brought to this business where you just have that clarity of view?*

She points out this could be in product development, marketing, or shaping company culture. Leaders should focus on these areas while delegating others.

#### Delegate wisely + stay accountable ⚖️

Camille also stresses the need for balance in delegation:

> *You have to pay some attention unless it's totally unimportant. And then you may ask yourself, […] why are we doing it at all?*

She stresses that even for delegated areas, leaders need to understand how to manage them effectively from an accountability perspective.

#### Cut unnecessary red tape **🚫**

Camille also cautions against excessive bureaucracy, especially during growth:

> *Please pay attention to your bureaucracy. That is one of the most important things you can do if you want to stay in founder mode […] be a successful company is like constantly evaluate the processes and ways of working and ask yourself, you know, do we need this extra approval? Do we need this meeting?*

### 3) 📚 **Lessons learned since writing "The Manager's Path"** ([30:38](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aeYzFLzs4jo&t=1838s))

Camille reflects on writing her influential book "The Manager's Path" and the insights she's gained since:

#### The value of fresh perspective **🆕**

Camille acknowledges that she wrote the book relatively early in her management career (7 years in), which she now sees as an advantage:

> *"I think that sometimes the best time to write is when it's just really fresh and, you know, sometimes you need to marinate, right? But I do like to write things when they're really fresh because I can still feel them."*

#### Evolving views on hiring managers **🔄**

One area where Camille's views have evolved is hiring managers:

> *"The one thing I'm not as sure about is actually the stuff that I wrote about hiring interviewing managers that may I would I may rewrite. I might change. It's probably the only thing that I can think of that I'm very clear about."*

She notes that her experience since writing the book has given her more nuanced views on this topic.

She also mentions that her upcoming book on platform engineering incorporates many of the leadership lessons she's learned since writing "The Manager's Path", particularly about scaled leadership and stakeholder management.

### 4) 🚀 Career advice for engineering managers ([42:40](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aeYzFLzs4jo&t=2560s))

Camille offers advice for engineering managers looking to stay valuable and advance their careers:

#### Focus on relationships **🤝**

Camille emphasizes the importance of good relationships at all levels:

- **Reports** — people have to want to work *for* you

- **Peers** — people have to want to work *with* you (peers and other teams)

- **Superiors** — your superiors have to want you to work for them

#### Maintain technical credibility **💻 **

For technical leadership roles, Camille suggests maintaining enough technical knowledge to ask good questions and understand the work:

> *"I can ask people really good questions about what they're doing and often understand what they say to me. And so […] because I work in pretty heavily technical areas, people like that."*

#### Represent the company well **🏢**

Camille reminds managers that they are representatives of the company:

> *"You are representative of the company. You can influence the company culture. But a lot of the value and the expectations of you is like, if your boss says you have to do something or something has to happen, how good are you at driving that thing to happen?"*

#### Continuous learning **📚**

Camille encourages managers to stay curious and open to new ideas:

> *"You've got to challenge yourself to look outside of your own world, talk to managers at other places, make friends externally that are managers and hear about what they're doing and just stay curious."*

### 5) 🔧 Platform engineering and future plans ([55:12](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aeYzFLzs4jo&t=3312s))

Camille discusses her upcoming book on platform engineering and her future plans.

She defines platform engineering as the "*discipline of building and operating internal platforms that enable productivity and leverage for our business*."

#### Balancing technology and user needs **⚙️**

She emphasizes the importance of not just building cool tech, but creating solutions that people actually want and need:

> *"I'm very passionate about doing that with 1) a very high standards of operational excellence and 2) building things that people actually want versus tech that you think would be cool or fun."*

#### Scale considerations **📏**

Camille notes that platform engineering is more relevant for companies of a certain size:

> *"Platform engineering is not something that a tiny startup should be doing. […] This is not something for your 20-people startup. […]
This is for: you're getting to some amount of scale where you have shared problems that multiple engineering teams are either all solving independently or stepping on each other's toes, trying to solve, that you need to think about how you pull that out and make that more of a shared function."*

#### Future plans **🔮**

Finally, she expresses her interest in continuing to work in the platform engineering space:

> *"I am sort of thinking about what's next for me, taking a little time off to get word out about the book and do some writing. I do think that I want to continue to work in the platform engineering space, either through advising, consulting or possibly at some point, going back and running another large or medium sized team."*

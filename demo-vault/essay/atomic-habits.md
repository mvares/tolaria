This time we have read Atomic Habits by James Clear, which has been on my backlog since… forever.

Personally, I am a creature of habit: as I have [written in the past](https://refactoring.fm/p/how-to-organize-personal-work), I schedule tasks on my calendar, create recurring slots for ceremonies, and think about most of the things I do as *routines*.

So, as such, I was already a fan of James Clear, even before reading the book. I have been following him on social media for many years, and I am subscribed to his newsletter.

This is a double-edged sword of course, because, as I approached Atomic Habits, I suspected I would be already familiar with most of its ideas.

It turns out I was wrong, and I loved it. 

The book not only elaborates on James’ most popular ideas, but it presents them in a coherent package that turns them from simple *insights* to a full-fledged *framework*. So 1) I learned new things, and 2) I understood better what I thought of knowing.

Here is what we will cover today:

- 🏃‍♂️ **Why habits hatter** — it’s about identity and systems.

- 🧅 **The three layers of change **— a handy mental model to think about progress.

- 🪧 **The fours stages of habits** —  and how to manipulate them to your advantage.

- 📌 **Bottom line** — my final take on these ideas and the book itself.

### 🏃‍♂️ Why Habits — the fundamentals

We often think of habits as a means to progress — a means towards a goal.

If I had to isolate only one teaching from the whole book, is that habits *are *the progress. Habits are the *goal*.

This shift of perspective is based on two fundamental ideas:

#### 1) Habits compound 📈

When you look at big stories in tech, sports, investing, and more, it’s very rare to find *true *overnight successes. 

When you look closely, you find that success stories are usually the result of producing decent performance for an inordinate amount of time, rather than extraordinary, one-off feats.

Most successful people I know, in fact, are 1) disciplined and 2) willing to think long term. And in a world where skills only get cheaper, this mindset may be the only true edge we have left. The more modern culture pushes us towards instant gratification, the more the ability to plan and execute on a *years* scale gets scarce and valuable.

Good habits compound over time, like the famous +1% every day.

#### 2) Systems > Goals 🔄

The second idea stems pretty much from the first, and debunks the most common way people try to make progress — that is, by setting end goals.

Goals have three problems:

- ⏱️ **Goals are temporary **—** **what happens when the goal is achieved? Goals are about *winning*, but once you win there may be nothing left to do. 

- ⛰️ **Goals are aspirational **— they do not tell you how to get there: that’s what you need systems for.

- ☀️ **Goals delay happiness** — sacrificing something today to get something tomorrow is often a smart play, but there is a catch. It is risky to do something you don’t like for a long time just because of some ideal reward at the end. The outcome you wish for might 1) never materialize, or 2) not be as good as you expected.

So wait a minute: didn’t we say it was good to plan long-term? 

Of course it is, but to get far you need to enjoy the journey, too. And when you do, you will find that you may not need a specific destination anymore.

There is a great quote in the book that says: *people don’t rise to the level of their goals: they fall to the level of their systems*. To me, this is similar to: *play games you enjoy playing, rather than only winning*, which I wrote about in a previous edition 👇

[https://refactoring.fm/p/expectations-vs-happiness](https://refactoring.fm/p/expectations-vs-happiness)

As an example, rather than thinking that you should “lose weight” and focus on a target weight, think about what it means to be a healthy, active person. What does this *version* of you look like? What do they do every day? 

Visualize this person and their life, and make it happen with habits.

That’s what James Clear wants you to do, rather than setting your *target weight*.

Still, goals *do* have their place. When you set to go in some direction, you *need* KPIs to check that you are on track. You can place *stepping stones* along the way. But know that goals only *mark *progress, they do not *make* it.

### 🧅 The Three Layers of Change

If this still feels a bit nebulous, think about change as made of three layers:

1. 🌟 **Identity** — is the person you want to become. It is the improved version of you that you look forward to, which unlocks the *why* you are doing this.

1. 🔄 **Processes **— are you systems, or habits, to bring you closer to your improved identity, and make the outcomes happen. 

1. 🏆 **Outcomes** — are specific things you want to *achieve*. It can only work in combination with the other two. 

James Clear argues that habits should be *identity-based*, rather than *outcome-based*. Outcome-based habits tend to be weaker and temporary, while identity is what you need to truly play *infinite games* and make things stick.

I have found the focus on identity and the depiction of systems vs goals to be very strong ideas. When I read through them, I was able to reflect on my *failed *habits through new lenses, and learned more about myself.

Those ideas also set the stage effectively for the second part of the book, which deconstructs how habits work and teaches how to turn this to your advantage 👇

### 🎨 The Four Stages of Habits

Habits are made of four main stages:

1. 🟣 **Cue** — the trigger that makes the brain recognize an opportunity for reward.

1. 🔴 **Craving** — the desire to achieve the reward. Occurs once the cue indicates the opportunity.

1. 🟡 **Response **— the action you take to get the reward.

1. 🟢 **Reward** — the resulting benefit gained from the response.

For a habit to stick, you need all four. You can also organize these stages in two phases: problem + solution:

- **Create the problem** → Cue + Craving 

- **Create the solution** → Response + Reward

So, the key to building good habits is to act on those stages:

1. Cue → make it **obvious**

1. Craving → make it **attractive**

1. Response → make it **easy**

1. Reward → make it **satisfying**

Likewise, if you want to break bad habits you do the reverse:

1. Cue → make it **invisible**

1. Craving → make it **unattractive**

1. Response → make it **difficult**

1. Reward → make it **unsatisfying**

But how do you do it? James unpacks these into individual laws 👇

### 1) Cue → Make it obvious 🪞

One of the reasons why you may not engage in a habit is that it is unclear when you should do so. 

The strongest cues are about **time** and **space**:

- **Time** — things you do always at the same time.

- **Space **— things you do whenever you get to some place.

To set strong time cues, schedule your habits on your calendar, and respect the schedule.

For strong *space* cues, instead, you may manipulate your environment so that you are reminded of doing something whenever you get to the designed place. E.g. you can put a book on your cushion to remind you to read before going to bed.

Another strategy for creating better cues is to ***stack *****habits** together. E.g. it would be hard to to floss *randomly* throughout the day, while its easier if you always do it right after you brush your teeth.

### 2) Craving → Make it attractive 💄

Another reason why your habits may not stick is that performing them is not *attractive* enough.

For improving on this, here are my two favorite strategies from the book:

#### **Get influenced **🧲

The author agues we are influenced by three main groups of people:

1. **The Close** — our family and friends

1. **The Many** — the majority

1. **The Powerful** — the rich, successful people we want to become

It is easier for a habit to become attractive when it is performed by people we are influenced by. While we don’t have full control on this, we can still strive to surround ourselves of people who engage in the habits we would like to adopt. 

E.g. if you want to start writing online regularly, you may join a small *mastermind* of people who are doing just the same. If you want to stop drinking, hang out with people who do not drink — and stop hanging with those who drink too much.

#### Bundle temptations 📦

Another great strategy is to bundle something unpleasant with something pleasant.

E.g. you may put your spinning bike in front of the TV so you can watch netflix while you bike. To get a step further, you may even *only* allow yourself to watch some show while you bike.

### 3) Response → Make it easy ✅

You may fall out of your habits because they are just too *hard* to perform. So here are strategies to make them easier:

#### Repetition

Part of the hardship is always psychological. For that, sticking with a habit for some time is enough to make it easier.

About this, I always think of the awesome [season finale](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=R2_Mn-qRKjA) of Bojak:* *

> *It gets easier. Every day it gets a little easier. But you got to do it every day. That's the hard part. But it does get easier.*

#### Remove friction

Any activity always includes winning some friction that is not related to the activity itself:

- To work out at the gym, you have to first change clothes and drive to the gym.

- To eat healthy, you need to spend time prepping your meals.

The more you can reduce such friction, the higher the chances that you will keep performing the habit.

In other words, for anything you want to do, you should **find the path of least resistance**.

For me, one of the tactics that work best is setting **dedicated environments** that are as turnkey as possible.

For example, I have always struggled at going to the gym, so I have set up a small one at our place. We are lucky to have a spare room we can use, so we bought some equipment and hard rubber floor. We also keep gym clothes there, so basically *everything-gym* happens in that room.

Friction is basically zero, and both my wife and myself are now working out more than we ever did.

I also find that, contrary to common sense, sometimes it helps to kickstart a habit by buying nice stuff. We didn’t need the good rubber floor to start working out, or a big mirror, but we bought them anyway. Now the room truly looks like a gym, it feels professional, which has an impact on our identities. We feel like people who train at home and are serious about it.

I used to poke fun at folks who start side projects by buying the domain name. Well, not anymore. These things matter — they can change the perception you have about that activity, and about yourself.

#### Start ridiculously small

Another great insight in the book is that there is no such a thing as starting too small. If you struggle at doing anything, make it smaller, even ridiculously so. What matters is that you commit psychologically to it.

- Turn “*I will read more*” into “*I will read one page per day*”

- Turn “*I will walk for 20 minutes*” into “*I will put on my walking shoes*” and not even leave the house!

James Clear calls this the **two-minutes rule**: commit to two minutes of work.

#### Flip it: make it hard not to do it

The dual strategy for making things easier is to make it hard not to do them.

For this, one of the things that works best for me is public commitment. When I want to do something challenging and I not sure I will follow through, I just tell everyone.

I am an amateur sim racer and two weeks ago I decided to join an official, super competitive race on an iRacing league, on Oct 27th. [This one](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=CeZQ-AAm9xU). There was a good chance I would eventually back out, because I needed a lot of prep: I had never raced neither that car or that track, it would be the longest race I ever did (1 hour), I was unsure about managing fuel, and more. Also, there was a good chance I would do everything right and still arrive last 🏁

So I told everybody about it. My wife, my parents, my friends, I gave them the link to the streaming, and I even [posted on X](https://twitter.com/lucaronin/status/1717874060900585658)! Eventually, the race went fine, and even got my [moment of glory](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=CeZQ-AAm9xU&t=4466s) on video 😄

### 4) Reward → Make it satisfying 🎁

The problem with good habits is that often times the reward is long-term, while the inconvenience is now. Conversely, the reward for bad habits is now and the damage is long-term.

So, if you can’t really turn unpleasurable things into pleasurable, you can at least treat yourself with something else whenever you do them. 

- Whenever you manage to get up early, get yourself a nice breakfast you look forward to.

- Whenever you complete all the tasks you planned for the morning, have a comfortable, longer lunch break.

- Whenever you do spinning, watch your favorite show on Netflix.

### 📌 Bottom line

Atomic habits it’s a great book. Even if you believe you already know a lot about yourself, routines, motivation, and more, you will likely find something to learn there. Or, at the very least, it will help you put the pieces of the puzzle together.

While I was reading the book, I *constantly* thought about my failed habits, and often found 1) a deeper explanation about my failures, and 2) ideas to make things better, many of which I am trying these days.

I also extracted a super quick cheatsheet, made of five questions, which I ask myself whenever I want to put a new habit in place (or I struggle with an existing one):

1. **Who do I want to become? **How does this make me a better version of myself? How do I see myself in ten years by doing this?

1. Is it **obvious** when I should do it?

1. Is it **attractive** to do it?

1. Is it **easy*** *to do it?

1. Is it **satisfying** to do it?

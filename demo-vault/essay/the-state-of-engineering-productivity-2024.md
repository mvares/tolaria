# The State of Engineering Productivity 2024

As many of you already know, in the past few months I have been working on a comprehensive survey about the **State of Engineering Productivity**.

Together with my friends at Hatica, we put together our respective communities to create the very best research we were capable of about what makes engineers productive (and unproductive 👀).

![image](../assets/420b32ce1c2d4c7c.png)

We followed these steps:

1. We ran some initial **1:1 interviews** with selected members of our communities

1. We used those insights to **create a wide survey** about personal & team productivity, collaboration, dev process, and the usage of metrics.

1. We distributed the survey and got **200 thorough answers** from developers, managers, and tech leaders across the world.

1. We cleaned up the results, **gathered insights**, and wrote this commentary.

We are publishing now the first edition of this report, exclusively to Refactoring subscribers!

Here is what we will cover:

- 🙋 **Demographics** — who we reached out to: roles, countries, company size, and work setup.

- 🎽 **Team vs Personal productivity** — how engineers and managers rank themselves and their team, based on their role and work setup.

- 🚀 **Productivity offenders & boosters** — broken down by several dimensions.

- 📊 **Engineering metrics **— which data teams use, how, and how they like them

Let’s dive in!

---

### 🙋‍♂️ Demographics

We got **204 answers **to the survey. We intentionally went for *quality* over *quantity*, which means the survey was substantial, with plenty of free-form questions that took a while to answer.

We took this route because productivity is a nuanced topic, and we didn’t want to *pidgeon-hole *answers by making people choose from predefined lists for things like bottlenecks, processes, and stuff they would like to do better.

A lot of free-form answers also means a lot of manual work to clean up, categorize, and collect insights from such data. I reviewed every single answer, attached tags to them, and drew correlations.

### 🎽 Team vs Personal Productivity

We asked people how they would rate their personal productivity from 1 to 10, and the one of their team. For various [psychological reasons](https://tonyflorida.com/ten-point-rating-scale-without-seven/), broad questions like this always average around 7, so no big insight here — but it is interesting to break answers down by role and work setup:

#### 1) Hybrid is the most common, but also the worst

*Extreme* setups, that is, either remote-first or 100% co-located, get the highest scores both in personal and team productivity.

![image](../assets/aa29ec4a6e3d4584.png)

This confirms what we have frequently heard: hybrid setups are the trickiest — even though they are the most common.

It is tough to create an environment where remote folks are not at a disadvantage with respect to those who meet IRL more frequently. From handling impromptu meetings, to getting face-time with managers, keeping the playing field level is extremely hard.

It’s probably not a coincidence that the lowest score in personally productivity is given by folks who work fully remote in hybrid teams.

#### 2) Your manager feels more productive than you

Another interesting result, which probably won’t help with managers’ reputation among engineers, is that the higher people raise through the ranks, the more productive they feel — to the detriment of the rest of their team.

![image](../assets/7a7fa2b1783c4acf.png)

While software engineers feel on average *less* productive than their team at large — making the case for widespread impostor syndrome — the same isn’t true for managers and Staff+ ICs, which generally feel significantly better about themselves than how they feel about their teams.

As we will see, factors reported as impacting personal productivity (negatively or positively) significantly differ based on people’s roles, which, combined with the above, points at a general *disconnect* between the needs of regular engineers and that of their higher-ups.

### 🚀 Productivity offenders and boosters

We gave respondents two free-form questions about productivity:

- ⬇️ **What is the most common *****offender *****to your productivity**

- ⬆️ **What is something that makes you *****more productive***

Here are the results, based on *tags* we put on individual answers:

![image](../assets/2427673331bd407e.png)

So, when it comes to what’s the most *annoying* during work days, meetings and context switch win hands down, with lack of clarity being a distant third. 

It is worth noting, however, that these items are not *orthogonal*. Too many meetings *cause* more context switch, just like too much context switch generally points to a lack of clarity.

In fact, this gets much clearer when you look at answers about productivity *boosters.*

What people truly need is simple — it’s two things:

1. **Clarity on what to do**

1. **Time and space to do it**

While we found this to be universally true across the survey, we could isolate different *flavours *based on roles and work setups.  

#### 1) Productivity for Software Engineers

Engineers suffer less from meetings than the other cohorts (managers, tech leads, directors, …) likely because they have to do less of them. Conversely, they are more impacted by bad dev process and cumbersome releases.

![image](../assets/b82e57410c9c43e6.png)

Being blocked by others also ranks disproportionately higher than for others (where it tops at 2%), which is also a bad process smell.

So, the recipe for happy and productive engineers looks simply to give them clear goals, and equip them with good DX and a lean dev process. No surprise here maybe, but it’s comforting to see it backed by numbers.

#### 2) Productivity for Tech Leads

Tech leads are a tricky role that is sometimes *trapped* between coding and management duties.

So it’s not surprising that the biggest threat, for TLs, looks like being unsure about what to focus on — it is the category that suffers the most from context switch and lack of clarity.

Coherently with the context switch issue, they are also the category that prizes working from home the most (35% vs 15% on average), as beneficial for their productivity.

So, looking at individual answers and crunching the numbers, the recipe for effective tech leads look like:

1. Setting clear boundaries between their duties to avoid confusion about priorities.

1. Enabling appropriate focus time and limit meetings and interruptions.

#### 3) Productivity for Staff+ Engineers

Staff+ Engineers report similar issues than Tech Leads, but with an even stronger focus on clarity, and a strong desire for autonomy.

![image](../assets/011bff145ef64075.png)

Staff+ roles are hard to generalize, but they are usually about large scale, cross-team initiatives with strong impact.

As such, their work is subject to several dependencies from other teams, which shows in the highest need for autonomy across all cohorts (13% vs 4% on average).

> *Autonomy, trust, clarity on what to do and focus time
****— Staff engineer in a full remote, 25-100 engineers team**** *

#### 4) Productivity for Engineering Managers

Managers are the largest cohort across all respondents. While this leads to less deviance from the average values, some differences are substantial nonetheless.

ICs may be overwhelmed by extraneous stuff, but when let they are free to work, they can recognize what’s good — managers need more help on the latter.

#### 5) Productivity by work setup

Answers were also different based on the various work setups. It is especially staggering to compare the two categories ranked respectively best and worst for productivity, which are full remote (with no office) and hybrid.

![image](../assets/484bbde19d864121.png)

The first thing to notice is that percentages are just lower *overall* for full remote setups. This is possible because people could answer entering multiple things (which is also why if you sum percentages it gives more than 100%). 

This means that full remote folks just feel more productive*. *This was already in full display in productivity ratings from before:

![image](../assets/bee9267c7a764bdf.png)

People from these setups also complain about different things: full remote teams have better dev & release processes, spend less time on maintenance, get less distracted and less blocked by others, while also doing less meetings. Conversely, they worry about engagement and doing meaningful work. However, these concerns 1) feel healthier — like a higher layer on the Maslow’s pyramid, and 2) don’t show up at an alarming level anyway.

### 📈 Engineering metrics

We wrapped up the survey with a series of questions about the usage of engineering metrics for productivity. This has been a hot topic lately, which we covered both [in the newsletter](https://refactoring.fm/p/how-to-get-started-with-engineering) and [in the podcast](https://refactoring.fm/p/how-to-improve-developer-productivity) multiple times.

Here are some of the strongest insights:

#### 1) Metrics usage is early

Only 35% of teams track and use engineering metrics. This also varies a lot based on work setup: the majority of full remote teams uses metrics, while only 17% of office based ones do.

![image](../assets/ba6fa58a903d48ea.png)

#### 2) Velocity & Cycle Time are #1

![image](../assets/fa71d4a61d05484b.png)

I don’t want to *hate *on hybrid teams, as I know many excellent ones, but I believe this gap is a reflection of different eras, or generations, of dev practices.

Cycle Time and DORA are backed by recent research and it is not surprising that they correlate well with full remote teams, who are just more likely to be young and forward thinking than hybrids, which in turn are more likely to operate on a Jira+Scrum+Velocity model.

Again, nothing wrong with the latter, but this gap in opinions and practices *does *exist for the better or worse, and is reflected in the survey numbers.

#### 3) In-house wins over dedicated tools

#### 4) Metrics’ bad reputation is overrated

Finally, we find that metrics’ supposed bad reputation across engineers and managers doesn’t match reality.

![image](../assets/dd61c1b6c2e04fc0.png)

Most respondents (60%+) are positive about their impact, and numbers don’t vary substantially based on roles (e.g. engineers vs managers) or current usage (those who already use them vs those who don’t and would like to try).

### 📌 Bottom Line

And that’s it! Here are my top 3 takeaways from the survey:

#### 1) Hybrid is hard

Teams who work in hybrid mode have the hardest time.

It is tough to create an environment where the playing field is level for everybody: there is less incentive to build truly a remote-first environment than in full-remote teams, while at the same time you don’t get the full benefits of co-location.

Committing decisively to one of the two extremes might bring better results in terms of productivity — but of course it can be more challenging in other departments.

#### 2) Productivity is mostly enabled by clarity and focus time

The recipe for good productivity looks suprisingly simple, and it stays the same whatever your role and works setup:

- **Good clarity** — I know what is the most important thing I should do.

- **Plenty of focus time** — I have the space and time to go after it.

These look like simple items, but you only achieve them when you have taken care of everything else: context switch, meetings, collaboration, etc.

The degree to which engineers and managers suffer from this is also different.

1. **Engineers suffer more from lack of focus time** → they largely know what they should do, but would like less interruptions ad more room to do it. 

1. **Managers suffer more from lack of clarity** → they feel more *productive* in the sense that they do many things, but they are unsure if these are the right ones. They pedal fast, but in an unclear direction.

So, while for most engineers direction is clear, but they would like to pedal faster, managers feel like they pedal fast, but in a questionable direction.

#### 3) Engineering metrics are early and feel like an opportunity.

Only 35% of the respondents uses engineering metrics on their teams, but a strong majority (60%) looks at them as an opportunity. This sentiment is shared equally by all the surveyed roles.

Also, more than 50% of teams who use them do so via custom tracking or by using the tools they already use (Jira, Github). Both setups are limited with respect to what you can achieve with dedicated tools, so there are likely strong margins for improvement.

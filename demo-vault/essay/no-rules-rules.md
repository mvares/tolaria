# No Rules Rules

### 📑 Materials

---

[Untitled](https://www.notion.so/3a6f00206c9a47c2aa6ada30e2fe0a0d)  

### ✍️ Post

---

Hey there! This is a **Book Edition** 📗 — where I publish our review + summary of a famous non-fiction book in our space.

It is made possible by the [book club](https://refactoring.fm/book-club) in the community, where every two months we decide on a non-fiction book to read, and we review it together in a live event at the end of the period.

So, over the last two months we have read [No Rules Rules](https://www.goodreads.com/en/book/show/49099937) — by Reed Hastings, co-founder and ex-CEO of Netflix, and Erin Meyer, management professor and author.

The subtitle of the book is: *Netflix and the Culture of Reinvention*, and, in fact, this is a book about culture. Strong, opinionated company culture. Culture you may *disagree *with.

This is, in a way, refreshing.

Company culture is often the land of the fairy tales: we say nice things about our values, about our mission, and find ourselves nodding to not-so-challenging statements.

Can you really disagree with *don’t be evil? *Or [*do the right thing*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Don%27t_be_evil)*?*

Netflix culture is different. It is not about universal rules, it is about the specific formula that works well for them. As a company, you may not be able to apply all of this advice, and as an individual, you may easily say: *this is not for me*.

In this review I will cover the main ideas from the book, adding my own commentary, and references to other books which cover similar topics. Sometimes it will be to reinforce the message, other times to provide a counter-example.

So, here is the agenda:

- 🦅 **Freedom & Responsibility**

- 🏅 **Talent Density**

- 📣 **Candor**

- 🔓 **Removing Controls**

- 🌎 **Going Global**

- 📌 **Conclusion**

Let’s dive in!

---

### 🦅 Freedom & Responsibility

Reed Hastings argues that modern companies have two main options for how to structure their organizations: rules-and-process, or freedom-and-responsibility.

1. **Rules-and-process** — is how most organizations work, and was born directly out of the industrial revolution. It prizes *order* over individuals’ freedoms*, *and it’s best for situations where maintaining consistency and safety is paramount.

1. **Freedom-and-responsibility** prizes *flexibility**** ***over consistency. It allows people to move and adapt quickly, which is best for businesses that rely on innovation.

However, while both have their merits, for Hastings the success of most modern companies depends on being relevant and innovative, rather than safe and consistent, so they should pick F&R.

F&R, though, is a destination. To get there, you need to take care of three pillars:

- 🏅 **Talent Density**

- 📣 **Candor**

- **🔓 Removing Controls**

In the book, these three ideas unfold throughout three main chapters, which, however, are not organized the way you think.

Instead of covering one idea per chapter, each chapter goes through *all* of them, each time in a more advanced way. They work like *beginner*, *intermediate*, and *expert *levels of F&R.

The reason for this choice, I believe, is that the book wants to be both a *manual* about good culture (according to Netflix) and a recollection of the *Netflix evolution* through various stages 👇

![image](../assets/4de013361c92469e.png)

So, the chapters read like evolutionary steps, and for good reason.

Today it is easy to take Netflix for granted as a modern tech giant, but the reality is: Netflix is older than Google. It was founded in 1997, and for more than *ten years* it only worked as a DVD rental service. 

It *reinvented* itself in 2007, as a streaming platform, and the rest is history (trivia: it has still been shipping DVDs [until last year](https://www.today.com/popculture/news/netflix-stop-shipping-dvds-rcna80319)).

So, the culture changed and grew together with its host, and the book structure (and all the stories and examples) wants to reflect that.

For this review, though, we are going to *transpose* this and review each pillar individually 👇

---

### 🏅 Talent Density

The first pillar of Netflix culture is **talent density**, which means: *only hire and retain the best*.

> *A great workplace is stunning colleagues.
*— Reed Hastings

This rule is backed by a three-part belief:

1. An agile company needs innovators

1. Innovators are high performers

1. High performers thrive among other high performers

The last part is crucial. An A+ team makes everybody perform at their best, and helps with retention too. But it is also incredibly hard to achieve. Netflix developed very practical recipes about it, especially when it comes to **retention** and **firing**.

#### 🔒 Retention — Pay top dollar

Hiring great talent is hard, so once you bring it on board you need to do everything in your powers to retain it.

To do so, Netflix pays top-of-market salaries, and even encourages employees to regularly *window-shop* to figure out their true market value. If they receive a superior offer, Netflix is happy to match it.

Netflix *pegs* salaries to market value, to the point where it ditches traditional performance bonuses and raise schemes. If your market value increases by 20% in one year, you get a 20% raise. If you are doing an excellent job but your value stays put, you receive no raise at all.

In the book it is unclear how managers figure out *top market value* for their employees, outside of employees telling them after shopping around, but Netflix has indeed a reputation for paying top dollar, so I am not going to nit-pick.

#### 🚪 Firing — The Keeper Test

Netflix notoriously fires fast, and gave its approach a name: **The Keeper Test**.

Each manager is supposed to periodically run their directs through the keeper test. For each of them, the manager needs to ask herself whether, faced with the possibility of losing them, she would fight to retain them. If the answer is no, the person needs to be let go.

Hastings admits this approach is ruthless and has the potential to create fear and anxiety. People care about job security, after all.

Netflix counters this in two ways:

- **Sticking to A+ talent **— top performers are less concerned with job security because they know they can always find a new job in little time.

- **Removing the shame of being let go** — Netflix is upfront about creating very *transactional* relationships with employees. Hastings is fond of saying the company is like a professional sports team, rather than a family. In a family everyone backs one another no matters what, while in a sports team turnover is natural, and there is no shame in changing jerseys.

#### 📌 Luca’s take

I have found the **talent density** ideas to be the most controversial in the book. Controversial is probably not the right word here — it is just advice that not all companies can apply.

That’s because hiring well is, by definition, a zero-sum game. Just like *paying top dollar* is.

Netflix’s core belief here seems to be that, if anything, top talent is *still* underrated*, *especially in tech, so you should kind of arbitrage that. In a nutshell: top engineers, paid top of the market, are still a bargain. 

You may or may not agree with it, but still, this is the cornerstone of Netflix culture, and *a lot* of the advice that comes next, in terms of organizational principles, have evolved having this in mind.

---

### 📣 Candor

The second Netflix pillar is about giving **great feedback** all the time. It is actually called *candor*, but I argue that such candor has a goal, and the goal is to create an amazing feedback engine.

In fact, Netflix has three memorable pieces of advice about it:

- 4️⃣ **The 4A Framework**

- 👑 **Giving the boss feedback**

- ⏳ **Giving feedback anytime**

- 📋 **Formalizing feedback**

Let’s go through them 👇

#### 4️⃣ The 4A Framework

Netflix developed a simple framework top help people give and receive feedback, it is based on four items:

**When giving feedback:**

1. **Aim to assist **— feedback must be given with positive intent, and be framed on how it can *help* the individual or the company. You should never give feedback to intentionally hurt a person.

1. **Actionable **— corrective feedback must focus on what the recipient can do differently, in a way that they can act upon it.

**When receiving feedback**:

1. **Appreciate **— the receiver needs to listen carefully and be open minded about feedback, without being defensive or angry. Consider feedback a gift. 

1. **Accept** **(or discard) **— the recipient needs to listen and consider all the feedback, but they are not required to follow it. This decision is entirely up to the recipient.

#### 👑 Giving the boss feedback

People typically refrain from giving corrective feedback because they want to avoid conflict. 

To avoid this and develop a culture of candor, managers need to lead by example, encouraging frequent feedback from their reports. As a manager. you need to work on this intentionally, otherwise it will likely not happen. You can ask for feedback directly, during meetings, and put *feedback* as an agenda item to encourage people to prepared items in advance.

The more your reports feel *safe* to give you feedback, the safer they will feel to receive it from you.

#### ⏳ Giving feedback anytime

The best time to give feedback is immediately, as soon as the matter happens. 

The sooner you give feedback the more context both you and the receiver retain, which makes it clearer and less prone to misunderstanding.

Hastings also stresses the importance of reinforcing feedback — a.k.a. positive feedback. Reinforcing good behavior is just as important as correcting bad one, which echoes our very own piece about feedback and the praise-to-criticism ratio 👇

[https://refactoring.fm/p/how-to-give-feedback-](https://refactoring.fm/p/how-to-give-feedback-)

#### 📋 Formalizing feedback

The final piece of advice is about making feedback a formal process in your organization. 

Netflix replaced regular performance reviews with 360° reviews, where each person receives feedback from all directions: their manager, their peers, their reports (hence the 360°).

These reviews are held annually, in written form, and are not directly tied to raises, promotions, or firings (but managers arguably get precious information from them).

Since 2010 they went even further and started having many of these 360s *live*, on several-hour sessions where all team members participate, e.g. over dinner.

Hastings understands that giving (and receiving) live feedback in a group session is tricky, and includes plenty of advice to get it right. However, he also stresses that there is no way to avoid discomfort. In a way, it’s like going to the dentist: you are not going to like the experience, but you will be grateful after it.

#### 📌 Luca’s take

As opposed to the talent density bits, most of the candor section feels like top advice for any team out there.

In my experience, a great culture of feedback truly separates elite teams from the rest. This shouldn’t come as a surprise by now, as it is echoed in plenty of other works, like [Radical Candor](https://www.google.com/search?q=goodreads%20radical%20candor&oq=goodreads%20radical%20candor&gs_lcrp=EgZjaHJvbWUyBggAEEUYQDIMCAEQIxgnGIAEGIoFMgYIAhAjGCcyBwgDEAAYgAQyBwgEEAAYgAQyBwgFEAAYgAQyBwgGEAAYgAQyBwgHEAAYgAQyBwgIEAAYgAQyBwgJEAAYgATSAQgyNDYwajBqMagCALACAA&sourceid=chrome&ie=UTF-8) from Kim Scott, and [Principles](https://www.goodreads.com/book/show/34536488-principles) from Ray Dailo, but it is great to get Netflix take on this, which is rich in examples and practical advice.

This is also one of the areas where the *evolutionary* style of the book truly shines. A culture of candor is a *destination* — you need a lot of work to get there. This is perfectly represented in the book, which starts small (”*say what you really think”*) and gradually amps things up till the very end (live 360s in front of all your colleagues!).

### 🔓 Removing Controls

Removing controls feels, in a way, like the true essence of the book. In fact, this chapter could be easily called **Freedom & Responsibility**, which is how Netflix describes its whole culture instead.

Also, it feels like the two other pillars act as preparatory steps for it: you can only achieve a culture of F&R when you have 1) top talent on board, and 2) a solid feedback loop.

As with the other sections, *removing controls* starts pretty tactical and gradually goes deeper 👇

#### Remove policies

In the early part of the book Hastings recalls how they ended up removing Travel, Expense, and Vacation policies.

He includes a lot of nuance and sensible advice, acknowledging well-known risks (e.g. no vacation policies, when left unsupervised, tend to make people take *less* vacation) and detailing how the process worked for Netflix.

The goal, however, is simple: **reduce complexity** and trust people judgement. Policies are the simplest way to start doing it.

#### Share everything

The next step of reducing rules and complexity is about data. 

Netflix shares pretty much everything with their employees: goals, financials, salaries, business performance — everything. It does so despite being a public company, which poses very practical risks, but Hastings believes the upsides easily trump the downsides.

#### Lead with context

The final and most impactful way of removing controls is on everyday decisions. Netflix strives to make people make their own calls, without being told what to do, nor asking for approval from their bosses.

To achieve this and make it effective, managers need to **lead with context**: they need to share *everything* they know about the goals to be achieved and what good looks like, so that people and teams can design their own initiatives and go for them autonomously.

This is, again, backed by 1) having trust in your teammates’ talent (talent density), and 2) being able to give good feedback and correct course when needed (candor).

---

### 🌎 Going Global

There is also a fourth, final chapter, called *Going Global*, which focuses on the issues of *scaling* these themes globally, across countries where people have diverse sensibilities.

This makes sense, as many themes of Netflix culture may look different in different places. What does *candid feedback *look like in Japan vs the US? What about Germany?

However, while interesting at times, it also marks a departure from the general structure of the book, and it doesn’t always feel cohesive with the rest. It also kind of feels like a plug to Erin Meyer’s other book, The Culture Map, on which most of the analysis is based.

So, being those issues largely* *related to the *candor* theme, I would have expected them to fit some of the candor sections, and probably occupy a little less room 😇

### 📌 Conclusion

I thought a lot about what to write in this conclusion, and I keep coming back to Hastings’ idea of the two options to structure an organization: **Rules & Process** vs **Freedom & Responsibility**.

Hastings makes the case for innovative companies to structure themselves around **F&R**, and makes a (now) famous analogy: the CEO is like the leader of a jazz band—the musicians (employees) need freedom and flexibility, but without the right conditions, the beautiful music turns into a loud mess.

I love this take. I believe it is *crucial* to have this conversation and promote F&R because, undoubtedly, we have been skewing towards R&P for decades, while innovation and technology need something else.  

#### Freedom vs rules is a fractal

However, I also believe that F&R vs R&P is a **fractal argument** that needs to be evaluated at many levels of granularity. It not only calls for different choices in different companies — it calls for different choices in different *areas* of the same company.

I am a musician myself, and the jazz band analogy definitely struck a chord (pun intended). In a jazz band you would leave your soloists free to create beautiful lines, but you would orchestrate drums and bass with rules & process. That’s because — to use Hastings words — drums are more about *safety and consistency*,* *while soloing is more about innovation.

Likewise, even on an elite team, you will always benefit from processes around those parts that benefit from safety & consistency. In engineering, think your release pipeline, or your QA process.

A few weeks ago I interviewed Kathryn Koehler, Director of Engineering at Netflix, and she said they do yearly and quarterly planning across the company. That’s process! Below that, teams design initiatives autonomously to achieve goals. That’s freedom!

#### Family vs sports team is a choice

The other memorable idea that, together with F&R, encapsules Netflix culture the most is the **professional sports team metaphor**, as opposed to team-as-family.

I have no objection to it — it makes sense and I am sure it works for Netflix, but it’s just not the only way.

Bill Campbell, whose [legendary coach career](https://refactoring.fm/p/trillion-dollar-coach?utm_source=publication-search) we reviewed a few months ago, went the extra mile to treat people on his team not just like family — like *close* family! He broke down the walls between the human and professional personas and cared about teammates as human beings. He argued that people who feel appreciated end up performing at their very best.

Ray Dailo, whose [Principles](https://www.goodreads.com/book/show/34536488-principles) we have mentioned before, and who shares so *many* ideas with No Rules Rules, including the *candor* and *freedom* elements, also skews towards the team-as-family approach. He believes a strong bond makes employees feel like they work towards a shared mission, which in turn is better than any transactional employment agreement.

So, as with all true cultural topics, those are choice to be made, rather than absolute best practices.

Founders shape the culture they are most comfortable with (rightfully so!) and hire the type of people who go along with it.

So, No Rules Rules is a fantastic example of a strong, opinionated culture, which should give everybody plenty to reflect, agree, and disagree on.

Isn’t that what we should look for in a book?

# Lessons learned from 25 years in tech

*Hey! This is a guest article written by my friend Umberto Nicoletti, Director of Product and Engineering at Proemion.*

*Umberto has had an incredible 25-year career in tech, and recently wrote a series of reflections about it on *[*his blog*](https://unicolet.blogspot.com/)*. I loved them and asked him to consolidate them into a long-form version for Refactoring, so here we are!*

*Find yourself a comfy place, get a hot beverage if you fancy so, sit down and I hope you enjoy the reading!*

---

When we read stories of successful people, they always seem to be going in a straight line, where each decision gets our protagonist one step closer to their goals, all part of a flawless execution which appears to be planned from the very beginning.

This story will no doubt look the same, but that’s far from being the truth.

I will omit the sleepless nights, the long working days, the spite, the anger and sometimes the regret that have peppered what might otherwise look like a fairy tale.

When we tell stories, hardship is easy and sometimes convenient to forget, but it’s as much a part of who we end up being as the successes are. So while you read this, cut yourself some slack. If you are currently struggling, or have been, do know that it’s ok — we all struggle, and no, we haven’t figured it all out either.

By the time I am writing this I have been employed professionally in ICT for ~25 years, starting on a peculiar date: April 1st 2000.

I had started using computers semi-seriously a few years earlier, in 1996. And in 1995, three things had happened around me:

1. **Microsoft launched Windows 95** — which got the whole world excited about personal computers at an unprecedented level of popularity.

1. **The Internet became a thing** — it was mentioned on TV and you might get it at home, for free, with dialup.

1. **I was about to start university** — and was looking around at what field to pursue.

For me, the decision couldn’t have been easier: it had to be Computer Science! Deep down I knew that the Internet and the World Wide Web would end up changing the world (if anything, I underestimated how much) and I wanted to be a part of that.

Also, and that helped although it was not the main reason, it appeared to be offering a promising career because demand was exponentially outgrowing supply.

Only 30 years later I would discover that this intersection of events is what the Japanese call *ikigai*. Ikigai is used to refer to a passion that gives value and joy to life, by sitting at the intersection of four pillars:

1. **What the world needs**

1. **What I love doing**

1. **What I am good at**

1. **What I can get paid for**

My sustained enjoyment of this work, 30 years on, is a direct result of the purpose inherent in the convergence of these four pillars.

### 🎓 University & Open Source

Pursuing a passion or doing a job you love is great, but there’s a better option: do a job that you love and that you can also make a good living out of. When we’re aligned with the universe, everything is easier, but then again the true innovators are those that are willing to go against the universe.

While at University I did something else that, when I look back at it, I would say was pretty reckless (at least for the time, or my family): I joined the Erasmus program as an exchange student. That gave me a chance to refine my English to a level that would have been otherwise unattainable. This was the end of the ‘90s when very few people spoke English and translators were still a booming business.

This hardly gained English fluency would prove supremely useful in the following years.

My first job out of University was glamorous (for the time) and came with a lot of wiggle room: I worked in a small tech company that dealt with large customers (mostly healthcare) and, when everybody was still writing desktop applications, was betting big on web ones. The job required sacrifices because it had a long commute, but I accepted because I intuitively grasped that it would allow me to gain experience not possible otherwise.

Having the company started out as an internet POP (point of presence), they also offered email and web presence services, which allowed me to tinker with mail servers, systems and network administration, web applications, databases, pre-sales, and even IT helpdesk. 

This varied experience shaped me into an extremely versatile resource, and I still think is my greatest asset today.

They were also deeply committed to Open Source, which meant that I would get to play (and sometimes contribute back) to some projects we used. Spoiler: it was through one of these projects that I eventually got to know the person that would hire me at my current job.

I stayed at this job for a (relatively) long time because I liked it and it was my *ikigai*. But I was growing anxious to prove my worth outside a circle that I started finding boring.

As I said, I’ve been an avid Open Source user who contributed back as often as I could. Last time I checked—a decade ago—I had a level 2 fosser and Kudos rank of 8/10 on [Blackduck](https://openhub.net/) (formerly Ohloh) which, quoted from their own page of the time was “*relatively uncommon, with about only 6% of the population receiving this rank*”.

This reinforced my desire to look for a new challenge.

> 💭 **A take away and a provoking thought**

### 🔌 Power Adapters & DevOps

It was at an Open Source conference in Germany that a fortuitous and definitely funny situation set in motion a series of events that eventually led to my new career.

As you might know power plugs are not the same everywhere, and I conveniently forgot this fact when planning that trip to Germany.

By the time I arrived at the conference location my laptop battery was empty. After registering, I went inside and, in a panic, asked the first person I saw if they could help me find an adapter. They ended up doing much more, and drove me to a place where I was able to buy a power cord that fit my power supply. Since the guy was part of the inner circle of this Open Source project, we stayed in touch and saw each other again at other conferences over the following years.

After years, in 2016 he switched jobs and I sent him a congratulatory email. He replied by offering me a part-time software developer position at his same company. By sheer coincidence I had just started a fully remote, part-time freelancing gig, and this company, called [Proemion](https://www.proemion.com/), became my first and only customer.

A few months later, Proemion hired me full time.

Proemion has been nothing short of fantastic for me: I went from part-time to full time, then started applying DevOps principles, which lead to me being assigned a DevOps team (yeah I know it is an anti-pattern), which grew to incorporate the Datacenter team shortly after.

In this first phase—about 3 years—the broad experience I had acquired at my previous job proved invaluable as I could effortlessly move through systems administration, monitoring, scaling, database, reverse proxies, and development. With my team, we built the foundation of the no-nonsense internal platform we still use today, even though it’s changed and evolved a lot since.

My next step, however, would require a completely different set of skills.

> 💭 **A take away and a provoking thought**

### 👑 Head of R&D

As Head of R&D, my job was to ensure smooth and reliable execution across different teams/domains, one of which—Embedded Systems—was also completely new to me from a technology point of view. What I knew about servers, virtualization, networks, software, etc, was not directly transferrable.

Because of this *gap*, I started looking into Leadership topics more—Leadership as opposed to Management—and I started realizing that if I wanted to be successful in this new unfamiliar domain I would have to learn to reach through to people, motivate them, and provide context for their daily decision making.

As Marshall Goldsmith describes it:

> *Leadership is working with and through others to achieve objectives.*

I believe these two aspects are key to my success as a leader:

1. **Being reliable, as in “faithfully doing what you have engaged to do”** (Charlie Munger)

1. **Listening to understand: remember the acronym WAIT** (Why Am I Talking)

The good news about Leadership is that there is an amazing amount of resources freely available out there. The bad news is that there is an amazing amount of resources out here, and that might be overwhelming.

Speaking for myself, I am an avid consumer of books, podcasts, and RSS feeds which, I am certain, play a key role in providing me with the insights necessary to tackle whatever challenge might be coming my way.

In case you are curious, I maintain a list of my favorite podcast episodes as a [Google spreadsheet](https://docs.google.com/spreadsheets/d/1fahTLmbSRO2YCCyMspEV5Y7Fi-EE3_6zOsKyvg7FRPI/edit?usp=sharing). To refresh the most salient passages, I rely on Readwise to remind me of them on a daily basis. I have lost count of the times that Readwise provided me with the insight I needed that day! It is truly mind-boggling and feels like magic.

However, as I hinted at the beginning, the path has not been without challenges: I met major setbacks, which at times made me question whether I belonged there. In the hope this can be useful to you, dear reader, there are three things that helped mitigate my imposter syndrome:

1. **🔭 Playing the long game**

1. 🪴 **Coaching**

1. 💬 **Communities**

Let’s explore all of them:

#### 1) 🔭 Playing the long game

Playing the long game is inspired by the book [Finite and Infinite Games](https://www.goodreads.com/book/show/189989.Finite_and_Infinite_Games), by James P. Carse. I would coarsely sum it up as about trying to cultivate every interaction with the purpose of being able to enjoy and grow it for as long as possible.

[https://www.natsnotes.com/p/finite-infinite-games](https://www.natsnotes.com/p/finite-infinite-games)

The opposite of that is the **finite game**, in which all participants aim at *winning*, like in a sports game. Someone playing the long game—we’re finite beings, and so I’ll treat long as a synonym for infinite in this professional context—is much less affected by occasional downturns, as that is merely a wrinkle in a much larger game. I have gotten better at mastering my emotions thanks to long term thinking, but it is still not always easy.

As with all things, I’ll admit it is far easier to *describe* playing the long game than to *practice* it, and that’s where coaching helped me rediscover such potent concepts when I needed them the most 👇

#### 2) 🪴 Coaching

They say it’s lonely at the top, but the truth is: it doesn’t have to be. When it is, it is most often **a choice** we make, by deciding not to get help.

Besides my peers and some exceptional reports, coaching has been my go-to strategy for getting help. I have worked with two coaches, both extraordinary and the last one is a name that is probably familiar to the Luca’s readers: [Joel Chippindale](https://monkeysthumb.co.uk/).

Joel didn’t just give me answers, he helped me find them within myself. His coaching approach unlocked the knowledge and skills I already possessed, making solutions feel authentic and personally meaningful. I felt the insights were truly mine and that led to better retention and real-world application.

#### 3) 💬 Communities

Being active in communities of interest greatly helped me fight back imposter syndrome. It made me notice how my contributions were valued by others, which reinforced the belief that, after all, I *did* belong.

I will add that a smaller community, or one closer to your current level, will likely make it more comfortable to join the conversation. The tool chosen by the community also influences the exchange, and I personally enjoy a *forum* like the Refactoring one more than communities just relying on Slack.

In one extraordinarily large online community where most of the active folks have written or are mentioned in books, it took me a lot more time before I switched from *lurker* to *contributor*. However, I will add that I found these super-famous folks to be incredibly approachable and open. To some extent, I regret not having joined the conversation sooner.

I will close with one last thing that for me was important for my career progression: **having a clear long term plan** (and regularly discussing with my supervisor). I could not imagine the last two roles happening without it. I’ll comment it with a thought from Dr. Lisa Su, CEO of AMD, which also nicely reminds me of how it all happened because of a power chord:

> “*Careers are very much by chance, but you always want to have a clear roadmap of what you wanna do*”

If you’ve read so far, thanks! And if I’ve helped one person with this, then it’s been worth it.

> 💭 **A take away and a provoking thought
***Never stop learning, you will never know when you will need it. And with AI we have literally no excuse to dip our toes in any field that might pique our interest or curiosity.
There is a difference between knowledge and experience, remember it.*

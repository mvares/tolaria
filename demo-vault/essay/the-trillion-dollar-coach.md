Over the last two months we have read [Trillion Dollar Coach](https://www.goodreads.com/en/book/show/42118073):** **The Leadership Playbook of Silicon Valley's Bill Campbell.

The book was written a couple of years after Bill’s death, in 2017, by Eric Schmidt, Jonathan Rosenberg, and Alan Eagle — three Google executives who worked with him for many years.

![image](../assets/ad9a6dd624d649fe.jpeg)

The first thing I want you to notice is in the title. It says: “*Silicon Valley’s Bill Campbell”*, rather than just “*Bill Campbell*”*. *When you think about it, it’s odd — we don’t usually attach people to places / environments this way.

I don’t know the authors’ intent, of course, but I suspect it’s about *pride.* Bill Campbell’s story has shaped modern Silicon Valley like few others, and now it just belongs to everyone, like the Golden Gate or the Sequoia National Park.

In fact, it is one of those stories you will find hard to believe — on so many levels. It is the story of a football player turned football coach, turned marketer at ~40yo, then turned executive coach to the likes of Steve Jobs, Larry Page, Jeff Bezos, and Sheryl Sandberg.

The story could easily fit a traditional, Walter-Isaacson-style biography, but these authors are not novelists: they are executives, so they created a *handbook *instead. A handbook* *of Bill’s leadership style and teachings, for leaders and companies to learn from.

We will review this thoroughly, so here is the agenda:

- 🛣 **Bill’s Life **— highlights from Bill’s incredible life.

- 🪴 **Team First **— smarts & hearts, the King Arthur’s round table, and other memorable takes.

- 🤝 **Building Trust **— how you *earn* your people’s respect, rather than demanding it.

- ❤️ **Power of Love **— breaking down the walls between the human and professional personal

- 📌 **Bottom Line **— my final thoughts on the whole book.

### 🛣 Bill’s life

Bill studied economics at Columbia, and played football there as a linebacker on defense and a guard on offense. In his senior year, Columbia won the Ivy League with Bill as team captain.

Later he got his master’s in education, and went on to pursue a career as a football coach. He spent ten years at Boston College, and then five years at Columbia again.

The Columbia years were tough — Cambpell would later blame his coaching difficulties on the fact that he was overly sensitive to his players' feelings — and at the age of 39, he decided to move on.

He got a job at an ad agency first, then joined Apple, and eventually rose the ranks to become VP of Sales and Marketing. There, in 1984, he created the famous Super Bowl ad inspired by George Orwell’s 1984 — one most famous ads of all times.

In 1994 he became CEO of Intuit, and in 2000 he joined the VC firm Kleiner Perkins, where he started working as a mentor and coach for the startups the firm funded.

He was 60 years old.

From 2000 until his death, in 2015, he simultaneously coached dozens of executives: Eric Schimdt, Larry Page (and the whole Google executive team), Steve Jobs, Jeff Bezos, Sheryl Sandberg, Dick Costolo, Marissa Mayer, and more.

Bill’s story single-handedly counters two of the biggest problems we have with tech culture today:

1. 👶 **Ageism** — that you can only achieve great things while young. A book was written only about what Bill did after 60, and other could be written about the stuff he did before.

1. 🔍 **Specialization** — that career is a ladder on which you can only move up or down. Bill had 3-4 different careers with, seemingly, little overlap.

How could he pull this off? Let’s explore Bill’s mentality, coaching and leadership style, through the lens of three words: *team*, *trust*, and *love*.

![image](../assets/880bf4f8bfb64bd3.png)

### 🎽 Team First

The first notable element from all the anecdotes and pieces of advice that Bill has given throughout his career, is that he approaches problems with a team-first attitude.

We hear that all the time, but what does this mean in practice? Here are the greatest hits of Bill’s advice on this:

#### 1) **Smarts & Hearts **⚖️

Don’t hire people who spell team with an “I”. 

The principle is that people skills matter as much as technical skills. When hiring, Bill looked for smart people who worked hard, had high integrity, and who were good at empathy and communication.

He wasn’t overly concerned with experience or tech chops — he hired people for potential.

#### 2) Work the team, not the problem 🔀

On the football field, if you have the wrong player in the wrong position, you lose.

So, for each problem you should get the right people (or the right team) tackle it, based on their skills. When you are confident with it, you can let go the nitty-gritty details and let people do their thing.

#### 3) Encourage diversity 🧑‍🎤

Just like you can’t have a team full of quarterbacks, Bill believed a diverse team is achieve greater results.

Diversity applies to everything: background, walks of life, sexes, ethnicities, and more. Diverse teams deflect the risk of groupthink. To create truly world-class solutions, you can’t have an engineer-only team, or a man-only team, or an under-30 only team, and so on.

#### 4) King Arthur’s round table ⚔️

Also known as, don't make decisions by consensus.

When facing a decision, all perspectives should be heard and considered. The team should have an open debate about which idea is best, and possibly converge autonomously.

If the team can't make up their mind, then the manager has to step up, make the decision, and end the debate — because no decision is often worse than a bad decision.

Bill called this the “**King Arthur Round Table**” decision-making model. Most of the time, the knights can figure it out. When they can't, the king has to make a ruling.

#### 5) Control the geniuses 🧠

Bill was aware that innovation requires out-of-the-box thinking and that creative people are important — but he was also aware that high-performing innovators are often terrible team players.

*Aberrant geniuses*, as he called them, need to be tolerated as long as their actions don’t cross ethical lines and their value outweighs their cost to the team. Managers should give *geniuses* autonomy to do what they do — but make sure they uphold a team-first attitude.

#### 6) Relentless encouragement 👏

Whenever the team is struggling, as a leader, you should recommit to the cause. Bill believed in practicing *relentless optimism*, even in the face of a losing record, and in being an enthusiastic cheerleader for the team.

In fact, he believed great leaders give continuous encouragement and push for bolder actions. Much of what he did during coaching sessions was to convince people they could do much more than they thought they could.

### 🤝 Building Trust

The second pillar of Bill’s attitude is *trust*. He believed you don’t *demand* respect from your employees — you *earn* their respect.

In fact, people actually *like* being managed if they respect their manager.

A manager's role is to cultivate an environment that encourages employee growth and development. This can be achieved by providing essential training and information, as well as respecting and trusting employees to perform their tasks effectively. In doing so, a manager establishes credibility.

And there is no alternative to this path. In fact, the more talented an employee is, the less likely they will simply follow orders from a manager — unless that manager has established their credibility.

> *Install a leader who is, at the same time, a caring coach, and a strong operations manager
— *Bill Campbell

So, here is how you build trust by your reports:

#### 1) Psychological safety 🤗

Psychological safety means ensuring that everyone can be fully themselves and take risks without fear of reprisal. ([View Highlight](https://www.shortform.com/app/highlights/ed27821c-d4c3-484e-94df-a7a0fef521d9)).

Bill insisted that high-performing teams are not made of people with similar personalities who never disagree about anything: they are made of people who feel psychologically safe.

So, *task conflict* should never degenerate into *relationship conflict:*

- Task conflict is healthy —  it leads to discovering the best way to get things done.

- Relationship conflict is unhealthy — it leads to low morale and poor-decision making.

#### 2) World-class listener 👂

As a manager, you should listen to people with your *full and undivided attention*.

Bill had a knack for listening, which included asking many questions. It is a take on the classic **Socratic method**: when you tune in to what someone is sharing and ask thoughtful, curious questions that get to the heart of the matter, it helps people feel comfortable to open up more. Plus, they feel highly valued.

#### 3) Tough love 🤎

For both strong relationships and strong performance, honesty and authenticity are key. This means, sometimes, giving tough feedback.

Bill’s advice is very similar to Kim Scott’s *tough love* concept, explored in her book [Radical Candor](https://www.goodreads.com/book/show/29939161-radical-candor). We also talked about it in our edition about how to give feedback 👇

[https://refactoring.fm/p/how-to-give-feedback-](https://refactoring.fm/p/how-to-give-feedback-)

An important observation by Bill is that, for negative feedback, prompt timing is critical. Tough criticism should always be delivered immediately — don't hold off tough criticism for a future performance review in a month.

#### 4) Don't tell people what to do 📣

Most of the times, when faced with a problem, Bill would simply listen and ask probing questions, then let people come to their own conclusions. Instead of saying, “*Do it this way instead of that way*,” he would say “*How could you do it differently?*”

It might feel like a small change, but people feel more engaged when managers reframe the way they communicate to sound less authoritative.

He also believed in the **power of stories**. 

Instead of doling out his version of the best decision, he would often tell anecdotes about events that had happened in his own career that might help guide people to making the best decision on their own.

### ❤️ The Power of Love

Finally, Bill believed in breaking down the walls between the *human persona* and the *professional persona*. 

He encouraged executives to talk to their employees about their personal lives, learn about their families, and care about them as people, not just employees. Instead of saying, “How are the kids?” Bill would say, “How was Hannah's last soccer game?”

Here is how he put this into practice:

#### 1) Doing favors for others 💁

Bill was generous with his time, money, and connections. Whenever he could pull a few strings, he would, without asking for anything in return.

Most of us think of favors differently depending on the recipient — but Bill would say, just do it. Especially in cases where such favors are asymmetrical: they may require only a few minutes on our side, but mean an awful lot to the recipient.

When you play the *long game* of good relationships, doing many favors is like sowing the ground: eventually, you will reap the rewards.

#### 2) Practicing people skills 🍻

Caring for colleagues takes practice. You may be a natural at this, or you may be more of an introvert who is not exactly enthusiast at the idea of many personal chats.

In any case, you can practice this, like a muscle. Start with small exchanges, like making eye contact when you walk in the office, or asking a co-worker how their day is going. 

And then, like [in BoJack](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=R2_Mn-qRKjA): “*everyday it gets a little easier, but you gotta do it everyday. That’s the hard part. But it does get easier*”

#### 3) Connecting people 🔗

Bill believed in building *community*, rather than just *teams*. He believed in creating long-lasting connections between people, both inside and outside of the workplace.

The book is full of examples of this:

- He formed a Super Bowl group who would travel together to attend the Super Bowl each year

- He held frequent golf and fishing trips

- He invested in a sports bar in Palo alto for friends to hang out at.

### 📌 Bottom line

Trillion Dollar Coach is a greatest hits of what *human leadership* looks like, and how such leadership can lead to extraordinary results.

It is an supremely important lesson, especially today, in 2024.

Last week [I talked with Lena Reinhard](https://refactoring.fm/p/leading-and-human-ing-in-2024-with) on the podcast about the many challenges that the tech industry is facing today: economic downturns, geopolitics, layoffs, AI. Whenever things get tough, companies have the tendency to *tighten up*, and become a little bit less human, so we may live under the impression that we have to *choose *between more profits and taking care of people’s wellbeing.

Bill’s story is the proof that caring for people is not only the *human* thing to do, it is also right for the business. This is a refreshing take, which we badly need.

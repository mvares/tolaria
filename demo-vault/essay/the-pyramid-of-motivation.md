# The Pyramid of Motivation

### 📑 Materials

---

### Mastermind lesson learned

### ✍️ Post

---

An investor of mine once told me that most startups fail when founders stop being motivated. 

On the outside, it looks like all sorts of practical reasons: cash is over, product-market-fit can’t be found, wrong hires — but these things mostly happen as a *consequence* of founders running out of energy and not trying anymore.

These days, I still think about this often.

Engagement and motivation makes or breaks teams, which in turn makes or breaks products. That’s why, when you hear advice from seasoned CTOs or VPEs, it is often about ethereal things like momentum, bias for action, and making people look forward to their job.

All these things may look fluffy… until they don’t.

So, in this article, we will explore the intricacies of motivation in engineering teams.

We'll dive into both theory and practice, drawing from my personal experiences, insights from leaders in the community, and proven strategies from successful tech companies. 

Whether you're a seasoned CTO or a fresh team lead, the goal is to give you actionable advice to transform your team's motivation and performance.

Here is the agenda:

- 🧭 **What is motivation** — understanding the core elements that drive engineers and how they vary from person to person.

- 🌟 **Motivation goes beyond productivity** — exploring the second-order benefits of a motivated team.

- 🔺 **The Pyramid of Motivation** — my own framework and guide to creating an environment where motivation can flourish, including key strategies and common pitfalls to avoid.

- 🏢 **Examples from successful tech companies** — learning how industry leaders have tackled motivation challenges and the results they've achieved.

Let's dive in!

---

### **🧭 What is motivation**

Anyone who has worked both in motivated and unmotivated* *teams can immediately spot the difference, and will always try to recreate that *mojo* in their teams.

But what exactly constitutes motivation in the context of an engineer, or an engineering team?

Let’s unpack it by looking at two popular lenses:

#### **1) Intrinsic vs. extrinsic motivation 🎭**

Motivation can be broadly categorized into two types:

- 🧠 **Intrinsic motivation** — which comes from within. It's the personal satisfaction an engineer gets from solving a complex problem, learning a new technology, or seeing their code make a real-world impact.

- 🏆 **Extrinsic motivation** — which involves external rewards or pressures, such as money, promotions, or recognition from peers and superiors.

You almost always hear that intrinsic motivation is good and extrinsic motivation is bad, but I personally believe you should strive for a healthy combination of the two.

We are humans, not *ascetics* who only live for their internal worlds, so there is nothing wrong at looking for validation, being happy when our peers appreciate us, or wanting to earn a lot of money.

In my experience, problems rather arise when one of these angles is too dominant:

- People who are driven too much by **extrinsic factors** tend to adopt opportunistic behavior, feel more insecure, and prioritize short-term gains.

- People who mostly care about **intrinsic growth** may overly focus on their own mastery, to the detriment of the overall team and business success.

As a manager, you should help with both sides:

1. 🔍 **Understand** — learn what motivates your teammates on an intrinsic level, so you can help them with their personal goals.

1. 🚀 **Incentivize** — Create an environment where individuals have healthy extrinsic incentives to do great things, like achieving a common goal, recognition and a good career.

This was echoed by Alina Mihai, a Senior Frontend Developer, on the community 👇

> *I believe my actions are influenced by intrinsic motivation, but highly motivated teams I worked with had common objectives that everyone committed to, felt psychologically safe to make mistakes, and ask for help. Team spirit, feelings of belonging, and the trust to rely on others also motivated me a lot to do my best work.*

To figure out where you stand on this, think at your individual team members and ask yourself:

- *Do I know what motivates them? Can I answer with something specific for each of them?*

- *Are their projects and duties aligned with what motivates them? *

Not all tasks are pleasant, but you should always try to develop avenues of work (however small) that align to people’s aspirations.

#### **2) The Three Pillars 🏛️**

We can’t talk about intrinsic motivation without covering Daniel Pink's book [Drive](https://www.goodreads.com/book/show/6452796-drive), which is probably my favorite work about it.

Daniel identifies three pillars behind intrinsic motivation, which I have always found particularly relevant for engineering teams:

- 🚀 **Autonomy** — the desire to be self-directed. Engineers thrive when given broad ownership and the freedom to choose how to approach problems.

- 📚 **Mastery** — the urge to get better at stuff. The best engineers have an internal drive to create high-quality tech and continually improve and learn.

- 🎯 **Purpose** — the yearning to do what we do in service of something larger than ourselves. Engineers want to know that their code is making a difference.

Just like with extrinsic vs intrinsic motivation, in my experience the *intensity* with which individuals feel these desires varies.

A few weeks ago [I interviewed Anna Shipman](https://refactoring.fm/p/good-tech-strategy-bad-tech-strategy?utm_source=publication-search), an incredibly accomplished CTO who worked at the UK government, the Financial Times, and now Kooth, a digital health company. The choices she made throughout her career where always driven by a strong sense of purpose, and he only joined companies whose mission was important for her.

Conversely, I know people who are incredibly motivated by technical challenges. They feel the need to grow their own mastery by attacking interesting and complex problems.

Finally, you have the entrepreneurial types, who thrive when they can own something from top to bottom. They are generalists, always have side-gigs, and perform best in high agency environments. I am probably in this camp.

So, why should all of this be useful?

Because, in my experience, **figure out what motivates people is hard**. If you ask them directly,  many are not even able to answer. Even when I think at myself, it took a long time and lot of connecting-the-dots-backward to learn.

So, think about these as ingredients that you can use to create recipes for your team. There is a good baseline you can achieve that works for everybody, but you’ll get the best out of everyone if you tweak things a bit on an individual basis.

### **🌟 Motivation goes beyond productivity**

Just a few days ago, on the community, a member asked how do you know if a team is high-performing, linking back to this [interesting Linkedin thread](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/jamie-parsons-94884b22_in-a-recent-interview-i-was-asked-an-interesting-activity-7219622003312336898-b6rj?utm_source=share&utm_medium=member_desktop).

I thought a lot about it and eventually replied that **high energy + high agency**, to me, are among the strongest signs of high performance.

In good teams you can **feel the momentum**: you see people who are autonomous and unafraid to go after (big) things, as opposed to staying in their lane and do incremental work.

At the same time, most of the bad things that you can think of eventually deplete energy and motivation, so it also works the other way around.

Why am I writing all of this? Because motivation is such a basic catalyst that a lot of the qualities of motivated teams are just the qualities of good teams, and you can easily conflate them.

In my experience, motivated teams display three qualities, which work together in a self-reinforcing cycle:

1. 💡 **Proactive **— they go after things autonomously

1. 🔋 **Productive **— they do things *faster* and *better*

1. 🏋️‍♀️ **Resilient **— they embrace challenges and stick for longer

We [talk about productivity all the time](https://refactoring.fm/p/the-state-of-engineering-productivity?utm_source=publication-search), so no need to elaborate on that, while I found that being proactive and resilient are second-order effects that are less frequently covered, and worth discussing:

#### Proactivity 💡

Motivation makes people more confident and increases their agency. This is different from productivity: this is people going after things they wouldn’t have otherwise.

This leads to more, invaluable benefits:

- **Proactive problem-solving** — people actively seek out and address issues before they become critical.

- **Less management** — people require less day-to-day guidance, allowing managers to focus on strategy.

- **Grassroots innovation** — ideas cross-pollinate more easily, leading to more bottom-up innovation and initiatives.

#### Resilience 🏋️‍♀️

Motivated teams don't just perform better during smooth sailing, they are also an incredible asset in stormy waters. 

- **Courage** — they are more likely to embrace and address difficult challenges, rather than avoiding them.

- **Adaptability to change** — when faced with pivots or setbacks, they adjust more quickly and with less resistance.

- **Retention** — it goes without saying, motivated engineers have longer tenure and are less likely to quit.

So, if I sold you on turning your focus to motivating your team, how do you do so? Enter the pyramid of motivation 👇

### 🔺 The Pyramid of Motivation

I believe team motivation builds on four key elements, which work together like layers of a pyramid:

1. ❤️ **Psychological Safety**

1. 💬 **Communication**

1. 🎯 **Alignment**

1. 🏆 **Autonomy**

Each level builds on top of the previous one, so you should address them in order: 

- There is no good communication without psychological safety

- There cannot be alignment without good communication

- Good autonomy is enabled by alignment

![image](../assets/2276b56284c64f40.png)

So let’s talk about these layers one by one.

#### **1) Psychological safety **❤️

I believe the foundation of any motivated team is people who feel safe to take risks, voice their opinions, and fail without fear of repercussion.

In my experience, here are the best way to foster this:

1. **Encourage people to give feedback **— especially to managers. If you are a manager, ask for feedback all the time from your reports; also because, if you don’t, chances are you will not receive it. When people are comfortable at giving feedback, they also become more receptive to the one they receive

1. **Hold regular retrospectives **— and group feedback activities. Sharing and receiving feedback in a group is scary, but it’s also incredibly powerful. Mastering retrospectives gives your team superpowers. We wrote a full piece about them 👇

[https://refactoring.fm/p/retrospectives](https://refactoring.fm/p/retrospectives)

1. **Sponsor people** **for challenging work **— which means, things they don’t think they would be capable off. Push them a tiny bit beyond their comfort zone, and clarify that it’s ok if they fail. Make people used to taking on challenges.

#### **2) Good communication 💬**

Psychological safety create a foundation of **trust**, which enables good communication. 

To me, good communication is about three things:

- 🪟 **Transparency **— share everything, and for everything you share, share the why, and the why before the why. Share goals, plans, strategic choices, and what guided them. When people comprehend decisions and their motifs, they are more likely to be invested in the outcome.

- **⏱️ Frequency **— share information regularly, both in 1:1 and team contexts. E.g. don’t just present OKRs once a quarter; grade them bi-weekly, share your concerns, opportunities, early reasoning about next quarter, and so on.

- ↔️ **Bi-directionality** (*is this a real word?*) — create avenues for team input and demonstrate how it influences decisions. Share top-down direction, and allow for bottom-up contribution. E.g. look at [the W framework](https://refactoring.fm/i/146780022/the-w-framework). 

![image](../assets/11b7bae0fc0b4e45.jpg)

> *“Making sure to give the team a forum to get their ideas heard, and then following up with the management to make sure action is taken, and finally and most importantly highlighting to the team how their input was taken into account in decisions just changed the whole spirit and lifted the team's morale.”

*— [Camille Lahoud](https://www.linkedin.com/in/camille-lahoud-11864618/), CTO

#### **3) Alignment 🎯**

Effective communication creates alignment. There are two *versions* of alignment that you should take care of:

- **Alignment of actions to company goals **— when engineers know how their work contributes to the bigger picture, they find purpose in their daily tasks. Their agency and creativity also increases, as they can autonomously find more actions that cater to the goals.

- **Alignment of company goals to personal goals **— for each individual, design a progression route that benefits both them and the company. Talk with them about their career and aspirations, and try to create a path which marries their individual progress with company impact.

#### **4) Autonomy 🛠️**

At the peak of the pyramid there is autonomy and ownership. When people are aligned on what needs to be achieved, trust is in place, and good feedback is constantly shared, you can focus on delegation and *expanding *people’s scope.

Here is what you may do:

- **Define boundaries** — establish areas where engineers have decision-making power. Clarity allows them to act confidently within their sphere of influence. 

- **Delegate meaningful responsibility** — give ownership over significant parts of the product or process. More trust usually equals more motivation. We wrote a full article about delegation 👇

[https://refactoring.fm/p/how-to-delegate](https://refactoring.fm/p/how-to-delegate)

- **Encourage initiative** — create space for engineers to do more. Can they own some features from top to bottom? Can they propose and lead entire projects? Empowerment may feel scary but most often leads to innovative solutions and a strong sense of accomplishment.

> "*Motivated teams own their work. They are responsible not only for delivering value, but also have a say in how said value is delivered.*" — Aidan Curley

### **🏢 Examples from successful tech companies**

Finally, here are some interesting examples of how successful companies have implemented strategies to boost motivation, along with a real-world example from our community:

#### 1) Microsoft's Growth Mindset Culture 🌱

Under Satya Nadella's leadership, Microsoft transformed its culture and reignited growth. There is an interesting case study about how this was enabled by instilling a *growth mindset* culture:

- 📑 [**London Business School Case Study**](https://publishing.london.edu/cases/satya-nadella-at-microsoft-instilling-a-growth-mindset/)

- 📑 [**Fortune Article**](https://fortune.com/2024/05/20/satya-nadella-microsoft-culture-growth-mindset-learn-it-alls-know-it-alls/)

> "*The learn-it-all does better than the know-it-all*" — Satya Nadella  

In a nutshell, here is what this shift is about:

- 📚 **Emphasis on learning** —** **encouraged employees to view challenges as opportunities to grow, not prove themselves.

- ❌** Redefined failure** — positioned failure as a necessary part of innovation and learning.

- 🤝** Cross-team collaboration** — broke down silos, encouraging knowledge sharing across the organization.

Results:

- Microsoft's market cap grew from $300 billion to over $2 trillion.

- Significant innovations were delivered across the board: from cloud computing, to devices, to AI.

- Improved ability to attract and retain top talent (as measured by internal KPIs).

#### 2) Atlassian's "ShipIt" Days ⛵

Atlassian hosts quarterly [*ShipIt *](https://confluence.atlassian.com/display/SHIPIT/ShipIt+Day+FAQ)[days](https://confluence.atlassian.com/display/SHIPIT/ShipIt+Day+FAQ), where for 24 hours employees can work on any project they choose.

Engineers partner with designers, marketers, and others, and create top to bottom products, moving from concept to demo.

This isn’t your classic hackathon or bug fest: over 60% of ShipIt projects get *promoted* to official projects and get implemented in some form. Many of them led to major product innovations, like Jira Service Desk and Bitbucket Pipeline.

This is one of the strongest cases of bottom-up innovation that I could find. As Scott Farquhar, co-founder, notes:

> “S*hipIt Days are not just about the products we create, but about fostering a culture of innovation and empowerment.”*

#### 3) Twilio's "[*Wear the Customer's Shoes*](https://www.twilio.com/en-us/company/values)" Program 👟

Twilio has implemented a unique program that boosts motivation by connecting employees directly with the impact of their work:

- 🔌 **Hands-on customer experience** — every employee, including engineers, has to spend time using Twilio to build a real application.

- 📞 **Direct customer interaction **— engineers have to regularly spend time engaging with customers to understand their needs and challenges.

- 🙅‍♂️** "No shenanigans" rule** — employees must use the same APIs and documentation available to customers, so they can identify pain points first-hand.

The result is engineering gaining more empathy for user experience, which in turn leads to more user-centric product development.

Engineers also report increased motivation through direct understanding of the product’s real-world impact.

Jeff Lawson, CEO of Twilio, explains: 

> *"When you're wearing the customer's shoes, you feel their pain. You see their struggles. And that motivates you to solve those problems in a way that reading a spec never will.*"

#### 4) Community example — turning around an unmotivated team 🔄

Finally, Aidan Curley, Engineering Team Lead, shared a powerful real-world example of motivating a struggling team, taken from his time in education. Here's his account in full:

> *I inherited an [unmotivated] team of teachers in a language school when I started in my role as academic manager. I was somewhat able to turn things around.*

What I love about Aidan’s example is that all his actions can be copy-pasted in an engineering team and work equally well. It truly shows that good management is universal.

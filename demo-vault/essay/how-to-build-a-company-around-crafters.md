### 💡 Ideas

---

Kaz Nejatan is a lawyer by training, and, early in his career, he would spend most of his time in search of meeting rooms.

This is literally the first thing he told me last week as we sat down, precisely, to talk about meetings.

Today Kaz is COO and VP of Product of Shopify, and, earlier this year, he hit the headlines for abruptly deleting 12,000+ meetings from the calendars of its employees. *Abruptly,* here, means he just ran a script. Kaz and Tobi Lutke, CEO, snapped their Thanos’ fingers, and, at some point between last Christmas and New Year’s Eve, all recurring meetings with more than three participants were gone.

So, as I did my homework for this chat, I felt prepared. I had a lot of questions: what’s the difference between good and bad meetings, how you can foster async/written comms in engineering teams, how to make remote work effective, and more.

The interview, though, turned out to be about much more. Shopify has a peculiar culture that completely permeates how people work. Understanding how and why they removed meetings requires understanding what Shopify believes about the world: what they cherish, what they fear, the lengths they would go to protect the former, and to ruthlessly fight the latter.

So, here is what we will cover today:

- **Chaos Monkey **— the raw numbers, before and after, of the operation that purged 12,000+ meetings.

- **Chaotic and different** — two cornerstones that protect Shopify’s culture.

- **Crafters vs managers** — the key conflict around which companies are built.

- **Empowering crafters **— how to build a company that is organized around building.

Let’s dive in 👇

### 🐒 Chaos Monkey

**04:24:95 Removing meetings: the numbers**

One of the key themes of Refactoring is how to make product/engineering teams productive.

This is always hard to address, because different people have different needs, partly based on their job — e.g. engineers vs managers.

So, one of the most divisive and debated topics in this space is, of course, meetings. Most tech companies these days agree about the need of reducing meetings, but it’s rare to see *big tech* actually walk the talk the same way Shopify did.

In just a few days, without notice, Shopify automatically deleted all recurring meetings with more than three people, from everyone’s calendars.

They dubbed this move *Chaos Monkey* — a nod to Netflix’s own [Chaos Monkey](https://netflix.github.io/chaosmonkey/): a tool that tests infrastructure resilience by randomly shutting down parts of it. Shopify’s Chaos Monkey did other things, too, like deleting some Slack channels, but the *meetings* angle got most of the headlines.

But what were those meetings? Kaz says that the first, very practical effect of this, was banning all *all-hands *meetings*.*

At Shopify this is a big deal: each person typically belongs to multiple teams, some of which are cross-functional and vertical to some product area, while others are about some horizontal craft. There is flexibility around how these teams are managed, but typically each of them would have a weekly all-hands. 

Kaz calculated that the average person was spending about 6 hours a week just in all-hands.

Fast forward 6 months, after the dust has settled, here are some numbers:

- -26% meetings on Wednesday (the most *dense* day)

- -16% meetings during other days of the week

- On track to ship +18% projects this year, thanks to faster turnaround in almost every part of the production cycle, from specs to PRs.

Now, in most companies — especially big, public corps like Shopify, with 10K+ headcount — there would be roundtables, change management plans, tests run on a small subset of the teams, and impact measurements, before expanding the initiative to the whole workforce. In Shopify, instead, this was done in just a few days, under festivities, and without notice for most people involved.

So, to understand how Shopify can afford to operate at the speed of thought of a small startup, we have to understand how Shopify *thinks* about itself in the first place.

### 💥 Chaotic and different

Shopify’s customers are entrepreneurs, creators, and all people who are successful thanks to their own ingenuity and resourcefulness.

Try to picture that.

Shopify lives and breathes entrepreneurship — it wins by empowering people with tools that support those qualities.

So, for many years (it was founded in 2006), Shopify survived as an underdog in a world of e-commerce behemoths by displaying the same qualities of its customers: creativity, speed, flexibility.

Kaz and Tobi know that, and as the company grows larger and larger, they hold on to the culture that made it special, even if that means embracing being *different* and a bit *chaotic*.

#### Different

**01:49:52 Bias for being different**

Shopify has a bias for being different. When most companies zigs, it looks for opportunities to zag.

Shopify is remote-first since long before covid, they built their [own version of JIRA](https://shopify.engineering/running-engineering-program-guide), called GSD (Get Shit Done), and have a lot of custom tooling for which they could otherwise use off-the-shelf products.

This is, of course, both an opportunity and a risk. Custom tools often turn into nightmares to maintain, and I personally do not recommend startups to build their own things unless they are *core* to their business.

But the argument here, by Kaz, is that such tools *are* core to Shopify, because Shopify is successful *because* is different, and for being different you just need different tools. 

So, even though different doesn’t always mean better, and you will definitely make mistakes, such a bias is a net positive for the company.

But how do you stay different when you are a public company with more than 10,000 employees?

It turns out, by embracing some chaos 👇

#### Chaotic

**13:44:00 Digital by default, RTO**

Kaz is not a man who steps away from controversy. He believes most big companies “*are fucked*” and “*usually have no idea of what they’re doing*”, so you shouldn’t copy them.

To avoid turning into one of them, Shopify moves at the speed of a team 1/100 its size, and routinely accepts some level of internal chaos, trusting that people will eventually figure out.

Kaz told me an emblematic story about this.

When the team was still in the hundreds, and before it was remote-first, they had to move from their office to new, larger one. The lease for the old office expired one month before the new one started, so there would be this one-month hiatus where no office was available.

Now, 9 out of 10 of CEOs of what was already a sizeable company would have probably just extended the current lease by a few months, not only to cover the gap, but also to get create a comfortable cushion during which both offices would be available.

Tobi said: “*screw that, for one month everyone will just figure out where to work from*”.

And it worked. No big drama, and that set the foundations for eventually making the company remote-first (or, as they say, *digital-first*).

But why do other companies *die*? Kaz has a theory 👇

### ⚔️ Crafters vs managers

Kaz believes that at the core of the issues that plague most large companies, there is a conflict between what Shopify calls *crafters* (e.g. engineers, designers) and *managers*.

There are way more crafters than managers at any company, yet systems, schedules, and big decisions are mostly dictated by managers. The problem with this is that crafters and managers do extremely different work, and get energy from different things — as per Paul Graham’s essay, [Maker’s Schedule, Manager’s Schedule](http://www.paulgraham.com/makersschedule.html) — so optimizing systems for managers makes good *crafting* eventually die.

Kaz says this is a historical problem: your average company structure today is very hierarchical — this is based on ideas that come from decades ago, and are suboptimal for today’s tech teams.

This is not to discount the crucial role of managers, but Kaz argues that companies should be organized around crafters’ productivity, not the other way around.

Conversely, when companies get organized around managers, they eventually start producing *career ladders* instead of actual products. People play promotion games, politics, and try to game the system to rise the ranks, because that’s where incentives are.

So, Shopify wants to build a company that is organized around crafters, instead. But how do you do that?

### 🛠️ Empowering crafters

How do you build a culture around crafters? Here is what Shopify does:

#### 1) Separate career tracks 🐾

Shopify has dual career tracks for managers and ICs. This is a reality in many companies today, but Shopify is adamant about this in two ways:

1. Becoming a manager is never a promotion, but always a step to the side on the ladder. It’s a different job, and the company is clear about this.

1. Crafters and managers have exactly the same opportunities in terms of career advancement and compensation. This is not trivial: many companies sport dual tracks but managers still get to earn and advance more than ICs.

#### 2) Default to no ❌

When your manager asks you for a meeting, Shopify encourages that your default answer should be no, unless you believe you can have it without losing productivity.

Conversely, managers know they should get information through *systems* rather than *people*, so when something’s missing the solution should be to make the systems better, while meetings are the last resort.

About this, Kaz tells me that Shopify treats meetings like a missing API: you won’t have an API for *everything* at any given time, and it’s ok, but you can be mindful that that’s what you should tend towards.

#### 3) Remove things ✂️

Across several experiments, [researchers demonstrated](https://www.nature.com/articles/d41586-021-00592-0) that, as humans, we have a bias towards addressing problems by *adding* new things (e.g. components, initiatives, ideas) rather than removing existing ones. This is unfortunate as *subtraction*** **is often** ***faster and cheaper* than addition.

Shopify, instead, has a bias towards removing things, which, coupled with the *chaotic *vibes, means it is not afraid to burning things down before building them back up.

### 📌 Bottom line

Shopify is a big tech that is terrified of becoming a big tech.

More than any other company of their scale that I know, they go to extraordinary lengths to retain what made their culture special as a startup. This clashes with their size, sometimes, but it’s a calculated risk.

“*People are more resilient than we think — they can handle a little bit of chaos. Eventually they just figure out”. *This insight by Kaz is key to understanding how Shopify thinks of itself.

I can’t help but thinking that you *can’t* pull this off unless that’s how you have always operated: you have always hired fast-movers, and you have always focused on crafters. Kaz studied as a lawyer, but he writes code almost everyday. Tobi is the CEO and pushes to production weekly.

You can’t just retrofit this.

Shopify is unique in how it takes the *hustle*, bias for action, and intensity of classic startups, and blends it with the *intentionality*, systems, and care for crafting of async, full-remote companies.

By looking at most examples in our industry, we may have lived under the impression that these qualities are incompatible. You are either an *Elon* company, or you are a GitLab, or a Doist.

Shopify says there is a third way — but brace yourself, it will be a ride!

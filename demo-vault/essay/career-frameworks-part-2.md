### 🔨 How to create the framework

How do you get started when you are doing this from scratch? 

Whatever process you adopt to create such framework, you will spend time and energy to align your ideas with those of various stakeholders. So, if you haven’t them already, I would start with creating general **engineering principles 👇**

#### Start with principles 🌟

When you need to converge with people around something, going top-down from first principles is often the most efficient way. On the contrary, when you start bottom-up, you may find people disagreeing on things without really knowing why.

For career frameworks, engineering principles are even more useful because they are like **high-level expectations** that stand true for all roles and levels. They are not only useful to bring people together, they are groundwork that you will actually use down the road.

To learn more about creating good engineering principles, you can check out this previous Refactoring article 👇

[https://refactoring.fm/p/principles?s=w](https://refactoring.fm/p/principles?s=w)

If you are skeptical about the usefulness of this, let’s make a concrete example taken from one of the most popular career frameworks out there: the one from [Rent the Runway](https://docs.google.com/spreadsheets/d/1k4sO6pyCl_YYnf0PAXSBcX776rNcTjSOqDxZ5SDty-4/edit#gid=0), created by [Camille Fournier](https://twitter.com/skamille).

The **Senior Engineer I** role has the following description of what is expected of their technical skills:

> ***Demonstrates knowledge of industry trends****, our infrastructure and our build system, including maven, jenkins, and git.*

The Senior Engineer II role has this one:

> *Provides technical advice and weighs in on technical decisions that impact other teams or the company at large. ****Researches and proposes new technologies****.*

I have put in bold two parts that are relevant to me, about staying on top of tech trends.

How do you come up with these? A healthy way is to have a broad company principle which values **personal growth **and encourages **spending time to learn new things**. The principle triggers discussions about how to make this happen, and you may end up allocating some fixed engineering time on this, creating personal OKRs, or whatever.

Without the principle, it is less trivial to recognize this as a company-wide need and to agree with people about it.

Of course, things do not flow top-down only. While principles may influence role descriptions, the opposite is also true. You may recognize the need for a new principle while in the process of creating role descriptions, and iterate from there.

#### Start with simple guidelines 📋

The best way to think at a career framework is to consider it as *guidelines* about what is expected of people at work. 

You start with principles because they are what is expected of *everybody*, and then create levels when it is time to separate guidelines across the various roles.

When you replace *career framework* with *guidelines*, it is clear that these are useful even when your team is small and you have maybe just a couple of different roles / levels.

Even if you don’t do performance reviews, you don’t have hiring managers and all the things people usually associate with career frameworks, guidelines help people behave consistently and help you have grounded conversations about their growth.

In my experience, it’s never too early for this.

#### Levels come before skills 🪜

Big career frameworks have usually separate concepts of levels and skills. A same set of skills is defined and reused across levels and tracks.

Some examples:

- [Rent The Runway](https://docs.google.com/spreadsheets/d/1k4sO6pyCl_YYnf0PAXSBcX776rNcTjSOqDxZ5SDty-4/edit#gid=0) has four *skills*: Technical Skills, Get Stuff Done, Impact, Communication & Leadership. 

- [Etsy](https://etsy.github.io/Etsy-Engineering-Career-Ladder/competencies.html) has 5 *competencies*: Delivery, Domain Expertise, Problem Solving, Communication, Leadership

- [CircleCI](https://docs.google.com/spreadsheets/d/131XZCEb8LoXqy79WWrhCX4sBnGhCM1nAIz4feFZJsEo/edit#gid=0) has 5 *areas*: Technical Skills, Delivery, Feedback/Collaboration/Communication, Leadership, Strategic Impact. These areas are further divided into 15 attributes and (*gasp*) 27 individual themes.

Now, if you are starting fresh, my advice is start with levels and ignore skills.

Skills are an abstraction — like with software, you don’t want to create them prematurely. It is better to have some duplication in the form of level expectations that repeat themselves, than to create a skill that doesn’t really fit and you have to revisit soon.

The other risk of working on skills is to focus too much on what people **should know**, rather than what people **should do**. In my experience it is vastly more important to get the latter right than the former. Levels should primarily reflect how work happens in your team.

So, each upper level should bring two elements:

- **You do some of the things you did before, but better**

- **You now do other things you didn’t do before**

For an effective progression, you need both. In fact:

- The ***old things but better*** are those that allow you to figure out who is already performing at higher level and is worthy of being promoted. For example, taking initiative on tasks before being assigned to them, or being able to own larger projects from top to bottom. 

- The **new things** are the responsibilities that are peculiar for that level. What are those? It depends! Might be mentoring junior hires, contributing to hiring, creating design docs, managing projects end-to-end, you name it. 

If there aren’t *new things *that are brought by an upper level, it is probably not worthy to create the level at all. 

A good tactic to create levels correctly is to identify an **ideal person in your team** for it. Ask yourself: what makes them perfect for the role? What do they do? And abstract from there!

### 🎓 Skills

Once you have a few solid levels you may want to create abstractions around the main skills that are required and how they progress throughout the levels.

There are many examples of skill structures — you can check out some of the ones above to get an idea. To me, a good starting point focuses on three main things:

- 🔨 **Technical Skills** — pure tech chops. Coding, testing, debugging, system design, and so on. 

- 💬 **Communication / Collaboration** — how you work in a team, deal with stakeholders, give and receive feedback, etc.

- 🎯 **Impact **— getting things done and creating value for the business. This is important to reward balanced trade-offs that are not only about technical excellence but about what’s ultimately best for the business. It also captures how more senior folks can have a wider impact by working on larger systems and projects.

You can have many more skills than these, and some specific ones may depend on your culture (what you value and what not). If you want to start simple, though, you may consider the three above.

How do you identify which skills are required by which levels? Two ideas:

- ❌ **Anti-behavior** — think of what you *don’t *want. Think of past examples where people did not behave the way you wanted. Sometimes it is easier to work backwards from there.

- 🏅 **Think of your best people** —  just like with levels, identify the qualities of your best people. What makes them special?

### 🔀 Tracks

Levels do not only progress linearly. They sometimes branch different paths, which means different career progressions altogether.

Here are the some common configurations 👇

#### 2️⃣ Two tracks: ICs & Managers

This is the most popular one. It is 2022 and most people agree that managers do a different job than individual contributors — but both have valuable impact on the business, so they can progress their career in either direction.

Career ladders with two tracks often start as a single trunk, with a couple of levels which are in common, and then branch off a managerial vs IC (individual contributor) path. 

![image](../assets/fd73d14d65fb4219.png)

At the moment of branching, the IC counterparts of Engineering Managers are often called Tech Leads. Boundaries between the two roles can be tricky and largely depend on the stage and size of your team. I wrote more about this in a previous article 👇

[https://refactoring.fm/p/non-technical-managers](https://refactoring.fm/p/non-technical-managers)

#### 1️⃣ One track: ICs → Managers

Even though people talk of the two tracks all the time, there may be situations where a single track makes sense.

If you are a small team, say under 20 people, you may not need full-time managers and you may conflate the management + tech leadership duties on the same people. 

In such a scenario, senior engineers can only be promoted into managers. 

I know, outrageous!

But in small companies there might be no way for an IC to progress further, because you simply don’t have the kind of work for which you need Staff Engineers and the likes.

So, will all the people want to turn into managers? No!

Are you providing equal growth opportunities to people want to stay ICs? No!

And that’s fine! Not all opportunities exist everywhere at a given time. Just be transparent about what exists in your company and what not.

![image](../assets/6d4b7a3c078f43e9.png)

#### 3️⃣ Three tracks: ICs + Leaders + Managers

In most scenarios, IC senior roles hold leadership responsibilities that contribute to a larger impact than coding alone. You may drive decision making, mentor teammates, and promote good tech practices.

There are specific situations, however, where an IC impact can grow while staying closely tied to some technical specialty. This usually happens around very specific areas, like databases, hardware, or infrastructure.

So you may have a dedicated track for these *super specialists*, but it should be made of way fewer people than the other two. It’s more of an exception to the rule.

Pat Kua covered this situation in his great article about the [Trident Model of Career Development](https://www.thekua.com/atwork/2019/02/the-trident-model-of-career-development/).

### 🚚 Rollout

Rolling out a new career framework is tricky because people are very personally and directly affected by it.

In my experience, a good rollout starts with a good creation process. This is true for pretty much anything that is company-wide: OKRs, career frameworks, remote policies, etc.

The challenge of such processes is that they should strike the right balance between being participated by the team (bottom-up) and following the vision of the leadership (top-down).

- **Top-down direction** delivers consistency and fast decision-making.

- **Bottom-up contribution** makes things grounded with your team’s reality, creates consensus, and leads to better decisions in the end.

I am a fan of the [**W Framework**](https://review.firstround.com/the-secret-to-a-great-planning-process-lessons-from-airbnb-and-eventbrite)** **for this, created by Lenny Rachitsky and Nels Gilbreth.

It is made of four steps:

1. **Context**: Leadership shares a high-level strategy with Teams

1. **Plans**: Teams respond with proposed plans

1. **Integration**: Leadership integrates into a single plan, and shares with Teams

1. **Buy-in**: Teams make final tweaks, confirm buy-in, and get rolling

This is a generic strategy that you can apply to pretty much anything that requires wide participation.

![image](../assets/5edcb14716e54144.png)

What may it look like for a career framework? Here is a possible implementation:

1. **Context: **Leadership provides high level direction: fundamental levels and tracks based on current team composition and future strategy.

1. **Plans:** Teams (e.g. your senior leaders) come back with the qualities and responsibilities they would like to see for the various levels.

1. **Integration: **Leadership challenges, integrates these elements and shares back with teams.

1. **Buy-in: **Final tweaks, Q&A + people are assigned to the levels.

As a practical example, here is how [Hovhannes Gulyan](https://www.linkedin.com/in/hovhannesgulyan/?originalSubdomain=pl), Senior EM, is working through this process at his company:

1. *I've shared the draft with senior engineers/leads in my team*

1. *We improved the draft together. It was also useful to observe their reaction, note down their questions.*

1. *Next step is to verify the list with a person from each level and see if it matches with their skills.*

1. *I've also verified the custom career levels framework against more high level/organization level guidelines*

1. *Share with my manager (VP) and get feedback from him*

1. *After all of that I'll share it publicly with my team 10-15 people. By that time career levels will be reviewed and I'll also be ready for most common questions that I've received during prior reviews.*

1. *Next step is to make the list publicly available and review them on 1:1 with everyone and verify where they would put themselves. I have no intention to downgrade anyone, just make them aware to fill in the gaps. And for people who are over-performing will be next on list for promotions during the next round.*

### 🔍 Assessing people’s levels

Out of the various steps, assigning people to roles and getting their buy-in is probably the trickiest. Here are the various ways you can do it:

#### 1) **Managers assign people **

Managers may hold 1:1s with their reports and give them the chance of rating themselves / advocating for a specific level, and then apply their judgment for a final decision.

#### 2) Peer review

Like in a [360 review](https://lattice.com/library/what-is-a-360-degree-review), people vote on the qualities of people who work with. This approach has the advantage of getting easy buy-in from people, because it feels fair and not based on personal biases. However, it is the most effective for the lower levels of the ladder, while it is tricky to evaluate managers and more senior staff this way. 

#### 3) Internal interview

This might either replace or complement the other methods. If there is disagreement about some evaluation you can run interviews with the candidate and establish their level this way.

### 📝 Assigning levels

In almost any scenario I have found it useful to give everybody the chance of rating themselves. 

Such conversation makes people feel they have a say on this — and they should — even when your final decision is different from theirs. Also, ~80% of the times they get the level right.

Disagreement is useful, too. If anyone is thinking they are performing better than they actually are, this is very important to know! As a manager, you can guide them to fill the gaps and reach that level.

You may hold a 1:1 with each report for assessing this at the beginning, and another one at the end to communicate their new level. People want to know two things:

- **What is expected of them at their level**

- **What they should do to get next level**

Make sure you cover them, and include detailed feedback about your evaluation, especially in case of disagreement.

Finally, whatever measures you take, **mistakes will happen**. Consider creating a shorter promotion cycle than usual after you introduce the framework — to be able to correct mistakes soon and reward over-performers.

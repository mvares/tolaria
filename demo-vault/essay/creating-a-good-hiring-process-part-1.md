Steve Jobs was obsessed by hiring. 

He believed it was his most important job, and repeated this countless times in interviews and speeches.

> *My #1 job here at Apple is to make sure that the top 100 people are A+ players. And everything else will take care of itself. [...] If the top 50 people are right, it just cascades down throughout the whole organization. [...] A+ players hire A+ players, B players hire C players. Do you get it?*

I have worked all my career in startups of various sizes. Startups are high-growth and have limited headcount, so each single person has an outsized impact on the business. 

In such conditions, hiring well is a matter of life and death.  

In fact, if hiring well fixes most problems, the converse is also true. The damage caused by hiring the wrong people is immense, and can literally kill your company.

Joel Spolsky, legendary programmer and founder of Trello and Stack Overflow, explained this well:

> *It is much, much better to reject a good candidate than to accept a bad candidate. Bad employees demoralize the good employees. And they might be bad programmers but really nice people or maybe they really need this job, so you can’t bear to fire them, or you can’t fire them without pissing everybody off.*

So how do you hire well?

### Hiring Process

In the past couple of weeks I talked with tens of managers and business owners about their hiring practices. In the Refactoring community this became the most discussed topic of all times 👇

![image](../assets/ff06d59a134141e6.png)

Why are people so eager to talk about hiring? Because, as you know, the market is incredibly hot today. I don’t know any manager who isn’t struggling to adapt to the new conditions brought by the remote shift and [The Great Resignation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_Resignation).

Out of these conversations, it is clear that good hiring is first and foremost a matter of process. You do it continuously, train people for it, and measure and improve it over time.

Hiring well requires to manage four main stages:

This article covers the *planning* and *sourcing* stages, while the next will focus on *selecting* and *converting*. Together, the two-part series will cover everything you need to know about creating and managing a good hiring process.

These articles are also the result of the most extensive research I have ever done on a topic — which is well-deserved given its importance.

Let’s dive in 👇

### 📈 Planning for growth

Good hiring starts with good planning.

[Julien Evano](https://www.linkedin.com/in/julienevano), Head of Engineering who oversaw the hiring process of multiple startups at various stages, told me about his framework: 

1. 🗺️ Create a **clear plan** for the teams’ structure — for now and future growth iterations.

1. 🪜 Define a **career progression** framework — to bring consistency to the various roles.

1. 👤 Identify the perfect **roles **and** profiles** — that can complement the team.

1. 🎖️ **Prepare your team** for hiring — by appointing recruiters and allocating engineers time.

Planning is paramount to make the recruiting happen at the right time.

In high-growth environments, the business grows fast and so do problems. It takes 6+ months to hire and onboard an engineer — in that time, a small issue may have turned into a gigantic bottleneck. You better act when problems are small.

Let’s see each of these steps 👇

#### 🗺️ Plan your teams’ structure for growth

If your company has ambitious growth targets, like growing 2x or 3x in a given time, it is impossible to do so without significantly changing how the team is organized.

Anticipating such changes allows you to get clarity about the roles and profiles that you need.

Here are a couple of previous Refactoring articles that reflect on this: 

- [**The Three Stages of Engineering Teams**](https://refactoring.fm/p/the-three-stages-of-engineering-teams?s=w)

- [**Feature Teams vs Durable Teams**](https://refactoring.fm/p/feature-teams-vs-durable-teams-?s=w)

To learn more, you can also check the [Structuring Teams](https://library.refactoring.club/dl/engineering-mgmt/s/895014/r/a.IbVQ7YFSfCsgNsYK.HvZA) section of the Library.

#### 🪜 Define a career progression framework

As your company grows larger, you need consistency in the way you assess people’s skills and responsibilities. This looks trivial, but it is easy for different teams to end up with a different idea of what a “*Senior Engineer*” looks like.

Progression frameworks define what is expected by people in the various roles. Here is an [example](https://docs.google.com/spreadsheets/d/131XZCEb8LoXqy79WWrhCX4sBnGhCM1nAIz4feFZJsEo/edit#gid=0) by CircleCI.

It can be simpler than that, of course, based on the stage of your company. Create something good enough to align stakeholders and avoid confusion.

For inspiration, check [progression.fyi](https://www.progression.fyi/) — a collection of frameworks from great tech companies around the world.

#### 👤 Identify roles and profiles

After you created your growth plan and a consistent definition of the various roles, you can ask yourself *who* you need to get on board.

Here are two crucial aspects you should plan for:

- **Junior vs Senior breakdown **— in the past I wrote about the benefits of [mixing seniority in engineering teams](https://refactoring.fm/p/the-benefits-of-mixed-seniority-in). There are many, including costs, growth opportunities, and reduced turnover. Most steps to make this happen require planning: setting up internships with universities, be active in events and conferences, and more.

- **Diversity** — likewise, finding people from non-traditional talent pools requires planning and effort. This is much easier to achieve if you start from early stages, because the more diverse is your team early on, the more diversity it will attract in the future. Conversely, a large “homogeneous” workforce becomes very hard to unsettle in later stages.

#### 🎖️ **Appoint recruiters**

By recruiters, here I mean people in your company who oversee the sourcing and selection of candidates. Recruiters are responsible for the general hiring process, advocate to allocate the right time, and push things forward.

Many companies believe that they don’t need recruiters and that hiring can be managed in a distributed way across the team. This is a fallacy, in many ways similar to those saying they don’t need managers.

Being a recruiter, just like being a manager, is first and foremost a **behaviour**.

In small teams, you may not need a full-time manager, but you still need someone who exhibits **manager behaviour** by taking care of 1:1s, alignment, and so on. As the team grows, this might become a full-time job for someone, and you get your first full-fledged engineering managers.

It is the same for recruiters. As a rule of thumb, a full-time recruiter can manage [**2-3 hires per month**](https://martinfowler.com/articles/bottlenecks-of-scaleups/02-talent.html#InvestInTheHiringProcess). If you do the math, this means 2-3 hours a day for each position.

Hell, that looks like a lot! Sure, but let’s take a look at everything a recruiter does:

- **Manage hiring channels** — job boards, communities, referrals, and more. This includes keeping a pulse on the market and outreaching to candidates directly.

- **Screen candidates** — to minimise the time wasted on bad candidates.

- **Deliver an exceptional candidate experience** — throughout the whole selection process, by managing the relationship with them, collecting feedback, and setting them up for success.

- **Relay with the team **— during the interview process, collecting their feedback.

- **Negotiate and close** — make candidates accept the offers.

- **Improve the hiring process **— collect feedback from all parties (candidates and the team), track KPIs, make everything better over time.

Do you absolutely need to do all of this? No. In fact, most companies don’t.

Is it worth to pay someone to do it, though? Most certainly yes.

#### ⏱️ Allocate engineers time

Recruiters do not do everything by themselves — engineers on your team need to be deeply involved in the process.

Engineers do many things:

- **Design the interview process** — create and update exercises, take-home tests, questions, and more.

- **Interview candidates **— and manage the subsequent feedback process.

- **Contribute to writing the job post** — setting requirements and responsibilities.

- **Bring in new candidates** — via referrals.

Time spent on hiring should be taken into account in the general planning, because it is sizeable.

Luca Marchi, Software Engineer at Picnic, reports on this:

> *All the senior engineers on the team have ****2 hours per week**** allocated for the interviews.*

In my experience, you may need even more time than that. Interviews require prep work, writing notes, decision meetings afterwards, and more. It is not uncommon for senior engineers to spend >10% of their time on hiring.

Joel Spolsky similarly advocates to involve most people in the team in interviews:

> *You should always try to have at least ****six people**** interview each candidate that gets hired, including at least five who would be peers of that candidate. [...] If even two of the six interviewers thinks that a person is not worth hiring, don’t hire them.*

Six people might be too much for someone, but the main takeaway is that hiring needs wide consensus. That’s because you are not only testing for technical prowess, but also wider cultural fit. You want people to feel 1) excited to work with the candidate, and 2) committed to the hiring decision.

### 📑 Sourcing candidates

Sourcing candidates is your *top of the funnel *stage. It is a crucial step because it is one of the few entirely under your control. Omar Bohsali from Coinbase explains this well 👇

> *While you can’t directly impact the accept rate and whether your team hits its hiring goals, you can control the number of applications you review and the number of candidates you reach out to by generating more lead lists or finding more sources of candidates.*

Last year I wrote an article about [the three (+1) main hiring channels](https://refactoring.fm/p/the-three-hiring-channels-?s=w) for your hiring. Similarly, Stripe identifies [four main channels](https://review.firstround.com/How-Stripe-built-one-of-Silicon-Valleys-best-engineering-teams) for sourcing candidates: 

- **Referrals **(network)

- **Outbound **(communities, social media)

- **Inbound **(job boards, aggregators, organic traffic)

- **Recruiters **(head hunters)

Let’s see them one by one 👇

#### Referrals

Referrals is the best way to bring high quality candidates. Full stop. It will naturally be your main hiring channel for a long time.

You may want to encourage referrals by setting generous rewards for people who bring them in.

How generous? A head hunter usually takes 15-20% of the yearly gross salary. Within your team, you may settle around ~10% after the probation period, and make the formula a bit simpler by simply defining fixed amounts based on the career level. 

Pamela Gotti, CTO at Credimi, leverage referrals to make people participate actively in the hiring process 👇

> *We do encourage everyone in the engineering team to be owner of the hiring process too, meaning that referrals are really encouraged, and everybody is aware that the responsibility if growing the team is not only hr's only :) 

The [hiring] process for referrals is the same, except that the first [screening] step is not done by HR but by whoever reaches out to the candidate.*

The flip side of referrals is that they hurt diversity. By relying too much on them, you risk creating a workforce with too much of the same background. You should pair referrals with a good outbound strategy 👇

#### Outbound

Outbound means reaching out to candidates directly. Contrary to referrals, though, you don’t know these guys just as well.

So, where do these candidates come from? 

- **Your product community** — in case your product has an active community, you may reach out to the most promising members there. This is one of the top reasons to build a community for your product, especially if it is developer-focused.

- **Other communities** — hang out and be active in communities where you can find potential new hires. This, eventually, builds trust and makes outbound easier. Several people got hired via the [Refactoring Community](https://refactoring.substack.com/p/community) already!

- **Social media** — Linkedin and Twitter are great places to build relationships and contact promising candidates. Contrary to popular belief, such outbound is not all spam. If you invest in your online presence and build good interactions, you will find good opportunities over time.

Overall, investing in referrals and outbound is paramount because the best people are usually not in the market. They just don’t send CVs.

The hotter the market, the stronger your referral + outbound strategy should be.

#### Inbound

These are people coming from job boards, your careers page, and job aggregators. 

Results are hit or miss, and seem to get worse and worse over time. Most companies I talk with — even high-caliber, well-funded startups — struggle to attract good candidates organically. 

#### Recruiters

Recruiters (external ones) have a spectacularly bad reputation in tech — mostly deservedly so. In reality, not all recruiters are created equal, and you have good and bad ones. 

In [The Three Hiring Channels](https://refactoring.fm/p/the-three-hiring-channels-?s=w), I listed two pieces of advice to work with them:

- **Consider them for leadership positions**: upper management and executive roles are often hired via recruiters, because candidates are rare, and having good reach via other channels is hard. Sometimes, before you worry about signal-to-noise, it's important to get any signal at all.

- **Build long lasting relationships with them**: the longer you work with a recruiter, the better it becomes at understanding what you need and what works for you. Context improves over time, so don't give up early.

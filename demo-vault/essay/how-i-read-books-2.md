# How I Read Books

### ✍️ Post

---

I have a complicated relationship with non-fiction books.

I have always read a lot — today, of course, as I do research for the newsletter, but even in the past — but not many books. 

I believe we can trace the controversy back to 2013-2014. At that time, I was a young CTO, extremely *hungry* for good material. Newsletters and quality articles were less of a thing back then, so for you professional growth you mostly had… books.

And boy they disappointed me. All the time.

Most of them seemed to me little more than glorified blog posts: the best ideas could fit ~3000 words, and were instead diluted into 50,000. So, for the following decade, you can count the business books I read on my hands.

Then, something surprising happened last year: **I read 12 books**. 

I can’t stress enough how big of a change this was for me — 12 books is probably more than all the (business) books I had read until then.

![The books I read in the last 12 months. I had already read Principles and The Hard Things, but it was a long time ago so I re-read them.](../assets/ee50d720a8934c43.png)

So, this article is the story of how I unexpectedly became a book reader, why I enjoy doing so now, and the process that works for me.

Here is the agenda:

- 📚 **Book Club(s)** — how accountability changed everything for me.

- 📋 **Using summaries** — why top-down reading is the best reading.

- 📕 **Reading the full book** — why it is worth it and how I actually read.

- ✍️ **Writing a review** — clearing my thoughts and storing them for the eternity

- 🌱 **Extracting notes** — the last step which ties in to my note-taking workflow.

Let’s dive in!

---

### 📚 Book Clubs

Exactly one year ago we started a [Book Club](https://refactoring.fm/p/book-club) in the Refactoring community. 

In the club, we pick a book every two months, read it on our own, and discuss it all together in a live community call at the end of the period.

Half of the books I read last year come from it:

- [Trillion Dollar Coach](https://refactoring.fm/p/trillion-dollar-coach)

- [Team Topologies](https://refactoring.fm/p/team-topologies)

- [Good Strategy / Bad Strategy](https://refactoring.fm/p/good-strategy-bad-strategy)

- [No Rules Rules](https://refactoring.fm/p/no-rules-rules)

- [Atomic Habits](https://refactoring.fm/p/atomic-habits)

- An Elegant Puzzle (still to be reviewed!)

The book club gave me two things:

1. **Strong accountability **— reading books is now literally part of my job. Book reviews also become newsletter articles, so there is no way I can give up on this.

1. **Easy selection **— for a long time I had a messy backlog of books I wanted to read, and choosing the next one always felt like a chore. The book club has a board where people can upvote entries, so every time we just pick the most voted one. Easy peasy.

![Our very first book review!](../assets/737fc28448b040d5.jpg)

The book club activity *compounded* nicely and unexpectedly with another facet of my life: my wife has always been a big reader. She keeps a book by her bed, and always reads a few pages before sleep.

This existing habit gave me the perfect *anchor *for my own, newfound, one — so I started doing the same, thus replacing the mindless scrolling on social media, and the blitz games on [chess.com](http://chess.com/) (which I would invariably lose).

After a while, this led to another surprise: my wife and I started talking about the books we were respectively reading. She had always told me about her books, but the fact that now I, too, was reading something, created more common ground and led to more interesting conversations.

It’s like another mini book club, even if we read different things.

#### Flywheels

So, reading for me has become an incredibly high leverage activity:

- 🎓 **Learning** — it makes me learn new things.

- 💬 **Community** — it makes the Refactoring community better.

- ✏️ **Writing** — it gives me things to write about on the newsletter.

- 🏡 **Family** — it even improves the relationship with my wife.

The lesson I learned here is to always try to create flywheels and synergies so that 1+1=3.

If you have a habit you are trying to adopt and you are struggling with it, think of how you can get more value out of it. Can you do it with your friends & family? Can you make it useful for something else?

This is all part of the *why. *Another big change in my reading, though, has been in the *how*.

![image](../assets/2ff1c57937624c40.png)

I read books in four stages:

1. 📋 **I read the summary**

1. 📕 **I read the full book**

1. ✍️ **I write a review**

1. 🌱 **I extract notes**

This looks like a lot of work, but 1) I don’t do it for all books, and 2) for good books it’s 100% worth it.

Let’s go through these steps 👇

---

### 📋 I start with the summary

Reading a non-fiction book from top to bottom takes usually ~4-5 hours. For many people this is a big investment, which means two things:

1. You should only do it when it’s worth it

1. You better get the most out of it

Som whenever I can (== whenever it is available) I start a book by reading its summary.

There are many services out there which provide summaries — I mostly use Shortform because it has a nice workflow: I take highlights there, these are sent to Readwise, which in turn are sent to my Notion.

![image](../assets/3ad83f22115b42e7.png)

A good summary is a ~30-min reading, which equals to 10-15% of the full book. This is a great deal to me, for two reasons:

#### 1) Stop early 🛑

For most books I start reading, I actually stop at the summary. I haven’t kept track precisely, but I think it’s about 60%. And for many of them, I don’t even finish the summary.

There are so many books out there which *I am not sure *I should read, for the most disparate reasons:

- Maybe they are not that good

- Maybe I know a lot of the topic already

- Maybe I am not *that* interested in the topic

I can figure out all these things pretty quickly through the summary. Not *perfectly*, but 30 mins spent on the summary put me in a much better place to judge than being 30 mins into the book.

For many books, I stop there not because I don’t like them, but rather because* it’s enough*. I don’t  have the desire to learn more. The best ones, though, leave me intrigued, and I move to the full thing.

(of course I always read the Book Club books in full, because I committed to them, but I start with summaries even in those cases). 

So, summaries give me a good way to *stop* early, which in turn makes me *start* more books, and the bottom line is positive: I end up reading more books, full stop.

Most of all, reading summaries is time well spent *even* if when I decide to read the full thing 👇

#### 2) Mental structure 🏯

The summary acts a fantastic intro to the book itself. 

I have found that reading a book *after *I read the summary is night and day in terms of 1) retention, and 2) ease of reading. It is just easier to follow the concepts when you have a good idea of the overall structure.

This is the same reason why many people tell you to go through the table of contents first. This is good advice, too, but the TOC is very small, while the summary feels like the right size to create a mental model of the book in your brain.

---

### 📗 Reading the full book

So, if summaries are that good, why bother reading the full thing? I have found two reasons for this — the first one is obvious, the second less so.

#### Summaries are not enough

Summaries leave out many things — most of all, stories and examples.

Take No Rules Rules, for example, which I have already mentioned above. The [Shortform summary](https://www.shortform.com/summary/no-rules-rules-summary-reed-hastings) is a *great* summary — one of the best I have ever read. It explains the framework and  the main ideas clearly, and is organized even better than the book itself.

However, it misses the true *spirit *of the book. 

No Rules Rules is first and foremost a book about Netflix: it contains tens of great examples, humorous stories, practical lessons learned, which all get lost in the summary. The summary is about the *destination*, while the book is about the *journey*, which, in Netflix’s case, is 20 years in the making.

#### Books are about *exposure*

I have long complained that non-fiction books are diluted and redundant*.*

While this is largely true, it also has a flip side, that is, you get **a lot of exposure** to the book’s ideas.

Being exposed for four to five hours to some ideas is not the same as going through them in 30 minutes — even if the ideas were exactly the same.

Five hours is a sizeable time. This leaves you with those ideas *installed* in your brain, and partially running in background for a long time. The best books leave you thinking in their terms — you start drawing connections between their ideas and things that happen to you everyday. Those ideas now live in your brain *exactly* because you dedicated several hours to them.

So, good retention not only depends on *understanding* things, it also depends on seeing them over and over for a while, which is exactly what a book does to you, even (especially?) a diluted one.

#### I read physical books

So, how do I read the actual books? First of all, I usually buy and read the physical one.

This is kind of recent — I used to do most of my reading on my kindle, but over time I have found myself drawn more and more to physical ones.

It’s probably about these three things:

- 🏛️ **Library** — I like seeing my library grow and go through all the things I have read over time. It’s a small thing, but it adds to the benefits of the habit.

- 🛏️ **Night stand** — I keep the books I am reading by the bed, and read mostly before I sleep. Of course I could keep my kindle there as well, but it’s not the same thing.

- 🤏 **Tactile feeling** — I like the feeling of physical books. Also, I prefer larger ones and almost never buy pocket editions.

The main drawback of physical books is highlighting, which is 100x more cumbersome, than on the Kindle. 

While on the Kindle I can use the same Readwise workflow I use for pretty much everything else, on physical books I usually take pictures or, more recently, [highlighter tape](https://www.amazon.com/Highlighter-Tape/b?ie=UTF8&node=490669011), and transcribe later.

However, this is not *as bad* as you may think because, surprisingly, I don’t take many highlights. For two reasons:

1. I feel that highlighting takes something away from the pleasure of reading. 

1. By the time I am reading the physical book, I have already done *a lot* of highlighting on the summary.

So, I tend to highlight no more than 10 to 20 passages, which lie at the intersection of 1) super interesting stuff, and 2) stuff that is missing from the summary. It’s usually particularly good examples.

### ✍️ Writing my review

After I finish the book, if it’s a *book club* one, we have a live community call where we discuss our thoughts about it.

The conversation is driven by a few simple questions:

- *What did you like the most?*

- *What was the most surprising?*

- *What could be better for you?*

People talk about how the book relates to their experience, and the whole chat is both interesting and useful. Different people generally point at different things, and make me notice things I had overlooked.

Finally, for each book I write my own review, which doubles as a short summary, and I publish it on the newsletter.

Book reviews are among the articles I enjoy the most writing, I don’t exactly know why. I believe it’s because I grew up reading *many* of them: I read maybe hundreds of movie reviews by [Roger Ebert](https://www.rogerebert.com/), and probably just as many tech ones, for example from [John Gruber](https://daringfireball.net/) or [MG Siegler](https://spyglass.org/author/mgs/).

So, writing book reviews is another of those habits that has multiple benefits for me:

- 📬 **Newsletter** — It creates content for Refactoring.

- 💭 **Thinking** — it clears my thoughts about the book and the topics it covers.

- 🏛️ **Library** — it creates a growing library of personal reviews, similar to the one by [Nat Eliason](https://www.nateliason.com/notes), which I pretty much envy.

Would I write them anyway if I didn’t have the newsletter and the book club? Tough question but I would say *yes —* but probably these would be less deep, and include less of the summary part.

### 🌱 Extracting evergreen notes

The very final step of the process is taking the review and extracting a few *evergreen notes* from it. This is something I do with all of my writing ([I explained it here](https://refactoring.fm/i/115866613/break-down-your-content-into-small-notes)) so book reviews make no exception.

![image](../assets/18950aa666ea4c67.png)

Individual notes are incredibly useful for future articles, for writing on social media, and as a general repository of my thoughts. I have 600+ of them by now.

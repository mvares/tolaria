### 💡 Ideas

---

In any company, you would be hard pressed to find any process that impacts people’s behaviour more than performance reviews.

People may ignore the company’s mission, principles, the supposed *culture*, but never ignore how their performance is evaluated. For this reason, reviews *shape *the culture more than anything else — by rewarding good behaviour and correcting bad one.

Yet, there isn’t much consensus on how to have them. Sure, we have plenty of examples, usually from big tech, but most other companies use their own recipes, which are sometimes similar, sometimes remarkably different.

Some companies don’t have reviews at all, some run committees, some run peer-based processes, and more.

So, like we often do on Refactoring, today we will try to reason from first principles. We will figure out the goals of performance reviews, list various approaches, and evaluate upsides and downsides — so that you can judge for yourself what is the best for your team.

Here is what we will cover:

- 🎯 **Why we have reviews** — let’s not take anything for granted here.

- 🍱 **What goes into a review** — summary, feedback, and goals.

- 🙋‍♂️ **Who contributes to the review** — the role of subjects, managers, and peers.

- 🤝 **How to have the review **— a step by step process.

- 🔀 **Alternative approaches** — case studies from unusual companies like Valve, Netflix, Morning Star, and more.

Let’s dive in!

---

### 🎯 Why do we have reviews?

Reviews are *personal* conversations about growth and career. You hold them to **provide feedback** and set people on a **growth path**.

Of course, reviews aren’t the only time (hopefully! 🙈) where you touch on these topics: good feedback is continuous — it happens daily, in 1:1s, retros, and more.

Such moments, though, are mostly *tactical. *They are meant to steer the ship to catch more wind, or to avoid a storm. Reviews, instead, are *strategic*. They are when you take out the map, lay it on the table, and decide where to go.

This difference is similar to what you see in other processes, like planning. Weekly cycles and (e.g.) quarterly goals **complement each other**. If you only ran weekly sprints, you would end up doing mostly small, iterative improvements. If you only did quarterly planning without frequent check-ins, you would set goals but likely diverge from them.

So, for this reason, it’s always a good idea to have performance reviews, regardless of your team size. Small teams and startups may hold *simpler* reviews, because planning is uncertain or career frameworks are simple (or absent), but some *version* of them is always useful.

### 🍱 What goes into a review?

A good performance review includes three main things:

1. 📋 **Summary of your work** — during the last period.

1. 🔍 **Feedback about your performance **— during the last period.

1. 🎯 **Goals and priorities **— for the next period.

There are more conversations that descend from these, most notably about promotions and compensation. But they do not strictly belong to the review itself, and I also argue you should have them separately (more on that later).

Moreover, for any of these items, there are three types of people who can contribute: 1) the **subject** of the review (for *self-review*), 2) their **manager**, and 3) their **peers** (for *peer reviews*). 

These all have a crucial role. Let’s look at reviews from the perspective of a manager, and then cover self-reviews and peer reviews.

### 📋 Work Summary

The work summary includes the projects people have worked on and what they specifically did in those projects, including notes on scope, impact, collaboration, and more.

You need to get this right, as it is the common ground on which everything else is built.

You can find a lot of advice online about *how *to get this right, which mostly says to go through everything your reports have done, just in the weeks/days before the review. Now, while you definitely *need* to dig through tools and docs, I have found that if you only rely on this, you are in for a bad experience.

In fact, when you go through stuff (e.g. design docs, emails, issue tracker) that is *months *old, most often you don’t have the context for a fair judgement anymore. Not to say that it’s *a ton *of stuff*. *Multiply that for, I don’t know, 6-7 reports, and it is just unbearable.

So, as engineers, one of the things we are good at is splitting big tasks into smaller ones that are easier to handle. My preferred way of *splitting *this is by **keeping a journal** about my reports.

The journal includes my notes about what they did and how. It isn’t for *tracking* who did what — you have tools for that — it is for storing my impressions: how they acted on feedback, how they worked with others, how they handled deadlines, and more.

I use these notes in two ways:

- **1:1s** — I use my notes to prepare for 1:1s. The 1:1 agenda is mostly set by my report, but if I see that the conversation stagnates, I can route it to some items from my notes.

- **Performance reviews **— notes give me context about stuff that happened during the period, so I can more easily catch up. They act as anchors to recollect things from my memory, and complement what I can put together from tools and docs.

I don’t have a *template* for such notes: I just keep a page for each of my report and jot down quick thoughts there every once in a while, possibly with links to other stuff. Then, a few weeks before the review, I go through the notes and combine them with the rest of the materials.

These notes are private and are *not* shared with my reports, as opposed to e.g. the [1:1 notes](https://refactoring.fm/i/35216383/weather-report).

### 🔍 Feedback about performance

Performance reviews are basically a *big* moment of feedback. So, most advice about giving good feedback also stands for the feedback you give in performance reviews.

I wrote a full article about this 👇 which is also one of the most popular *ever* on Refactoring

[https://refactoring.fm/p/how-to-give-feedback-](https://refactoring.fm/p/how-to-give-feedback-)

Two key points are:

- 🪟 **Make it clear **— good feedback is specific, grounded in examples, and actionable, so that people know what to do with it. This stands true both for good and bad feedback: just like you wouldn’t say to anyone “*you are bad*”, you shouldn’t equally say “*you are doing great*”*. *What is it they are doing well? Why? 

- ⚖️ **Make it balanced **— people should receive feedback on *all* the spectrum of their behavior, which means that most feedback is usually *reinforcing*, rather than *corrective. *[Research](https://hbr.org/2013/03/the-ideal-praise-to-criticism) indicates that in the highest-performing teams, the praise to criticism ratio is >5:1, that is, people receive more than five positive comments for every negative one.

![image](../assets/ee77d7b350fd4835.jpg)

Sharing balanced feedback is especially important in performance reviews, where feedback acts as the foundation for **setting personal goals** and priorities. I see many managers setting goals mostly based on issues and personal gaps. Those are ok, but you should also *double down *on what works. What are the strengths of your report? What are they naturally inclined to?

> *We must have a clear development plan (PDI) after each performance review with clear to do's and deadlines not only for the main 3 gaps ("needs focus"), but for the 3 main strenghts as well (over-perform). As managers, we have to be careful to not focus on the gaps and forget to enhance the strenghts, which are the person's differential and differentiate them from others.

— *[*Gabriel Bechi*](https://www.linkedin.com/in/gabrielbechi/?originalSubdomain=br)*, Head of Business Ops at G4 Educação*

The goal is not to shape a perfect, all-round professional who is equally good at everything — nobody is — but to figure out the journey that is ideal for their aspirations and their impact on the business.

#### 🔄 Feedback is continuous

I believe performance reviews are mostly useful as a prime moment to *act* on feedback — by setting goals and priorities — rather than *sharing* it. 

In fact, in healthy relationships, feedback is frequent and mainly mostly shared 1) on the spot, and 2) in recurring venues like 1:1s. So, feedback in performance reviews should mostly *ratify* what has already been discussed privately.

![image](../assets/e44cea806d0243b1.png)

As a rule of thumb, if people are surprised by your review, you did something wrong.

#### 🪜 Career frameworks

Career/skill frameworks are somewhat a **prerequisite for good performance reviews**. In fact, they provide a benchmark to set expectations against, ensuring reviews are *fair* across the company because less reliant of subjective judgement.

They also provide a blueprint for setting goals, having career conversations, and they generally make a manager’s job easier by lifting a lot of what would otherwise be on them.

That said, you should have reviews even if you are a small company and you don’t have a career framework. Yes, these will look more like informal talks with managers, will be subjective, not necessarily fair — but it’s better than nothing.

If anything, *accelerate *on creating a minimum viable skill frameworks that works for you. I wrote more about this in a previous, two-series article 👇

[https://refactoring.fm/p/career-frameworks-1](https://refactoring.fm/p/career-frameworks-1) 

### 🎯 Goals and priorities

During a review, you should set a few personal goals for the next period.

I like these to be *orthogonal* from regular team activity. That is, to focus more on the *how *rather than the *what — *as the latter goes through regular planning.

Some examples are:

- **Grow people on your team** — e.g. mentor a junior co-worker on a project you both work.

- **Increase your technical impact** — e.g. own a meaningful feature from top to bottom, from design doc to release.

- **Grow your management skills** — e.g. break a large project into tasks, create and execute a roadmap for it.

These examples can all be considered part of the *how* you perform some specific work.

As a rule of thumb I like goals to be:

- 🤏 **Short** — ideally, three at most. True priorities should always be few.

- 🪁 **High level** — it’s ok to provide examples, but I like to keep goals high-level and not too prescriptive. The person should figure out how to achieve them on their own. Then, for any doubt or correction, you always have 1:1s and day-to-day feedback.

### 🙋‍♂️ Self-reviews

It is a good idea to make people write down a *review* for themselves, as opposed to only rely on the manager’s one. This is especially important for the manager to reduce the chance of missing important things that happened during the period.

Such review is shared in advance, and the manager can use it to adjust its own before the live discussion.

> *The person is asked to answer briefly to these questions:
– 3 things you do/have done well. Provide links to threads (or internal documentation) describing something you’re particularly proud of that you worked on.
– 2 areas or skills you’d like to develop further — the more specific, the better.
– 1 way that I (the manager) and the company can better support you.
– A sentence or two on what most excites you and how you want your career to develop here.

— *[*Luca Sartoni*](https://www.linkedin.com/in/lucasartoni/)*, Leadership Coach & ex Director of Engineering at Automattic*

The self-review should perfectly mirror the format and content you find in the manager’s review — to the point where many templates include with two columns for each part, to be filled out by the respective parties.

So, your self-review shouldn’t only recap what you did during the period, you should also provide feedback about yourself and state the areas/skills you believe should be your the priorities going forward.

For this reason, most of the advice I shared before for managers also stands true for self-reviews. Most notably, I suggest people to keep a *journal *about their contribution, just like managers should hold a journal about their reports. 

When this journal is about yourself, it is often called ***brag book ****—* you can find [here](https://www.semasoftware.com/blog/how-to-build-an-accomplishment-journal-brag-book) a fantastic article by my friend [Matt Van Itallie](https://www.linkedin.com/in/mvi/) about how to create a good one.

### 👯 Peer reviews

In many companies, performance reviews also include feedback from the subject’s co-workers. 

Such reviews are often called *360*, because individuals are meant to provide feedback in all directions: sideways to their peers, upwards to their manager, and downwards to their reports.

This has obvious upsides:

- **More balanced perspective** — it counters personal biases and surfaces more information than a single manager’s review.

- **Creates room for *****upwards***** feedback** — it gives people have a structured* *way to provide feedback on their managers.

- **Creates more trust** — research shows that people are more willing to trust and act on *participated *feedback, rather than on individual one.

When done well, 360 reviews are extremely powerful, to the point that some companies (e.g. Netflix, Valve) have scrapped the top-down, manager-driven process entirely, and only rely on those.

However, this is easier said than done. In my experience, good 360 reviews are tricky to pull off, for a variety of reasons:

- **Selecting the right people **— for each individual, you should select a wide pool of people who will provide feedback.

- **Creating the right culture **— people might feel uneasy at giving feedback on others. Many companies reported starting 360 reviews in anonymous fashion, and only later (and not always) switched to signed feedback.

- **Finding the time **— 360 reviews are time-intensive: they require a lot of work from everybody, and create a lot more information for managers to digest and reflect on.

My advice, however, whatever your size and stage, is to incorporate 360 feedback, even in a small fashion. 

You most likely don’t need extensive 360s that require people to comment on all the various skills of the company career framework. You can start in a simple format, like the *Stop/Start/Continue*, used by Netflix. I covered it, together with other retrospective formats in a previous article 👇

[https://refactoring.fm/p/retrospectives](https://refactoring.fm/p/retrospectives)

Also, you can start running these anonymously, and only switch to signed feedback when you feel ready.

As a great example of how to blend 360 and peer reviews into a wider review process, check out this Notion template by [Titouan Benoit](https://www.linkedin.com/in/titouanbenoit/?originalSubdomain=fr), CTO at Dotfile 👇 that he uses to run reviews.

### 🤝 Review process

So, once you have all the pieces in place, what should the review process look like? Let’s start with cadence.

#### 🕰️ Cadence

When thinking about the right cadence, the most important goal is making sure people get timely and frequent feedback. You might feel this already happens with good 1:1s, retrospectives, and good management in general. Maybe you don’t have official reviews but managers have always held career conversations and people. In this case, you can safely hold performance reviews no more than **twice a year**.

My take is that twice a year is a good end game. Once a year feels too little, and once a quarter usually feels too much.

However, if reviews are kind of new for your team instead, I suggest start with more frequent iterations to get more feedback and correct mistakes faster. Once **every quarter **is fine — you will eventually make them more sparse when the process has settled.

#### 🐾 Steps

Here is a list of the main steps:

**1) Collect material **— managers go through their notes, tools, and docs, to put together their reports contribution. If you are holding reviews every 6-months, you should start *at least *1 month in advance.

**2) Collect 360 reviews **— people write reviews about their teammates. This can run in parallel with the first step. 

**3) Collect self-reviews **— people write their own review and share it with their manager. This also runs in parallel.

**4) Write your reports’ reviews **— write a first version *before* you look at the self-review and the feedback from teammates. Hold your ground on your impressions, as they should be your own, but correct mistakes (e.g. about the work summary) that you are able to identify.

**5) Deliver in advance **— share your review with your report ~1 day in advance. Not too much as people are usually eager to discuss this, but not too little so people have the time 1) to reflect, and 2) to cool off first reactions. 

**6) Discuss it in person **— hold a meeting (or a call) to discuss the review face to face. Explain all the points, ask how they feel about the review and answer any question they may have. At the end, also ask for feedback about what could make the review better. At this point you shouldn’t revise your review unless there have been some glaring mistakes. Make people understand this is *your* review — they may feel bad about it (they but shouldn’t if you shared frequent feedback during the period) but this is the impression they gave you, so let’s just build on it and figure out how to grow together.

> *Reports write their part and managers write their part (not always later but we try to not look at each others parts before the review). Manager and report take one dedicated hour to review everything together.

— Titouan Benoit, CTO at Dotfile*

**7) Compensation **— talk about compensation in a separate, following meeting. Talking about money makes people less receptive of the actual review, which is instead the most important thing.

This process is no one-size-fits-all. Tailor it to your own team based on what feels best to you.

### 🔀 Alternative approaches

To give you more food for thoughts, here are alternative takes by companies that run performance management in pretty unusual ways 👇

#### 🍿 Netflix — peer-based

Netflix approach to HR and performance has generated a lot of discussion in the industry. Reed Hastings explained it in [No Rules Rules](https://www.goodreads.com/en/book/show/49099937) (which is a fantastic book by the way), and has been since covered many times.

At Netflix, performance reviews are 100% driven by your colleagues. They give you input about what works well and what doesnt. More in particular, they adopt the stop/start/continue template.

360 reviews started anonymously, then switched to signed, and today they even have them face to face to promote a culture of full transparency.

There exist more companies that work this way, but very rarely of this size. This is what makes Netflix’s approach *radical*.

#### 🏥 Alan — no managers

[Alan](https://alan.com/) provides modern healthcare products to businesses. It is a 500-people team that employs more than 80 engineers, and works with ~20,000 companies.

It does so without managers.

Alan is very aware that running a tech team is about more than just writing code. So they identified three main managerial responsibilities:

- 🔨 **Technical Leadership** — guiding design choices and overall technical decisions

- 📂 **Project Management** — planning work, communicating with stakeholders, removing obstacles.

- 🌱 **People Management** — nurturing people’s growth and wellbeing. Taking care of hiring and careers.

These responsibilities are *deconstructed* and the duties distributed across several roles. When it comes to performance management, here is what they do:

- Reviews are distributed and peer-based.

- Compensation for all employees is public across the company and determined by level.

- Promotions are determined by reviews + special promotion committees, assembled temporarily.

You can find more info about how Alan runs engineering on their [medium blog posts](https://medium.com/alan/how-our-engineering-teams-run-without-managers-5deef596d2f7).

#### 🕹️ Valve — stack ranking

Valve is one of the biggest names in gaming. In 2012, they published the [handbook](https://cdn.cloudflare.steamstatic.com/apps/valve/Valve_NewEmployeeHandbook.pdf) that is given to new employees about how the company works. At the beginning it says: **welcome to Flatland**.

When it comes to reviews, peers give each other feedback about their work about once a year. A set of people interviews everyone in the company to figure out who each person has worked with, and identifies the right set of peers for everybody.

Feedback is anonymized, delivered to reviewees, and used as a basis for the *stack ranking, *which adjusts each person’s compensation based on who’s providing the most value at the company.

Groups are asked to rank their own members based on four metrics:

- **Technical Ability** — How difficult and valuable are the kinds of problems you solve? Are you uniquely capable of solving a certain class of problems?

- **Productivity / Output** — How much shippable, valuable, finished work did you get done?

- **Group Contribution** — How much did you contribute to hiring, integrating people into the team, improving workflow, amplifying your colleagues, or creating tools used by others?

- **Product Contribution** — How much do you contribute at a larger scope than your core skill? How much of your work matters to the product?

Once the group ranking is done, the information gets pooled to be company-wide.

#### 🍅 Morning Star — CLOUs

Morning Star is the world’s largest tomato processor, handling between 25% and 30% of the tomatoes processed each year in the United States, and employing more than 3000 people.

Every employee at Morning Star creates a personal **mission statement** about how they will contribute to the company’s goal.

To implement their mission, each employee creates a yearly personal *contract* with their peers, called **Colleague Letter of Understanding** (CLOU).

At the end of the year, employees receive feedback from their CLOU colleagues and defend their performance over the previous 12 months. Each team member justifies their use of the company’s resources, acknowledges missteps, and presents plans for improvement.

Special **compensation comittees** are elected to validate self-assessments and uncover contributions that went unreported.

At the end, the committees set individual compensation levels, ensuring that pay aligns with value added.

2024 is closing, and will go down as a tough year for engineers.

Combined with the equally challenging 2023, in the last two years we dealt with the end of ZIRP, layoffs, the economic downturn, up to the latest series of returns to office.

Of course, this was also the year of the full-scale explosion of AI. And let’s be honest: for engineers, AI so far is a mixed bag. 

On one side, there is the *promise* of more productivity. New tools are good and the impact is real. On the other side, though, there is the *expectation* of more productivity, which is often overblown and cast onto engineering teams from clueless leadership.

I feel that all these things, together, make it harder than ever for engineers and managers to navigate their own career. How should you think about your skills? How do you stay relevant?

We wrote a lot about this in the last few months, from the [evolving role of engineering managers](https://refactoring.fm/p/how-engineering-management-is-changing?utm_source=publication-search), to the [rise of product engineers](https://refactoring.fm/p/how-to-become-a-product-engineer), to my own [predictions about AI ](https://refactoring.fm/p/predictions-about-ai)just a few weeks ago. 

Today, we will put everything together in a guide to **help you design a great professional journey**. 

So here is the agenda:

- 🎯 **Skills vs impact** — how to figure out if you are on the right track

- 🎨 **Generalists vs specialists** — getting broad vs getting deep

- 💼 **Startups vs Big Tech **— should you trust the clichés?

- 🔀 **Changing Jobs **— should you stay or should you go

- 🎽 **IC vs EM **— how to navigate the big crossroads

- 🍀 **The Four P’s** — a useful framework to think about your career 

- 🤖 **How to use AI for good **— to super charge your career, instead of hampering it

And more! Let’s dive in! 👇

### 🎯 Skills vs Impact

What do you *need* to grow your career?

Because of the way schools and universities work, we are conditioned to focus on **skills**. We *study *things: this or that language, the latest framework, system design, kubernetes, etc.

Skills matter, but they are a very small part of why people get promoted and have successful careers.

By and large, people get promoted by displaying impact. This is widely acknowledged for managers, but it is the same for individual contributors, too. Your tech skills are only useful as long as they create value for the business.

I strongly believe this is the healthiest way to think at your skills, that is, almost not thinking at them… at all! If your track record is a collection of home runs, nobody cares that you know this or that language, or, at least, good companies don’t. 

This rings especially true today, as AI provides engineers with a kind of "universal basic proficiency" across various tech. While some hard skills may become more commoditized in the future, the ability to create real value will always remain vital.

So, focus on doing great work. That’s it.

Displaying impact makes people trust you, which expands your *influence*. In turn, influence and trust enable you to take on bigger responsibilities, which enable even higher impact, in a virtuous, self-reinforcing cycle.

![image](../assets/12fbdf02815c8074.png)

Chasing impact is the healthiest mental model for your career. For two main reasons:

- **It challenges your comfort zone **— higher impact usually means more ambitious initiatives. As long as you go after bigger things, it is less likely to stagnate.

- **It treats your skills as a means to an end **— skills are meant to increase your leverage. As long as you frame them this way, you will likely make the right calls about what you want to learn. E.g. you will likely embrace AI (to chase higher leverage) instead of being afraid of it.

So, it’s great to wanting to improve some skills, but always ask yourself: how does this help to create more value? That is, what skills enable impact? Let’s talk about autonomy, ownership, and the infamous T shape 👇

### 🎨 Generalists vs specialists

Maybe this is a bias from my time as a founder, but I strongly believe in chasing ample **autonomy** in any job you have. To me, high autonomy creates **ownership**, which in turn amplifies impact.

I have found that *autonomy *is enabled by two factors:

- **Adequate skills **— e.g. as a developer, being proficient on the whole stack so that you can own features from top to bottom.

- **Healthy work environment **— a working process where people are given the opportunity to own meaningful tasks.

So, I am a believer in chasing *full-stack *experiences, especially early in your career. When I say *full-stack,* it’s not just about the tech — it’s about doing *more:*

- Making meaningful decisions about the product

- Talking with customers

- Being on-call for your code

- Collaborating with designers

- …

Going *wide* early in your career has strong benefits:

- 📈 **Efficiency **— for most skills, Pareto applies: you can go from 0 to 80 in just a few years, and then it takes 10+ years to get from 80 to 100. So it is a better deal to spend some initial time to build a few 80s, before committing to 100%-ing one.

- ➡️ **Direction **— getting in touch with different things helps you figure out what you really enjoy and want to get better at. You develop a more balanced perspective and avoid bias 👇

- 🔑 **No lock-in **— when you specialize too early in something, you risk to get cornered into a *local optimum* that is difficult to escape. You have all your eggs in one basket, which also makes you less resilient to changes in the industry.

> *I got stuck for a long time as a PHP contract developer because the market was so hot and in the UK the tax treatment very favourable for contract vs permanent, but I always knew I wanted to get back into management, having led a team at a startup back in 2011-12. But it was never financially worthwhile! It was only Brexit + BoJo's November election + IR35 changes that killed the contract market dead long enough I felt I had to get a perm job. I'm not sure what the lesson is here; I was definitely stagnating in my last couple of dev jobs and it really turned out to be a boon to be forced to change.

— *[Kendrick Curtis](https://uk.linkedin.com/in/kendrickcurtis) — VP of Technology at Codacy

So, if to you this seems like an ode to generalists, it kind of is, especially in your early years. 

To build your own T *shape*, it is more effective to go after the horizontal leg *before* the vertical one, which it also informs you on *which* vertical leg to build later.

So what jobs should you chase for this?

### 💼 Startups vs Big tech

There is common advice that says:

- **Go with startups** — if you want to have more responsibility and a generalist experience.

- **Go with big tech** — if you want to specialize into something and earn more.

There is some truth in this but it is hard to generalize — especially because neither startups nor big tech are cohesive categories that always behave the same way.

So, when people ask me for advice about choosing jobs, I tell them to stay open and judge more based on the conversations they have during interviews, with people from their future team, rather than the overall company *reputation*.

Things vary a lot, also within the same company. There are lean teams inside big orgs that own high-growth products from top to bottom, operating like a startup, and then there are convoluted, bureaucratic experiences with long chains of commands in order to release any small change.

Also, not all startups make for good experiences. Some are messy, excellence is nowhere to be found, and the only thing you will learn is how to burn out.

So, my advice is to spend considerable time *talking* with your prospective employers, and asking a ton of questions:

- *How does the team work? Tell me about more the product dev process*

- *What are some ambitious projects you are working on?*

- *Are you raising money? What are the plans for the company?*

- *Is the company growing? Are you hiring for other roles?*

Think like an investor in the company, because you kind of are. You are investing your time in it and you are betting that it will be good for your growth and career.

### 🔀 Changing jobs

If your early years are for broad *learning*, then you will probably benefit from **changing jobs often**.

This might look controversial, but it is just common sense: if you want to **see different things**—from a technical, product, and organizational point of view—you just need different companies.

How long you should stay depends on how much you can learn. A couple of years are usually a good amount of time to have good impact and learn some new tricks. In healthy, high-growth contexts, you can benefit from staying more, while you can stay less when things are not going the right way.

As a hard and fast rule, I would always stay at least 18 months in any position that is not completely pointless or toxic. Less than that, the onboarding tax you pay is just too high and cuts into the value you can create for the company and for yourself. Also, a long string of shorts experiences doesn’t make for a good impression with future employers.

More advice on when to quit your job 👇

[https://refactoring.fm/p/quitting?utm_source=publication-search](https://refactoring.fm/p/quitting?utm_source=publication-search)

Later in your career, when you get into senior positions, I would look instead for environments where you can stay longer and have a long term impact. Leadership and upper management positions, in fact:

- ⏳ **Need more time to create value** — initiatives are bigger, planning cycles are longer, and you just need a longer period of time to accomplish anything.

- 🤝 **Are built on strong trust** — every time you change jobs you lose all the trust you built in your previous experience, which is crucial to advance to leadership roles. In that respect, changing jobs too often can even *slow down* your career. Many companies do not even hire for e.g. VP or Director roles, because it’s too hard to vet candidates at that level. They hire for a level below and possibly promote the candidate fast.

### 🚪 Being in the right room

So, growing your career is a matter of always putting yourself in the *right room*.

There is a famous saying that goes: *if you are the smartest person in the room, you are in the wrong room. *So you can go a bit farther and ask yourself: what does the *ideal *room look like? Who should be there?

My answer is:

1. Someone you deeply admire for their **skills** and from whom you can **learn from**.

1. Someone who is receptive to your needs, sponsors your work and **gives you the opportunity** to shine.

1. Peers you **get along well** and with a diverse set of skills with respect to yours.

As long as your work has all three, you are set!

### 🍀 The Four P’s

I am a fan of the Four P’s Framework, by [Thiago Ghisi](https://www.linkedin.com/in/thiagoghisi/), for growing your career either in management or technical tracks.

The four P’s are the areas you should look at to positively impact the team/company you are working in: 

1. 🧱 **Platform **— it is the hard tech. The codebase, infra, CI/CD, and engineering practices. You should master those and build your contribution on top of them.

1. 📱 **Product **— it is the application from the user perspective. Their experience and what they are able to accomplish. Focusing on the product means delivering value to the final users.

1. 🔁 **Process **— it is the *how*. It is the culture, rituals, systems, and metrics. You impact these by optimizing and *strenghtening *the process.

1. 👥 **People **— this is about *nurturing *your team and your relationships via mentorship (giving advice), coaching (supporting personal development), and sponsorship (giving opportunities).

While the most successful engineers and managers excel at all four, you should probably focus on them at different times:

- ⬆️ **At lower levels** — *Platform* and *Product*** **are the primary drivers for growth and promotions. You want to look for impact on specific areas of the product and of the codebase.

- ⬇️ **At higher levels** — *Process* and *People*** **is where you should focus your attention. In fact, your role becomes a *multiplier* whose impact is primarily measured in how well you *enable* others to do their best work.

### ⚖️ Learning more about yourself

Everyone has **strengths and weaknesses**, things they like and don’t like.

Sometimes, especially early in your career, it is hard to separate those from what you are just more or less expert in. But over time, if you pay attention, you will just know what are the things you can *improve*, but will never be your *forte*, and those for which you are more of a natural.

I will tell you a couple of mine:

- I tend to be a *people-pleaser* and I am not comfortable with *conflict* — I can handle it, I can receive and deliver tough feedback, I am ok with hard conversations, but these all cost me a lot energy. So, I don’t really shine in environments where the culture is extremely direct, or people are challenged too often.

- I am good at picking up new things and owning something from top to bottom — I have been a founder for 10+ years so it’s just how I am wired. I am not scared by things I don’t know and if you give me something to do, I will just find a way to do it. So I thrive in flexible environments where I am not *pidgeon-holed* into one narrow function, and I can be creative.

When you play to your *strengths*, your energy is multiplied, and so is your progress.

> *Accepting promotions without being aware of this can land you in a position where you’re doing more of something you aren’t good at, or something you don’t enjoy.*

### 🗺️ Making a plan

Just like with your skills, your initial years are best used to figure out what these strengths and weaknesses are, so you can later double down on the former.

And *doubling down* includes making a plan about where you want to go.

I know that some people are particularly *allergic* to making plans for their lives. The answer I can give, based on what I learned about myself, is that the actual *plan *might turn out to be crap sometimes (but you get better over time), but planning is almost always useful.

At the beginning, it is useful as an act of introspection about what you want to achieve. And at the end, it is useful for a retrospective of what went right or wrong. By doing these two things regularly, you are naturally going to achieve more in your life.

I maintain a *triple* plan for myself, that looks more or less like this:

#### 1) **5-years plan** 

This should be a rough sketch of what my ideal life might be in 5 years, across all the axes: work, family, health, relationships, personal stuff, etc. I tend to be optimistic about this, because 5 years is a lot of time and we often underestimate what we can achieve over a long time.

I also learned about the [*Odissey Planning*](https://medium.com/@belinda.rowse/odyssey-planning-re-framing-the-five-year-plan-7a0948707615) method, which I try to incorporate 👇

> *Odissey Plan template:*

#### 2) **1-year plan**

This is a more practical plan that advances the 5-years one in a meaningful way. I write down a few main themes (2-3 max) and both some qualitative and quantitative goals.

I describe the *what* and almost never the *how.*

#### 3) 3-months plan

This is like personal OKRs. Three to five main things to accomplish for the current quarter. I started doing them in 2020. 

If you think this is way too much, consider that the *depth* you want to go with it it’s entirely up to you. You can write down a couple of sentences for each level of the plan, of go full-blown with goals, KPIs, etc.

There is no right or wrong, it depends what fuels your motivation vs anxiety

### 🎽 IC vs EM

A pivotal moment in many people’s career is switching to a management role, or deciding to stay an individual contributor. There is often a lot of anxiety associated to this, as the difference between the roles is huge, and people feel there is no going back.

I spoke with a lot of people, especially on the podcast, about the transition, and here is the best advice I collected:

1. **Prototype the switch** — by taking on some EM duties as an IC. This is usually possible in some fashion: talk with your EM about the best way, how can you help them? In most companies, people become EM after having displayed some managerial behavior as an IC. You can explicitly go after it to figure out if 1) you are good at it, and 2) you like it.

1. **Design an off-ramp **— becoming an EM is not a promotion, is a full career change, so it’s entirely possible you will figure out it’s not for you. You can discuss with your manager setting a *probation period* into the role (e.g. 6 months) which you can easily revert to your IC role if things don’t go as expected.

1. **Consider the pendulum **— managers benefit from understanding technology, and engineers from understanding management. Both things change over time, and I believe it can healthy to swing between IC and management roles over the years. Charity Majors explained this way better than I ever could in this [awesome article](https://charity.wtf/2017/05/11/the-engineer-manager-pendulum/). If you want to get this change, look for healthy environments in which managers and ICs are effectively peers. In any case, do not consider the transition a one-way street: it is not, and I think we will see more and more hybrid roles in the future.

### 🤖 How to use AI for good

I can’t write a respectable career guide without addressing AI.

As a software engineer, there is exactly one correct way to approach AI, which is: letting go of your ego and embracing it full-scale.

Be the AI-guy at work. Stay on top of new tools and models, try them, teach others at work, give presentations to your team.

AI is going to change how we write software forever — the thing is: we still don’t know *how*. I believe there is a fantastic window of opportunity for engineers who adapt to (and discover) new workflows, and it’s a no-brainer to try to be one of them.

So the question might be: how to stay on top of everything that comes out? Honestly, it is easier than it seems. On my side, I just send myself every morning a daily digest ([via Mailbrew](https://mailbrew.com/)) with the top page of hacker news + top page of product hunt. Plus, I scroll X ~30 mins a day. That’s enough to be ahead of 99% of people.

### 📌 Bottom line

So, here are the main takeaways from today:

1. 💪 **Focus on impact over skills** — your career growth is driven more by the value you create than by the technologies you know. Use your skills as a means to an end, not an end in themselves.

1. 🌟 **Embrace the T-shape** — start broad (full-stack experiences) early in your career, then specialize. This approach provides efficiency, direction, and flexibility in your professional journey.

1. 🔄 **Be open to change** — don't limit yourself to startup vs. big tech stereotypes. Judge potential jobs based on team dynamics and growth opportunities, and be willing to change roles every few years early in your career.

1. 📊 **Leverage the Four P's** — focus on Platform and Product at lower levels, then shift to Process and People as you advance. This framework helps guide your impact at different career stages.

1. 🤖 **Embrace AI proactively** — position yourself as an AI advocate in your workplace. Stay current with new tools and models to ride the wave of change in software development.

One of the most frequent questions I get from readers is a *meta *question — that is, how I study, do research, and prepare for articles.

I have written [other times](https://refactoring.fm/p/how-i-write-and-grow-refactoring) about this, but never in full detail. So, I am now publishing a *massive*, two-part series on my whole writing and organization process, including templates and workflows that you can start using right away.

But before we dive into this, I want to answer two questions:

1. Why this is interesting / useful to you

1. Why you should listen to me

These are the core questions I ask myself before I start writing any article, and, for this one, I feel I need to answer them explicitly.

The first thing I want to say is: I am not special.

Sure, I have a decent experience with startups and software teams, but I was never the CTO of a billion dollar corp, I never worked in big tech, nor raised money from a16z. Yet, Refactoring today is among the top 10 biggest engineering newsletters out there, with almost 60K subscribers, 1400 paid members, and 170+ original articles.

I am proud of all these numbers — in particular the 170 articles.

There is no way I could pull this off from my experience alone. Sure, I could probably *coast* the first 20-30 articles, but then what made a real difference, over the distance, is *process*.

As of today, I am very happy with my organization and note taking. This is not only about Refactoring: I use the same systems for *running* my life in general, which is why I believe this can be useful to you. Whether you are a newsletter writer, an engineer, or whatever, the right workflow can supercharge your personal growth.

So here is what we will cover today

- ☑️ **Notes == getting things done **— a practical approach on taking notes.

- 🪣 **Capturing notes **— how to optimize for quick saving.

- 🏷️ **Organizing notes **— how I use projects, responsibilities, and topics.

- 🌱 **Evergreen notes **— a special kind of notes that forms the backbone of my writing.

As anticipated this is the first of a two-part series. This one will focus on process, while the next one will include my full workspace template + automations so you can get started with zero effort!

Let’s dive in!

### Notes are about getting things done

I have always liked to take notes, and I have always thought I was good at it.

When I studied computer science at university, few courses relied on actual books — professors mostly used presentations they created themselves, which means taking notes was crucial.

During those years, I worked a lot on taking good notes.

I organized notebooks religiously and I made experiments to bridge the gap between physical and digital, including using the unbelievable (back then, but kind of still today) [Livescribe smart pen](https://us.livescribe.com/collections/smartpens/products/echo-2%E2%84%A2-smartpen).

In hindsight, what *fueled* this process was that it was attached to a real purpose. I could *measure* how good I was doing by means of how easy it was to study on my notes, plus what grades I would get at the end.

Why am I telling this story? Because I believe the #1 reason why people fail at [personal knowledge management](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Personal_knowledge_management) is that they don’t have a real purpose for it. Taking notes and organizing them is expensive — if you don’t have a good reason for it, chances are you will eventually just give up.

So, one the core themes of this article is that notes are, primarily, an aid to get things done, as opposed to, I don’t know, a repository of your knowledge for some abstract future use.

Fast forward today, my whole work *entirely* depends on notes. My note taking has three precise goals:

- 🧵 **Publishing one original, long-form essay** — every Thursday

- 💡 **Publishing three short-form interesting ideas** — every Monday

- 🐦 **Publishing one good idea on social media** — every day

So let’s look at what I do for this.

### 🪣 Capturing Notes

I have found that useful stuff can come from literally anywhere: a website you visit, a bit of wisdom from a friend, a sign on the street — so you should have a reliable way to capture *any* kind of source.

This *capture* stage should be optimized for minimal friction. You should be able to capture anything super fast, otherwise you just won’t do it.

Also the capture stage should only focus on exactly that: *capturing*. Don’t bother about organizing anything at this stage, or figuring out where to put things. This only makes the capture more cumbersome, and makes you do less of it.

In my experience, separating *capture* from *organize* is a key element of good note taking. Some people corner themselves into unsustainable workflows where they e.g. take a picture to something and need immediately to send it to e.g. Notion, put the right tags, etc.

This doesn’t work in the long run — you end up taking 1/10 of the pictures.

So, whenever you capture something, only capture it, and review it later.

To being able to capture things fast you need several methods, each optimized for a different type of source / input.

Here are mine:

- 🖥️ **Websites** — [Notion Web Clipper](https://chrome.google.com/webstore/detail/save-to-notion/ldmmifpegigmeammaeckplhnjbbpccmm). I use this unofficial Notion saver to send any interesting website to my Notes database. The chrome extension allows to create various templates for different types of websites, and is able to fill database fields automatically. 

- 💭 **Casual thought** — I create a new note on [Bear](https://bear.app/), on my iPhone/Mac. Bear is super fast, is offline first, and has good sync across devices. Those are my only requirements, so Bear works fine but I would probably do just as well with Apple Notes and other similar apps.

- ✏️ **Article passage** — I highlight the passage on [Readwise](https://readwise.io/read), which automatically creates a note on Notion for that article with all the highligthed parts.

- 📗 **Book passage** — I usually read on a Kindle and highlight passages there. These are also sent to Notion automatically by Readwise.

- 🎧 **Video / Audio passage** — I use Bear, transcribing the passage manually — I don’t like this, it is slow and cumbersome, which is one of the reasons why I don’t listen to many podcasts.

- 📷 **Misc life stuff** — I take photos with my iPhone.

![image](../assets/ffa48f0066514bf2.png)

I also make a heavy use of AI-generated summaries. For articles and videos I am not sure I want to consume in full, I go straight to the summary first (I wrote about this [here](https://refactoring.fm/i/131615721/research-studying)) and decide based on that. About 50% of the times, the summary is enough and I simply copy parts of it into a new note.

### 🏷️ Organizing notes

The organize part is where you take all the mess that you captured and figure out what you should do with it.

I do ~80% of my organization in batches — in particular, I have 1h 30m (three pomodoros! 🍅) blocked every Monday after lunch, where I organize my notes and plan my tasks for the full week.

This *review* covers three main areas, which roughly take 30 mins each:

1. ✏️ **Notes** — I organize all items I captured during the previous week that are still “unassigned”.

1. 💬 **Comms** — I go through email and DMs everywhere. I also reply to messages everyday, of course, but I find useful to do a full pass once a week to make sure nothing falls through the cracks (something always does).

1. ☑️ **Work** — I review what I did the previous week and plan what to do for the next. This is the last part of the ceremony and for a good reason: notes and communication often inform what to do next.

But what does it mean to *organize* notes, in practice? 

To me, it means figuring out what I should do with them, to answer the question: *why am I saving this?* Or should I, really? When I organize stuff, I end up trashing ~50% of the things I captured the previous week.

I found that one of the side effects of decoupling — also temporally — the capture from the organization, is that many things that *seemed* important on the spot, become very less so after just a few days, so you can safely remove them.

But *how *should you organize things? Where do you put them?

In my workspace, I have three kinds of buckets: **Projects**, **Responsibilities**, and **Topics**.

#### 1) 🔨 **Projects** 

Projects are things I want to achieve on a defined timeframe. They have a beginning and an end, and are bigger than a single task (I can’t complete them in one sit). 

![My projects for this quarter.](../assets/227f0ab4e1d441c8.png)

I plan most projects on a quarter basis, and that includes both Refactoring projects and life projects, e.g. like the recent relocation we did, or our honeymoon.

#### 2) 🔄 **Responsibilities** 

Responsibilities are activities that I do on a regular basis, for which I need to maintain a standard. E.g. writing on social media, or running interviews with guests. 

![image](../assets/fbd12938a0714d1b.png)

The defining feature of a responsibility is that it is recurring: it doesn’t have an end. So, while projects require *plans *or roadmaps, responsibilities require *procedures* about how to perform them.

I usually have a recurring task on Todoist for each responsibility, linking back to the procedure on Notion.

![image](../assets/da3c078c1c41476b.png)

#### 3) 🏷️ **Topics**

Are areas of interest. A topic is anything that can be useful in the future. Examples of topics are “frontend”, “no code”, or “sim racing”.

![A small sample of notes I tagged with “No Code”. They are mostly tools and come from the web clipper.](../assets/4fb0e16714354373.png)

- Picture

The whole organization phase is about taking notes that are *unassigned* and attaching them to at least one of these buckets. Each note may belong to *either* a project or a responsibility—rarely both—and can have topics attached.

EXAMPLE

For doing this, I use Notion. Notion can model the projects/responsibilities/topic organization through database relationships, which is flexible and powerful in many ways. Its ability of using views, formulas, and rollups, to me, is simply unmatched.

The main drawback of Notion, if you ask me, is that it is *slow*, and a bit *cumbersome* on mobile. This would be an issue if I used it for capturing stuff, but I don’t, so all good!

To me, for organization, flexibility > speed.

### 🌱 Evergreen notes

A special kind of notes are *evergreen notes*. Evergreen notes are small notes each about a single, atomic idea. I call them evergreen because they should be *timeless*: each of these ideas should stand the test of time, so that the notes form a growing body of knowledge.

![A snapshot of some recent evergreen notes. The “related to” tags are topics](../assets/9fcc4779b8eb469d.png)

Why do I take these notes, and how?

I am a content creator and I create various types of content: from long-form essays, to individual tweets. Having a repository of all my ideas, possibly organized by topics, allows me to mix and match these ideas and create new content with ease.

There are two main ways evergreen notes get created:

- 📖 **From my readings** — during the organization phase, I may turn some highlights into new evergreen notes.

- ✏️ **From my articles** — after I publish an article, I go through it and split it into, typically, 4-5 new evergreen notes.

As of today I have collected more than 500 of such notes. The main way I use them is for social media: every week I sit down one afternoon and plan social media content. Each tweet and linkedin post is typically an evergreen note, adjusted for the medium.

![Example of a recent evergreen note. It has a topic and is linked to two articles: the one where it originally appeared (How to Build a Company around Crafters) and a recent Monday edition.](../assets/37f5d77f9908455c.png)

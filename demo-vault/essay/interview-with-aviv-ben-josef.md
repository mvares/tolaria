### **1) 🎯 Journey into Tech & Consulting (**[**02:04**](https://youtu.be/mw44fyx-HiY&t=124s)**)**

Aviv's journey in tech began at age nine when his parents enrolled him in a Pascal programming course. What started as a hobby evolved into a deep passion for making computers execute his ideas.

After military service at 18, where he worked with "crazy, brilliant people," Aviv's career took him through various roles including IBM and several startups. His transition to consulting happened organically:

> *"When I did coding, we worked with dozens of companies here in Israel... I noticed that the founders got so much value just from chatting and seeing what I was seeing across different companies."*

This observation led Aviv to transition from freelance coding to "*CTO as a service*," and eventually to executive coaching, where he helps good leaders become better.

### **2) 💰 Profitable Engineering (**[**11:37**](https://youtu.be/mw44fyx-HiY&t=697s)**)**

Aviv emphasizes that engineering teams must be profitable for their companies - a concept many startups overlooked until recently. He identifies the key aspects of profitable engineering:

- 📈 ***Slow***** growth** — engineering team size should *not* grow linearly with business growth; instead, it should grow in strategic jumps aligned with new products or expansion.

- 🎯 **Focus on value** — engineers should understand business impact rather than just focusing on technical excellence.

- 💡 **Strategic investment** — avoid investing disproportionate engineering resources in small contracts or features that won't pay off

> *"We need to transition from being precious snowflakes, who like old magicians used to say things and no one understood the words and just accepted it. That no longer is enough. We now have to be real parts of the company."*

### **3) 👥 From Tech Manager to Business Executive (**[**27:26**](https://youtu.be/mw44fyx-HiY&t=1646s)**)**

Aviv developed its own framework for engineering leaders to transform their organizations, starting with their own growth into true business executives. 

This transition requires **developing business acumen** and building strong peer relationships across departments - skills that, while different from technical abilities, are entirely learnable.

> *"If someone would come to you and say 'now we have AI and LLMs,' virtually everyone now understands LLMs to a degree because we had to... If we can do that with things as complex as that, why couldn't we do it with running a company?"*

The second crucial element is **establishing cultural alignment** within the team. This means shifting from a purely technical focus to understanding what makes a healthy tech organization and measuring success through business impact. Aviv emphasizes that while technical excellence remains important, it should serve business goals rather than exist in isolation.

The third component is what Aviv calls **product mastery** — ensuring engineers deeply understand their product and users.

### **4) 📊 Measuring Engineering Impact (**[**38:32**](https://youtu.be/mw44fyx-HiY&t=2312s)**)**

Aviv introduces the concept of "*meta retrospectives*" — quarterly reviews that go beyond typical engineering metrics to evaluate real business impact. These sessions involve both engineering and product teams, focusing on:

- 🔍 **Feature usage** — Analyzing which features are actually being used and to what extent

- 🗣️ **User feedback** — Incorporating direct user feedback, potentially using AI to analyze customer calls and support tickets

- 💼 **Business results** — Connecting engineering efforts to tangible business outcomes

> *"I literally asked 100 or 200 CTOs, VPs of engineering... 'How do you tell yourself, how do you measure? Did we do good?' 80% said we delivered the roadmap. Okay, which I say that's like, you know, was the restaurant good? I went and they gave me my order. Wow, right."*

Aviv stresses that the goal isn't just to ship features or maintain clean code, but to deliver value that users appreciate, even if the product isn't perfect. He cites Twitter's early "*fail whale*" as an example of a product that succeeded despite technical issues because it provided significant value to users.

### **5) 🤖 AI's Impact on Engineering Teams (**[**53:06**](https://youtu.be/mw44fyx-HiY&t=3186s)**)**

Aviv found there are three main ways engineering teams use AI today:

- 🛠️ **Product integration** — incorporating AI directly into their products for enhanced functionality

- 💭 **Dev support** — using AI as an intelligent rubber duck for debugging and ideation

- ⚡ **Internal tooling** — creating AI-powered tools to boost team productivity and support other departments

> *"If you look at it, most people, if they really lean in, you get some very nice velocity bumps, but not with all the tasks you have. But there are certain things that are now just demonstrably much faster."*

Aviv believes that while AI won't replace engineers anytime soon, it can significantly enhance productivity when teams approach it with the right mindset. Success with AI tools like GitHub Copilot comes from being willing to work with "*80% there*" solutions and refining them, rather than expecting perfect code generation.

The future of engineering teams, according to Aviv, will reward generalists who can connect different parts of the organization and innovate, while AI handles more routine specialized tasks. This shift emphasizes the importance of understanding the broader business context and being able to work across traditional boundaries:

> *"AI is not going to be innovative for a while... But if you want some innovation, you have to have those people who can connect different parts of the organization. So that's where I think we should all aim to be."*

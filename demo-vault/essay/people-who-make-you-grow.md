People naturally help each other during their career. Sometimes we help others, sometimes we are the ones who get helped.

The best of such relationships propel each other forward at high speed. We can all think of someone who changed our life through their advice and support. 

These people also benefit from doing so.

Helping others is rewarding by itself and makes for a strong growth experience. It might also be a core part of your professional life: as a manager, you are there to support your reports and you need to understand what’s the way to be the most effective.

This article explores the nature of such relationships and how we can create successful ones in our lives.

We will discuss how to have the most impact based on what the learning objective is, how you can nurture them inside your company, and how to look for them outside of it.

The goal of this article is to make ourselves more intentional and effective about getting help from more experienced people and giving to those who may benefit from it, to accelerate both their growth and our own.

We will cover:

- 3️⃣ The main types of growth relationships: **Sponsors**, **Mentors**, and **Coaches**

- 💬 How to figure out what is the **right approach **for your situation

- 🏢 How to develop such relationships **inside** your company

- 🏡 How to develop such relationships **outside** your company

Let’s dive in 👇

### 3️⃣ The three types of growth relationships 

For any pursuit in your professional (and personal) life, you need to figure out **what** to do and **how** to do it.

You can classify how people can help you based on what kind of impact they have on your goals.

![image](../assets/d4149b151e9249a2.png)

#### 📣 Coaches

Coaches are people who help you figure out what the problem really is. 

They ask you questions and force introspection. They do not provide specific solutions, but rather challenge and encourage you to find your own.

Coaching helps you with the “**what**” at a fundamental level, when you still have no clue.

#### 📚 Mentors

Mentors are people who have gone through similar experiences as yours, and can **give you** **advice**.

Mentors might say:

- *When I was in your situation, I…*

- *You should…*

- *Have you tried…*

Mentors help you with the **how**, when you have already figured out the **what**.

Mentoring can be the relationship between two colleagues in which the more experienced uses their knowledge to support the development of the less experienced.

While coaching helps the individual find their own solution, mentoring is **directive** and provides specific advice.

#### 🌱 Sponsors

Sponsors are those people who **do** practical things to help you. 

They might advocate for your promotion, approve budget for your project, or intro you to useful people. 

While mentoring is about giving advice, sponsoring is about empowering and **giving opportunities**.

### 💬 What is the right approach?

Each of these relationships might or might not be beneficial to you, based on the stage you are in and what you need help with. 

Also, different people can be useful for different things. For example:

- Coaches do not necessarily need similar experiences as yours to be effective. 

- Good mentors can’t always be sponsors, too. That is, they aren’t always in the position to help you directly.

The best approach is often a blend of all, it depends on circumstances and can even change within the same relationship.

I do some professional coaching and one of the hardest parts is understanding what is the best way to help my *learners*.

- When they have an issue where my experience closely overlaps theirs, I can step in as a **mentor**, suggesting practical ways to move forward.

- When it isn’t so, though, I step back and act as a **coach**: make them reflect on the core issues, support their reasoning, and make them find the best solution.

The latter is harder, because as human beings we are more wired to act as mentors. Give advice. Tell what to do. Coaching instead requires listening to the other person, embracing their challenges and helping with introspection.

Paradoxically, the more experience I get, the less often I feel entitled to act as a mentor and give specific advice. Life and work are full of complex scenarios — what worked for me might not work for others. Also, direct advice tends to take away some ownership from the people who receive it, which is not what you generally want.

### 🏢 Relationships inside your company

In a team, people help each other all the times (or they should!). So when and how are these relationships relevant? How can you put such knowledge to work?

Here are three main examples:

- 🎒 **Onboarding**

- 💪 **Training of junior engineers**

- 🎖️ **Manager / report relationship**

#### 🎒 Onboarding

The onboarding process is a great opportunity to pair new hires with **mentors** who provide advice about how the company works.

This is often called [buddy system](https://hbr.org/2019/06/every-new-employee-needs-an-onboarding-buddy) and is applied with success in many companies big and small. A buddy also helps new hires understand social dynamics like lunches, internal memes, and all that stuff that doesn't come with documentation. You can keep a list of people who wish to become mentors, and choose from that every time.

I wrote a high-level article about onboarding [here](https://refactoring.fm/p/the-three-levels-of-onboarding-) and (spoiler) I will write a new, more specific one next week!

#### 💪 Training of junior engineers

Assigning official **mentors** to junior engineers is a win-win solution to: 

1. Supercharge the growth of the latter.

1. Provide a growth opportunity to former — mentoring makes you better at working with people and also increases your understanding of the topics you teach.

Great mentorship is a memorable experience, both for the mentor and the mentee. It makes them happier, other than more productive. The benefits vastly outweigh the time you take away from dev work. 

[**Andrea Antonioni**](https://twitter.com/_aantonioni) talked with me about his experience in [**Subito.it**](https://subito.it/):

> *When I joined *[*Subito.it*](https://subito.it/)* back in 2017, I was a Junior iOS Engineer with very little working experience. [...] I spent my first 3 months working side-by-side with a Senior Engineer. He gave me a full list of software engineering books to read, like if I was back to school. We spent a lot of time pairing and doing white-boarding sessions together. I learned how to think like an engineer, how to manage tech debt and write clean code. Today, when someone asks me to mentor them, I share the same list of books I received from him.*

Here are a couple of **action items** you may take from this:

- As a manager, ask yourself: how am I enabling the growth of junior engineers?

- As an mentee: ask a senior co-worker if they would like to mentor me with regular calls

#### 🎖️ Manager / Report

The best managers I know alternatively act as sponsors, mentors, and coaches, based on what is needed the most:

- As a **sponsor **— you can help your report get recognition and opportunities. E.g. you can suggest them as a good lead for a new project, or to give a talk about something they are expert about.

- As a **mentor **— you can be directive and give advice to make your report perform better.

- As a **coach** — you work with your report to figure out what their goals are, what makes them happy, and what is the direction they would like to go with their career. 

Finding a balance between the various modes is critical.

- Managers who are **too directive** tend to take ownership away from their reports. This is often the case with first-time managers, who tend to remain pretty much *hands-on*, as an extension of their IC role.

- Manager who are too *coachy* and **hands-off** risk to make their reports hit a wall every now and then. Removing obstacles is a big part of a manager’s work, other than helping people identify where such obstacles are.

- Sponsorship is generally underrated. For example, studies have shown that women and people from minorities are [over-mentored, but under-sponsored](https://hbr.org/2010/08/women-are-over-mentored-but-un).

While these interactions happen on a regular basis between a manager and their reports, there is nothing that prevents you to sponsor / mentor / coach someone who *isn’t* your report. 

As a *learner*, if there is someone within the company that you look up to, don’t be afraid to **ask them directly** if they want to have a call / meeting every once in a while to share advice. Most people are happy to help!

Andrea shared another great example from his experience:

> *I was lucky enough to have a Scrum Master in my team with +20 years of experience in this field. When I met her for the first time, I told her I didn't know what a sprint was and that I didn't have a clear idea of what a Product Owner is. We started having casual coffee breaks about agile best practices. Today, one of my strongest skills is the ability to drive a team to a process improvement.*

### 🏡 Relationships outside your company

If you are not able to develop such relationships within your company, you can look for them outside.

Here are a few places you might look at:

- **Your network **— this is obvious and should be your first bet. Think at people in your network you would be happy to get mentored by, and reach out to them. You may ask directly if it’s ok for them to have periodic calls, to keep each other updated and get their advice. You will be surprised by how often people are happy to do that.

- **Communities **— engaging in communities is a great way to get “distributed” mentoring and get to know new peers you could build relationships with. This is exactly what we are going for with the [**Refactoring community**](https://refactoring.fm/p/community). If you haven’t already, you should [fill the survey](https://airtable.com/shrb2I6TW2QyDqtEZ) and apply to join! Other related communities are: [**Engineering Managers**](https://engmanagers.github.io/), [**Rand's Slack**](https://randsinrepose.com/welcome-to-rands-leadership-slack/), and [**Dev Interrupted**](http://devinterrupted.com/).** **

- **Networking apps **— apps such as [**Shapr**](https://shapr.co/) and [**Lunchclub**](https://lunchclub.com/)** **help you meet professionals who are relevant for your career. They create serendipitous encounters and work pretty well.

- **Professional coaching **—  there exist platforms where you can get professional coaching and mentoring. I do some coaching myself on [**BestPracticer**](https://bestpracticer.com/), which is very good. Other options include [**CTO Craft**](https://ctocraft.com/cto-coaching/)** **and private mentors (more in the resources section).

[**Titouan Benoit**](https://twitter.com/titouan_benoit),** **from the community, shared his experience with some of these options:

> *I found interesting people on Shapr to discuss about my project. Actually I was looking for a Business CoFounder and found some advisor. But there is a specific category to find mentors in Shapr.

For Linkedin, it is really difficult and the success rate to have an answer is very low. No magic trick, try to explain what you are looking for (mentorship) and you think he/she can help you. Sometimes you can hope to have an answer. I answered to some on Linkedin (from others founders who wants advices).*

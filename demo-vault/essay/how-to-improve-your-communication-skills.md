A few months ago I ran a wide [survey about engineering careers](https://refactoring.fm/p/state-of-engineering-careers).

The survey provided many insights, one of which is that people believe non-technical skills are crucial to advance their career — even more so than technical ones like coding or system design.

![image](../assets/c82e47a6abaa4f0c.png)

In fact, 3 out of the top 4 skills people are looking forward to improve are non-technical.

Now, while I have covered management many times, I never really wrote about how to improve your own communication skills.

Over time I collected *a ton *of notes and advice on communication. I did so by reflecting on what helped me during my career, and by speaking with some truly excellent *communicators*.

This week I took all of this and created an article that covers the two angles that are the most relevant to me:

- 🏗️ **Structure **— how to structure information to be delivered in an effective way. I will cover proven frameworks and mental models that can help you with that.

- ❤️ **Empathy **— how to focus on what needs to be heard, rather than what needs to be said. How to communicate in a way that is trustworthy and non-violent.

I will also include practical advice about situations you may encounter at work, and plenty of additional resources to learn more.

Let’s dive in!

---

### ⚖️ Structure and Empathy

Communication is such a wide topic that I have, of course, no pretense of being exhaustive. My angle, that splits between **content** and **empathy** is not the only possible one, but to me it is one of the most useful.

In fact, over my career I have routinely met engineers whose growth and impact were limited by one of these two factors.

Several years ago I worked with a brilliant DevOps engineer who had great tech chops and was capable of breaking down complex ideas perfectly to a non-technical audience. 

However, he was also rather *disagreeable*.

He took criticism personally and mostly cared about being *right*. Over time, his perfectly good ideas were received with growing skepticism, and people avoided working with him. We eventually parted ways, and I suspect he never truly understood why.

Likewise, I know empathetic, trustworthy people capable of doing great work, but, under the best intentions, not being effective at promoting it with PMs or executives. The perception about their impact is let down by a *language barrier* that none of the parties is able to overcome.

Let’s try to fix both scenarios, starting with the latter 👇

### 🗣️ The Language Barrier

Building products in tech requires many specialized skills and roles. Engineers are radically different from designers, which, in turn, are different from PMs and Executives.

These people just talk [different languages](https://randsinrepose.com/archives/managementese/) — and they do because they ultimately care about different stuff.

So, the language clash is most often a *goals* clash.

### 🎯 Understanding the *What*

In many situations, at work, we communicate with somebody to obtain something. We may want a peer to approve our design, an executive to give buy-in on a project, or a PM to green light some estimates.

In these situations, we often focus on *our own* agenda, while it is helpful to reflect on what the other party wants to achieve. In many cases, their agenda is different, or do not care about the same stuff than us.

> *It’s hugely helpful to first think about the key points your audience needs to hear and understand, and then spin your narrative around that. Natural human tendency, unfortunately, is quite the opposite: We start thinking about what we want to tell.

— *[*Corvin Deboeser*](https://www.linkedin.com/in/deboeser/en/?originalSubdomain=de)*, Senior Data Scientist*

So, we should strive to deliver information in a way that helps them with their goals, rather than only focusing on our own. Let’s make a couple of examples: 

#### 1) Example 1  — Design trade-offs

As an engineer, you are presenting the trade-offs between two designs to a PM. You want to build the best technical solution possible, which also repays some debt from the past. 

The PM, on the contrary, cares more about velocity and about shipping the feature as soon as possible to customers.

A good delivery of your proposal may take that into account and highlight the practical ways in which the better design helps your velocity down the line.

So, do your homework and be specific. Here are three ways of presenting the same thing, with increasing effectiveness:

- 🟠 **Okay** — this is a better design that will probably take a couple of days more, but it will repay some technical debt.

- 🟡 **Better** — this is a better design that will probably take a couple of days more, but it will allow us to cut the time we spend on maintaining this other thing.

- 🟢 **Best** — this is a better design that will probably take a couple of days more, but it will allow us to cut the time we spend on maintaining this other thing. Last quarter only we spent 5 days doing that totally useless work, and it’s only going to get worse over time.

#### 2) Example 2 — Executive buy-in

As an EM you are presenting a piece of roadmap to an executive, to get buy-in. You present reasonable but rough estimates. Many requirements are not clear yet — you want to make sure you have enough time to build things right, and that you don’t create false expectations by committing on tight timelines for doing nebulous stuff.

As an executive, you want to make sure engineers do not spend too much time in a vacuum to deliver something that is unproven and might eventually fail. You need them to start that other big project a couple of quarters from now, so you want something to be delivered within six months, so you can recalibrate allocations after that.

As the EM, you can tailor your presentation to counter these issues:

- 🟠 **Okay **— look, many things are still foggy — we can reasonably complete this within six months.

- 🟡 **Better** — look, many things are still foggy — *this* is the core subset of the design we know best today and we think we can deliver in about 4 months. So we will have 2 other months for: 1) other parts that we can clarify in the meantime, or 2) as a buffer for the first part in case something goes wrong, or 3) to improve on what we delivered after we get some feedback.

- 🟢 **Best** — look, many things are still foggy — *this* is the core part of the design we know best as of today and we think we can deliver in about 4 months. So we will have 2 other months for: 1) other parts that we can clarify in the meantime, or 2) as a buffer for the first part in case something goes wrong. In any case, we will optimize to ship working software weekly and have a checkpoint with stakeholders every two weeks. So, if we discover we are going in the wrong direction, we can sit together again and steer the ship timely.

### Nailing the *How*

These examples work because they speak to your interlocutor’s goals, but are, admittedly, a bit simplified. With complex/nuanced topics it is not easy to deliver your point of view with clarity, even if you know what all the parties want to achieve.

One of the most influential works about structuring effective communication is [The Minto Pyramid Principle](https://www.goodreads.com/book/show/33206.The_Minto_Pyramid_Principle), by Barbara Minto. 

In her book, she introduces the **SCQA framework** for presenting ideas in a way that captures the recipient’s attention and increases the chances they understand them. Each idea should be organized into four sections:

1. **Situation** — the context, or the status quo. E.g. *we need to deliver the product in six months.*

1. **Complication **— a change or a problem in the current situation which makes this communication necessary. E.g. *current requirements are unclear, so we are not sure about what we can deliver in six months.*

1. **Question** — the main problem statement. E.g. *we want to agree with you on the process we should adopt to minimize risks and deliver the best possible solution over these six months.*

1. **Answer **— your best answer to the posed question, or the process to put in place to get such answer. E.g. *we define a core subset of the design to be delivered in 4 months, while keeping 2 months of buffer for learnings, improvements, and setbacks. We will ship working software every week and keep stakeholders posted so we can steer the development fast if we realize we are going in the wrong direction.*

At this point, you typically need to support your proposed answer with arguments and ideas. Minto proposes to apply *The Pyramid Principle* to do it effectively 👇

### 🔺 The Pyramid Principle

The principle advocates that ideas in writing should always form a *pyramid* under a single thought. In this case, such thought is the answer you propose.

Below that, you should summarize and group the next level of supporting arguments. Then, for each argument, you may break it further into more ideas, to form a pyramid.

The Pyramid Principle is based on three key concepts:

1. **Start with the answer first** — most people think better in a top-down fashion. Start with the answer and present supporting ideas later.  

1. **Summarize and group supporting arguments **— force yourself to limit the number of arguments you propose, by grouping them and focusing on the main ones. Minto suggests the [Rule of Three](https://medium.com/lessons-from-mckinsey/the-rule-of-3-c1cd82dbc96e).

1. **Logically order your supporting ideas **— make sure that the ideas you bring together under each group actually belong together, are at the same level of importance, and follow some logical structure.

### ❤️ Being right vs being trustworthy

Communication is not just about content. 

We are humans, and whenever we talk to each other we have a big mess going on with our egos, insecurity, need for appreciation, and more.

I love [Elizabeth Spelke](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elizabeth_Spelke)’s quote on this (thank you [Corvin](https://www.linkedin.com/in/deboeser/en/?originalSubdomain=de) for reporting it 🙏):

> *All too often, especially when we are passionate about something, we get into a conversations of differing opinions. We naturally get defensive of our own position, and for good reasons at first. However, I’d dare say most of us ended up at least once defending something “just to defend it” - and this is perfectly normal human nature. We want to save face, ego, pride etc.

However, this can get in the way if you are truly interested in finding the right answers. When arguing in any scenario, circle back to this quote frequently and ask yourself: Is this still the right path, or am I just defending something because it was “mine”.*

This reminded me of another great quote, in [Ray Dailo’s Principles](https://www.goodreads.com/book/show/34536488-principles) (one of my favourite books), in which he argues that we need to **replace the joy of being right, with the joy of finding the truth**.

I believe this is good advice for life in general, but especially for communication at work.

What I have seen, in fact, is that being right is only useful to some extent. What other people truly want from us is to be **trustworthy***:* 

- A trustworthy person *accepts feedback* as is, and reflects on it even when they disagree.

- A trustworthy person values the final result more than their own contribution.

- A thrustworthy person speaks with empathy and cares about what needs to be heard, other than what needs to be said.

In other words, skilled people may be right *often*, but trustworthy people are right *eventually — *because their mindset puts them on a growth trajectory 👇

### 🔪 Non-violent Communication

The last topic I want to cover is [non-violent communication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nonviolent_Communication). 

This is not—as many believe—a technique to limit or end disagreements, but rather to make those more peaceful.

> *Disagree without being disagreeable* — [Chiamaka Nwolisa](https://www.linkedin.com/in/chiamakanwolisa/?originalSubdomain=de), Senior Software Engineer

This has been invaluable to me in the past, especially because I don’t have a confrontational personality, and I am not at ease with conflict. So, expecting peaceful disagreements is crucial to me to be able to speak my mind fully, especially in situations where I have to push back on something, or deliver a tough feedback.

Non-violent communication focuses on creating empathy between the parties, based on four principles:

- 🔍 **Observation **— we should first present facts—what we have seen, or heard—without attaching any judgement or evaluation of meaning. When we combine observation with judgement, other people may feel attacked and resist what we are saying, so it’s important to separate those, to build common ground on facts first.

- ❤️ **Feelings** — this is like an emotional audit of yourself. You should describe the sensations you are feeling in relation to the observations above. For example: “*when this happens, it makes me feel like…”. *Stating your feelings, rather than your judgement, makes you connect more with others. That’s because 1) feelings don’t feel like personal attacks, 2) it’s hard for others to refute them, because they are our own.

- 🤗 **Needs** — you can usually correlate your feelings to some personal needs that have been / or not have been met. Examples are appreciation, support, ownership, impact, or purpose. So the pattern becomes something like “*when X happens, I feel like Y, because I need Z*” 

- 🙏 **Requests**: once you've expressed your feelings and needs, you may ask for something that can improve the situation. Requests are different from demands because it's important to respect the other person's answer, even if it's a "no". That doesn't mean, however, that you should give up. Instead, take some time to understand why, before deciding how to continue the conversation.

#### Example 

To make this more practical let’s make a simple example.

Your boss is an EM who has retained some strong technical views, and tends to micromanage you how on you should build features. They routinely give you prescriptive advice on technical designs, but they often miss context about the codebase, or you simply disagree with them. This leads to unpleasant conversations.

To address this directly you might be tempted to say something along these lines: 

> *Hey, thank you for this design. However, to be honest I feel like this is a bit prescriptive with respect to my role. I would like to take care of this myself, as part of my contribution. So, what if, we start from a high level brief of what we need to achieve, then I create a draft design and we review it together? what do you think?*

This is straightforward and not bad by any means. It would work fine with many managers I know. However, it may also backfire in a few ways:

- You get to a conversation about what is or is not prescriptive, where your opinions differ.

- Your manager suspects you simply don’t trust their tech chops and feel attacked.

- You proposed a final *design review* as an easy way out, but this ends up just *shifting* the problem to that moment.

A non-violent, and more effective way of addressing this might be:

> *Hey, thank you for drafting this. To be honest, though, when you create such designs in advance (****observation****), I feel unsure about my role (****feeling****). To me, it’s important to feel ownership of this kind of work, as part of my impact on the project (****need****). Would you be ok with sharing even detailed requirements about what we need to achieve, but then letting me try my hand with the design? (****request****)*

This formulation focuses on your feelings and needs, and creates more empathy. It doesn’t feel like an attack in any way, and is more likely to get a positive outcome.

You might think this is exaggerated, and it may be true, based on the situation or the person you are talking to. There are people you can afford to be more direct with, because you know they are fine with this, and there is also everyday stuff where a simple request might go.

But there are also people with stronger egos, or tough situations that require more tact.

As always, this is not a silver bullet. It’s important that you internalize *principles*, and then implement them each time based on your own sensibility.

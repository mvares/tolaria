Last week I had a fantastic chat with Jean Hsu, who is VP of Engineering at [Range](https://range.co/), and writer of the popular newsletter [Tech and Tea](https://jeanhsu.substack.com/).

Jean has been an obvious inspiration to my work, for a long time. Her focus on improving how people work in tech shows in everything she does:

- She builds [a product](https://range.co/) that improves the way teams communicate and work together.

- She writes [a newsletter](https://jeanhsu.substack.com/) about — in her own words — leadership, teamwork, parenting, partnership, and creative self-expression.

- She runs [leadership training](https://coleadership.com/) for engineers, and has [interviewed](https://www.youtube.com/playlist?list=PLufm2ljcdWj6jai-qbfvu0imEh2adMGJL) some of the most successful leaders in tech.

Our conversation naturally gravitated towards those topics. Today’s article is a summary of our conversation, plus more ideas and resources to learn more. Here is what we will cover:

### ⚖️ Hardcore vs Calm work

[Link to the video chapter](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=khlPwJkjf1A&t=85s) 📽️

In [an article](https://refactoring.fm/p/is-elon-right) from a few months ago, I argued that there is an increasingly big divide in tech about two types of culture:

1. 🏃‍♂️ **Hardcore culture** — Move fast, break things, revolutionary, intensity, all in the same room, synchronous, speed of results.

1. 🧘‍♂️ **Calm culture** — Iterate, small increments, evolutionary, focus on quality, distributed, asynchronous, long-term thinking.

People are increasingly polarized and each group tends to look at the other as evil.

- Hustlers see calm people as lazy/ineffective.

- Calm folks see hustlers as toxic.

The debate is far from settled, with a lot of big tech recently pushing return-to-office policies. Now, of course, working in an office doesn’t necessarily equate to *hustling* or *hardcore*, but there surely is some correlation between the two.

Jean argues that a lot of this *swinging* is a reflection of the *leverage* engineers have.

In fact, remote work is very obviously a *wellbeing* theme. For a lot of time, companies have tried hard to make engineers happy, with flexibility being a big perk that has a real impact on people’s lives. The downturn made such leverage shift, and the pendulum is swinging back.

So, this big divide might boil down to the divide between leaders who think **happy engineers == productive engineers**, and those who think **happy engineers == lazy engineers**.

Executives, especially in US, are also biased. In big hubs, like San Francisco, it is super expensive to live close to where offices are, and executives are the ones who can afford that. Also, *in-person* is how companies have been run in the past decades, so that’s what most leaders know best.

[Link to the video chapter](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=khlPwJkjf1A&t=243s) 📽️

So, in the end, the recent push for return-to-office might be more about a mix of familiarity and misaligned incentives, rather than productivity.

But both Jean and I think that the genie is out of the bottle and remote is here to stay. If anything, remote **solves hiring **in a big way, by simultaneously increasing the pool of candidates, and reducing the labor cost. Engineering talent is spread out, and there will always be companies who are willing to meet talent where they are.

Also, remote work is only going to get better over time. Tools get better, and, crucially, people get better too. There is a generational element to it: as more and more *remote-native* engineers grow into management and leadership roles, the push for remote-friendly policies will also increase.

#### 🏋️ But does hardcore work?

After we spoke about remote, the conversation between Jean and I naturally gravitated towards *hardcore* work and hustling — the both of us coming from intense startup experiences.

And here is the thing: personally, I have fond memories of those days. The *long* days, and weekends, spent at the office working with the team on a crazy goal. It wasn’t just the work — it was the beers, the laughs, and just the incredible energy.

But was I getting *more* done because of this? *Maybe *— I lean towards a *yes*,* *but I wouldn’t really bet on it.

I believe a lot of this working style is also related to how young engineers (or founders btw) naturally work: there is a lot of **throwing *****spaghetti**** *🍝 at the wall to see what sticks, rather than intentional, thoughtful building, and this is more a byproduct of their (lacking) skills, rather than a *strategy.*

More experienced people might not want to grind like that anymore, and maybe they *don’t need to*, because **they know how to do stuff**. Of course, those people are also more likely to have families and more personal duties, so such *intensity* gets out of the question.

In the end, it’s ok if you want to work nights and weekends — I *wanted* to do that — but another thing is the expectation that everyone does that. When you throw it upon the whole team, aside from the big question about productivity, you going to **hurt diversity** and you will have a hard time hiring and **retaining senior folks**.

#### 🦸 Heroics culture, rewarding the invisible, and WWI

Another peril of *hardcore* culture is that it lends itself to rewarding *heroics* — which is pretty often **anti-engineering**.

When you praise *hustling*, you may end up rewarding the guy who puts out the fires, rather than the one who prevents them. Instead, the best engineering work is most often **nothing glamorous** or heroic. It is invisible, and even boring.

This reminds me of the famous WWI story about [planes and survivorship bias](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Survivorship_bias). During the war, the US military analyzed planes that were coming back from engagements, to figure out where they would need the most armor. For a good while, they focused on the areas where, statistically, planes got hit the most, and reinforced those.

Abraham Wald, a hungarian mathematician who had fled to the US to escape the Nazis, and was working with the military, pointed out that those numbers only considered planes that *came back *— despite getting hit. Instead, engineers should focus on areas that apparently *didn’t get hit* much, because planes that got hit there probably crashed.

![If the aircraft is only reinforced in the most commonly hit areas, this is survivorship bias because data from crashed planes is ignored; in fact, those hit in other places did not survive.](../assets/bd996ddb8ab64876.png)

So, just like with the planes, what’s most visible ≠ what’s most valuable.

### 🏃‍♂️ Productivity in **engineering**

[Link to the video chapter](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=khlPwJkjf1A&t=422s) 📽️

How do these factors — remote vs office, balanced vs long hours — impact productivity and overall work output? Well, it’s complicated.

Our first observation is that, in engineering and knowledge work in general, productivity only *loosely* correlates to how many hours you work. Productivity is more about how you feel, how much you sleep, how clear the strategy is, and other intangibles.

In the real world, **10x engineers are those who are well-rested and motivated**, rather than in a depleted state.

So, as managers, we strive to keep people motivated, but we should equally care for their energy and general wellbeing. Jean tells me that she often encourages engineers she works with to **take time off**, especially parents. These people have a lot of stuff going on — how are you going to do great work if you do not take care of these first?

And she has to *encourage* them, because many people are not comfortable taking vacations when there is a lot on their plate. It’s on managers to 1) build processes that let people take time off safely, and 2) create a healthy culture where people are comfortable doing it.

### 💻 Remote and productivity

[Link to the video chapter](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=khlPwJkjf1A&t=655s) 📽️

In our experience, remote work has different impact on different people.

For parents, remote work can be extremely good in terms of productivity and pure work output. You can pick kids up and get back online later, or squeeze some hours in the evening when you have a setback during the day. There are plenty of situations where, working in an office, you would need to take a day off, while remotely you can just rearrange your day.

There are also things that remote makes *worse* out of the box, and you need to be more intentional about, like:

- 🆘 **Asking for help **— remotely, it feels like there is a higher barrier to ping someone and ask for anything. This is especially damaging to new hires and young engineers, who need more help and may get stuck otherwise.

- 🤗 **Building rapport** — in the office you get a lot of things *for free* that let you build familiarity with co-workers. You may meet someone in the kitchen and chat, even though they work in marketing and you work in engineering. Or you may just get up and have a coffee with your buddy.

In my experience, you need to put in more work to recreate all of this remotely, but if you do so, things turn out just fine — or even better and more inclusive than they ever were.

### 🧠** **Hustling and mindset

[Link to the video chapter](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=khlPwJkjf1A&t=923s) 📽️

In the second half of our chat we talked about *personal* values and work ethics.

In fact, I feel there are two kinds of *hustling*: one that comes from your company culture, and one that is *intrinsic *to your values and to how you feel you should work.

Separating the two is not easy. When I was a founder, I worked long hours but I was convinced that I *had* to do that for the survival of my startup, not because I enjoyed that. And when I started doing Refactoring full-time, I thought I would finally work *less* and enjoy a more balanced lifestyle.* *

But surprise! I still work just as much as I worked at my startup. Maybe more!

This time I have no excuses: Refactoring is profitable and, if I stuck with the basics — i.e. the weekly writing — I could probably run it by working *part-time. *But I don’t want to! I want to do more: so I set goals, run the community, develop the library, talk with sponsors, run events, and so on.

The truth is, I feel slightly uneasy when I am not doing something *productive*. This is common for many people in tech, who have a lot of their *identity* tied to the idea of building and achieving something.

I would call them *achievers*,* *but not in the sense that they *already* achieved something—not like *over-achievers—*but in the sense that they *perennially* want to achieve things.

Maybe they haven’t so far, or maybe they have and they got to the point where they thought they would feel fulfilled and… they don’t.

For these people, there is a lot of unlearning to do, but I am not sure how.

Jean tells me that, for her, having kids shifted her perspective completely:

- **On a practical level** — some things are just not sustainable anymore. Weekends become even more tiring than the work week, so if you keep working like you did before, you just get depleted and burned out.

- **On a mindset level** — family and kids make you more easily *decouple* your definition of personal success from financial outcome. 

I can’t speak for this because I don’t have kids yet, so for me what kind of works is to think that I am not in total control of outcomes. Luck and external factors always play a big role, so I learned to be happy with the journey and the effort I put, rather than the destination — what I actually achieve.

### The Hierarchy of Spaces

[Link to the video chapter](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=khlPwJkjf1A&t=1748s) 📽️

Speaking of productivity vs carving out space for yourself, Jean told me about her personal experience, which she also covered in a [two-part series](https://jeanhsu.substack.com/p/space-to-do-whatever) on her newsletter.

She remembers of a time, years ago, when she felt extremely exhausted and didn’t want to engage in creative things anymore. She always thought of herself as a creative person, so she wondered: *maybe I am not creative anymore*?

Over time she realized that nothing had changed — her need for creativity and self-expression would still emerge, but there were other *needs* and *spaces* that needed to be fulfilled before she could go to the next level.

So she came up with a *hierarchy of spaces*, similar to Maslow’s hierarchy of needs.

1. **Space to not be exhausted **— this is about your basic needs, like good sleep and eating well. Even basic needs can be challenging with young kids and demanding work, but if you don’t take care of those, you can’t move up to the next level.

1. **Space to do whatever** — this is about giving yourself the permission of spending some *unproductive* time. Like watching Netflix, or doing absolutely nothing.

1. **Space to enjoy** — only once you have some spaciousness to do whatever, you can engage in activities you like and enjoy doing. These might be games, hobbies, and anything that makes you happy.

1. **Space to be creative** — this is about activities that let you express yourself in a way that is uniquely *you*. Jean believes that true creativity can only emerge once you have satisfied your other spaces. 

Personally, I have found that the lower you are in this hierarchy, the harder it is to rationalize the need for such space. E.g. it is easier for me to justify time spent on activities I genuinely enjoy — e.g. sim racing, or playing guitar — rather than e.g. watching Netflix, which looks more like *space to do whatever*.

But even with stuff I do for pleasure I sometimes have the temptation of finding a *productive* angle, like setting targets or goals.

One time, about a year ago, I set a quarterly goal of improving my ranking on [iRacing](https://iracing.com/), kind of like a personal OKR, with precise targets and everything. Fortunately, after a while I looked at it and thought “*what am I doing?!*” and scrapped it.

#### 🔨 Engineering space to do whatever

[Link to the video chapter](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=khlPwJkjf1A&t=2413s) 📽️

While speaking, we found that such hierarchy of spaces is also relevant at work. 

There are many examples of companies explicitly carving out *unproductive* space for engineers. Like the famous 20% time at Google, or [Hack weeks at Dropbox](https://refactoring.fm/i/123257681/hack-weeks-bug-bashes), that we discussed with Andrew two weeks ago.

Based on the situations, these are either *space to do whatever*, or *space to enjoy*, or something in between. These spaces are great for people to decompress — and a lot of great ideas and projects came out of them anyway!

In general, leaving some slack at work is about treating people like humans, trusting that by taking care of their basic needs they will be able to do an overall better work.

Jean also wrote about this in a [recent article](https://jeanhsu.substack.com/p/space-to-do-whatever-at-work).

### ✍️ Post

A couple of months ago we run a comprehensive survey about the state of engineering careers.

We surveyed hundreds of developers, managers and tech leaders on topics like their favorite trends, professional growth, feelings about their current job, and more.

[https://refactoring.fm/p/state-of-engineering-careers](https://refactoring.fm/p/state-of-engineering-careers)

At some point in the survey we asked a simple question: **are you thinking about changing jobs?**

![image](../assets/db9ba45b2bd64c1a.png)

Replies are telling.

Only 27% of respondents replied that they plan to stay at their current company for the foreseeable future, while ~50% were either in the process of moving or might move in the next 6 months.

In March 2021, in the aftermath of the first year of COVID, Microsoft famously released an [in-depth survey about hybrid work](https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/worklab/work-trend-index/hybrid-work#:~:text=Employees%20are%20at%20an%20inflection%20point). They found that 41% of employees were considering leaving their current employer that year — almost *double* the figure reported in previous years.

This event was dubbed ***the great resignation***** **and, if anything, it only seems to get worse.

[Another poll](https://www.randstad.co.uk/about-us/industry-insight/great-resignation/) run by Randstad later that year found that 69% of UK workers were ready to move to a new job in the following two months.

In the tech space, it also doesn’t help that most news people hear these days is about layoffs.

**Why do people quit their job? And when should you do so?**

In the past couple of weeks we had a thorough conversation about this on the [Refactoring community](https://refactoring.substack.com/p/community). People, from veterans to freshmen, shared their personal stories, mental models, and everything that helped them decide to jump ship.

It turns out the reasons why people quit are few and well known. What is hard, instead, is recognizing them. Picking up the clues and acting on them.

That’s because, for the vast majority of people, quitting is hard — it is the most stressful time of their career 👇

![image](../assets/5395ca4795b54994.png)

People don’t change jobs because they like to — they do because they find abundant, impossible-to-ignore evidence that they should.

This article explores this topic from two main angles:

- 🚪 **Why you should quit** — the main reasons you should leave your job.

- 🔍 **Picking up the clues** — the signs you should pay attention to.

The goal is to equip you with ideas and mental models that can help you make the best decision when the time comes.

In the article you will also find many contributions from the Refactoring community. This is a very personal topic and many people decided to share their stories in the community in the past couple of weeks.

![image](../assets/a79c79ec73d04b05.png)

I did my best to include the best contributions and do them justice.

Let’s go! 👇

### 🚪 Why you should quit

There are three main reasons people leave their jobs:

There is a long tail of other reasons, too, but they add up to a small share with respect to the main ones.

Let’s see each of these categories.

### 🌱 Growth

The tech space moves at crazy speed, and people know it. 

Growing your skills and staying up to date isn’t only the right move for your career — it is a big source of motivation, too.

**Mastery**, defined as the desire to improve our craft, is one of the pillars of engagement at work:

[Embed](https://twitter.com/lucaronin/status/1579743622424977408)

Conversely, when your learning stalls, chances are you will also get progressively less and less engaged, in a vicious cycle that handicaps your growth.

How do you know that your learning is stalling? Here are a few signs:

#### 1) 📅 This year looks suspiciously similar to last year

Instead of thinking about your skills, which might be a bit of an abstract exercise, just think about what you have done last year, compared to this one. How did you spend your time?

Sometimes, five years of experience is [just the same year](https://cate.blog/2021/11/29/5-signs-its-time-to-quit-your-job/#:~:text=Sometimes%20five%20years%20of%20experience%20is%20just%E2%80%A6%20the%20same%20year%20of%20experience%2C%20five%20times%20over)… five times over.

#### 2) 🩺 You can predict your emotional state

This is a great tip by [Aadil](https://twitter.com/aadilmaan):

> *Has my learning stalled? How do you know this: you know precisely what time of the calendar year you will experience what kind of emotion. At Apple, by my 5th year, I knew when in the release cycle I was going to have extreme anxiety, slow work, sudden ramp up etc.*

#### 3) 🩹 You are learning coping mechanisms rather than skills

This is taken again from this [fantastic article](https://cate.blog/2021/11/29/5-signs-its-time-to-quit-your-job/) by Cate.

All companies have quirks, sacred cows, and politics you may need to work around. Which is fine to some extent.

Just ask yourself how much effort you are spending on those*. *How much of what you do (tech, processes, practices) makes you proud? How much of that would be good and reusable in another job? 

### 💰 Money

Financial opportunity is one of the biggest drivers for change, and yet it is a tricky subject, for two main reasons:

1. 🔍 **Reading the market** — it is not trivial to figure out how much you are worth on the market and what opportunities do exist out there.

1. ⚖️ **Balancing with the rest** — a large pay check is sometimes used to compensate for other undesirable qualities, like your wellbeing (e.g. working long hours), or personal growth (e.g. writing COBOL for a bank).

How to handle these problems?

#### 1) 🔍 Reading the Market

There exist tools and services to help you keep a pulse on the market. [Levels.fyi](https://www.levels.fyi/) is probably the most famous.

On Levels you can find benchmarks of compensations for various roles and companies, and you can also book additional services, like [resume review](https://www.levels.fyi/services/resume/?from=home_page_below_nav), [career coaching](https://www.levels.fyi/services/coaching), and [salary negotiation](https://www.levels.fyi/services/) to help you navigate everything related to changing jobs.

I haven’t used the additional services myself but I know a few people who did and were very happy with it. If you are thinking at a big career move — it’s probably money well spent.

Another strong advice I often received is: **keep interviewing**. Some of the best engineers and managers I know do interviews regularly. This has several benefits:

- **You stay up to date** — with what’s needed of your role.

- **You get comfortable with interviews** — which is very important to reduce the friction of changing jobs.

- **You develop your network** — by meeting people in other companies you like.

Of course, there is something to be said about the ethics of accepting interviews when there is zero chance you will change jobs. But if there is even a slight one — I would say go for it. It’s a learning experience and in the end it’s up to the other company to sell you on the opportunity.

#### 2) ⚖️ Balancing money… and everything else

This is a very personal subject — the way you rank money against other stuff largely depends on your values and personal situation.

When it comes to myself, I always try to think long-term. I suspect this will never change — right now I think long-term for myself; when I am older I will probably do so for my children, and so on.

I do so because I believe in two fundamental things:

- 📈 **Compounding** — Incredible results are most often not the result of extraordinary *burst* performance, but rather the result of *adequate* performance sustained for an *uncommonly long* period of time. This stands true for everything: personal finance, health, relationships, you name it.

- 🐠 **Swimming against the current** — In modern society, long-term thinking seems both increasingly uncommon and increasingly hard. So, people who are able to do so consistently should have an edge.

That said, I know what you may be thinking now:

- “*Luca is saying I have to work my a** off and sacrifice work-life balance, at least early in my career*”

- “*Luca is saying I need to prioritize learning over money*”

This is what people most often associate with *long-term*.

So, actually, no.

Long-term applies to everything. By working long hours you may be sacrificing your relationships, or your health, for which you also want to think long-term.

So, in the end, money is just one piece of the puzzle. Our lives are made of many moving pieces and you want to be thinking at them holistically, and long-term, to make the decision that is best for you.

### ❤️ Wellbeing

In a work environment that is toxic, or just wrong for you, your emotional state may degrade over time. This is at the same time the strongest argument for changing jobs, and the one for which clues are the hardest to pick up.

That’s because such problems do not spawn over night. It’s most often a long, weary road, where things pile up ever so slowly.

Here is how some people from the community decided it was time for them to go, for reasons connected to their wellbeing:

#### 🏃‍♂️ Engagement:

- [Joel Chippindale](https://twitter.com/joelchippindale), CTO and Coach: [*I leave*] *when I stop being excited about what I believe the future will bring*

- [Aadil Maan](https://twitter.com/aadilmaan), Senior TPM: *Has my emotional state deteriorated or continuously positive? This comes from when you are excited about the work, enjoying the challenge of the project etc.*

- [Andrew Twyman](https://www.linkedin.com/in/andrewtwyman/), Staff Engineer & Coach: *It came down to being disengaged, unexcited, and unmotivated, which would eventually manifest itself in being more hesitant to take on new challenges, or find productive things to do with myself. That in turn reduces learning and growth, which is a negative feedback loop.*

#### 🤗 Appreciation

- [Chris](https://twitter.com/christopheek), Backend Engineer & Consultant: *When an employer sees the work that you output, and nonetheless does not treat you or compensate you according to that output, even though you might have constantly reminded them and showcased it, you would understand that this employer does not value you they way you should be valued, it might be time for you to move on. Lack of appreciation is not about monetary compensation, it's also about receiving a phone call that says "You've been busting your butt off these past 2 weeks, take a long weekend off".*

- [Vaibhav Arora](https://www.linkedin.com/in/vaibhav-arora-startupguy/), Frontend Engineer: *Not feeling valuable […] or when team is not trusting me enough to let me handle big things. It stalls personal growth. At same time, I feel I am not bringing enough value to table in comparison to my capabilities*.

#### 🔪 Culture

- [Jose Escobar](https://www.linkedin.com/in/jaescobar/), CTO: *the last change I did, it was for the exact same pay and with much higher personal risk, but at that point I had been thrown head first into corporation senior management politics and the passive aggressive emails, the backstabbing and the general not caring of top level management made me quickly approach burnout.*

---

These stories may be all different, but I believe they are brought together by a common thread: **energy**.

We all know the feeling of having *high energy* — we feel proactive, confident, happy to take on the challenge. We may end up in a place where we don’t feel that at work. **We are not bringing our best self at work anymore**.

#### 📌 Bottom line decision

Quitting is hard.

Everything is stressful: looking for a new job, interviewing, negotiating, joining a new team of strangers — this is all way out of our comfort zone.

For this reason, if you find yourself pondering to go through all this, chances are you already know it’s the right thing to do.

Most often, gut feeling knows it best, but it can be helpful to reflect on the items above, and even writing down your own thoughts.

As Andrew shared:

- Andrew Twyman: *I often ended up going through a rational process like writing down a list of pros and cons in order to get my rational mind on board with making the step I emotionally wanted. With major life decisions I find that kind of process is often a way of bringing my brain and my emotions into alignment, more than it is a case of discovering an answer which I didn't already have (at some level).*

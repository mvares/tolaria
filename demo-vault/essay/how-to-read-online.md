### 📋 Outline

---

I am pretty much a *process* guy.

I always try to reflect on the things I do, how I do them, and turn them into **workflows** that I can write down and improve over time.

So, a few months ago I published [**How I Write Refactoring**](https://refactoring.fm/p/how-i-write-refactoring-) — a detailed explanation of what I do every week to create a new article. It became my most successful article at the time, and many people reached out for more advice about writing.

Among the most frequent questions there is one about **reading**, that is, how do I make sure I keep reading relevant stuff, and I do it regularly week after week?

### 🍝 Reading is Food

I have always thought at reading / writing as an **input / output** relationship. To write new articles I need to be inspired by what I get in touch with every day, and what I read plays a major role in that. 

But even if I didn't write a weekly newsletter, reading well would be crucial for myself as a human being.

I like to think at information as food. So I can ask myself:

- Am I eating the informational equivalent of **junk food**?

- What's the **nutritional value** of the information I consume?

- What does exactly my **diet** looks like? And why?

### 🏴‍☠️ The Pirate Framework for Reading

I have always read a lot, but it's only after I started writing that I developed a process to read online in a structured way.

This process is made of four steps:

I call it the *Pirate Framework* for Reading, because pirates say ***ARRR. ***It is also a nod to the great [pirate metrics](https://500hats.typepad.com/500blogs/2007/09/startup-metrics.html) by [Dave McClure](https://twitter.com/davemcclure).

How does it work? Let's see each step together 👇

### 🎯 Aim — define your *sources*

As a very first step, I make two lists:

1. **Topics** — I want to read about. They can be anything that is of interest to me, work-related or not. Just make a list of <10 areas I want to learn more or keep myself up to date with.

1. **Sources** — I currently read. Newsletters, magazines, even my favorite twitter accounts, I list everything that is relevant to me and I keep reading regularly.

After I have these, I **match topics with sources** and look at how things look like: 

- Are there topics with *few or no* sources?

- Are there topics with *too many* sources?

I may refine both topics and sources, adding / removing a few until I am satisfied with what I have.

### 📥 Receive — create an *inbox*

This second step is a bit counterintuitive. 

I believe the best way to consume content is not by directly reaching out for it — like you do if you open Twitter and start scrolling — but by making the content available to you in **asynchronous fashion**.

So I created an **inbox** where to put *everything* I might read, from which I can refine and choose what to actually consume.

In order for this to be effective, content should come to the inbox by itself. But how? I do this in two steps:

1. 💌 **Turn everything into newsletters**

1. 📬 **Send newsletters all to the same place**

#### 💌 Turn everything into a newsletter

I use [**Mailbrew**](https://mailbrew.com/) to create digests about pretty much everything that doesn't already come through email. Among these:

- *Tweets* from my favorite accounts

- New posts from *magazines* and *blogs*

- Top posts from *aggregators* (e.g. Hacker News, Product Hunt)

- *Stock* prices

I heavily recommend Mailbrew, but I realize it is a paid service ($10 / month). In alternative, you can setup something similar (albeit less powerful) for free with [Feedly](https://feedly.com/) + [Zapier](https://zapier.com/).

![image](../assets/79a3cae6892a420e.png)

#### 📬 Send newsletters** all to the same place**

After turning everything into digests and newsletters, make sure they all land to the same place. I created a **dedicated email address** just for them.

If you have already several subscriptions and it is too much of a hassle to migrate them to a new address, you can also match most of those emails with this **Gmail filter** 👇 and then forward them to the new address:

```javascript
(unsubscribe OR from:noreply OR from:no-reply OR from:no_reply OR from:do_not_reply OR "[New Article]" OR "[New Post]" OR "make sure you keep getting these emails" OR "this email was sent to" OR "turn off these emails" OR "You’ve received this email because" OR "this e-mail is sent to" OR "this email is sent to" OR "received this email by mistake" -{"confirm your" subject:urgent subject:alert label:notifications})
```

I stole this a while ago from [Fabrizio](https://twitter.com/linuz90) 🙏 (can't find the exact tweet).

In alternative, Mailbrew has a dedicated space for newsletters now. I don't really use it because when it launched I had already the other system in place — but I might switch at some point.

### 🔍 Refine — seed your *backlog*

The truth is, I still **read only ~20%** of what comes to my inbox. This is natural — for example, many newsletters I receive are collections of links and I am only interested to a few at a time.

So when I go through them I still triage between items I actually want to read and those I discard instead. I save articles I want to **read later on **[**Instapaper**](https://www.instapaper.com/), via the web clipper or by forwarding emails to it.

The web clipper is also useful for when I find things to read randomly on the internet, without them passing from the "newsletter inbox".

The Instapaper list becomes my primary **backlog** of things to read. It contains only the very best items because they went through two levels of filtering: 

- They come from **trusted sources** that I decided to receive via email (*receive* step)

- I further **selected** them among all other items in the inbox (*refine* step) 

### 👓 **Read **— consume items at a *later time *

The final step of the process is to actually read what you have saved! 

I mostly read things from my Instapaper backlog, so that means that I very rarely read *on the spot*. That is, I always **save and revisit** instead of just reading what is in front of me.

This **time-shifting** makes me escape the *reactivity* loop. By separating the moment I find something that looks interesting from the moment I actually read it, I am able to apply a better judgement to what it is actually worthy of my time.

Many things feel urgent to be read just because of the **state of mind** we are in. Things we want to read when we are angry, anxious, or bored, are very different from what we want to read when we are happy, or at peace.

By constantly saving content for later, I find that what **seemed interesting** at a given time, often it isn't anymore even just a few hours later — maybe when I am relaxed on my bed with my iPad.

But to make this work you really have to **make it a habit**. Don't read on the spot, save things and revisit them later.

### 🏃‍♂️ **Iterate — improve the *****process***

This is a bonus step, but it is essential. It is hard to setup a complex process and make it work for you from day one. It will take time.

Here are a few ideas not to get stuck:

- ⏱️ **Schedule your habits **— At the beginning, schedule fixed times to go through your inbox and save things for later. You can also schedule time to read from the backlog. 

- 🙅‍♂️ **Don't you like what you read?** Remove / replace sources from which you are rarely saving for later.

- 🤯 **Is your backlog ever growing?** Reduce sources or become more restrictive in what you save.

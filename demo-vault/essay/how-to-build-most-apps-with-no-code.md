### ✍️ Post

Last week I wrote a piece covering the [role of a CTO](https://refactoring.fm/p/life-of-a-cto) in early stage startups. At some point in the article, I recommended using no-code / low-code tools to build your product faster and reduce your total [cost of ownership](https://refactoring.fm/p/the-operating-cost-of-new-features).

When I talk with startup founders, this is among the top 3 most frequent advice that I give.

Likewise, the most frequent answer that I get is: **our product can’t be built with no-code**.

A good 90% of the times, this is straight false. No-code has come a long way to cement itself as a legitimate solution for building things, but most developers and tech leaders still **don’t know how to build** a lot of basic stuff with it.

About one year ago I wrote an article which listed some of my favourite tools 👇

[https://refactoring.fm/p/the-best-no-code-tools-you-should](https://refactoring.fm/p/the-best-no-code-tools-you-should)

This is a 2.0 version of that article, in which you will find:

- **Updated tools** — in the original categories, plus new, relevant ones.

- **Case studies** — either real companies built with no-code, or how you can build familiar products with it.

You will find many many links, but no affiliation. These are all recommendations based on my personal usage.

Here is what we will cover:

- 🔒 **SaaS** — how to run memberships, recurring payments, gate content, and more. 

- 🛒 **Ecommerce **— how to sell digital or physical products.

- 🤝 **Marketplaces** — how to run p2p marketplaces, with two different examples of building Airbnb with no-code.

- 📱 **Mobile **— how to create fully-functional mobile apps.

- 🔌 **Automation **— how to connect parts together and remove manual work.

- 🗄 **Backend **— how to model your data, store it, and expose APIs.

- 🧑‍💻 **Internal Tools **— how to build dashboards, workflows and tools for your operations.

- 💼 **Agencies** — the best partners that can help you create no-code products that are cheap and easy to maintain.

- 📚 **Resources** — the best resources and communities to learn more and find experts.

### 📃 Marketing Pages

Marketing pages are those pages designed to be the first touchpoint with your product. 

They are mostly non-interactive — they inform your customer and may produce an action at the end, like signing up or requesting a demo.  

They are an ideal candidate for no-code solutions, for two reasons:

- **High touch **— copy and content needs to be changed often, ideally by non technical people.

- **Commodity tech** — content is mostly static, so technology is not particularly challenging.

Here are your best bets for them

#### 🔗 [Webflow](https://webflow.com/)

**My absolute favourite**. It has plenty of gorgeous templates to start with, it is flexible enough for developers to use, includes a CMS, and plays nicely with other tools in the space.

Plenty of large companies have built their marketing pages with Webflow, including [Mural.co](http://mural.co/) and [HelloSign.com](http://hellosign.com/). Their pages respectively serve more than 3M and 4M customers / month.

You can find plenty of case studies [here](https://webflow.com/customers).

#### 🔗 [Carrd](https://carrd.co/)

At the absolute opposite of Webflow, you can find Carrd, which is arguably the simplest tool to build **one-page sites**.

Very simple, but still remarkably capable. You can build pages that include forms, payments, various widgets, and custom code.

#### 🔗 [Umso](https://www.umso.com/)

If you are building a classic **startup landing page**, Umso is very hard to beat. It is incredibly easy to use and it includes exactly everything you need — and nothing more.

I built two landing pages with it and I was delighted by the experience.

#### 🔗 [Notion](notion.so) + [Super](https://super.so/)

If you are familiar with Notion, you should consider building your pages with Super.

Super allows you to use Notion like a CMS, and create fast, beautiful static pages on top of it.

Over the last years it has grown into a very flexible tool capable of building almost anything, including landing pages, documentation, directories, blogs, and portfolios.

Check out the [templates](https://super.so/templates).

### 🔒 SaaS

I hear you saying: what does it even mean to build a SaaS with no-code? Each SaaS proposition is unique.

That is true, but most SaaS also have a bunch of features that are commodity, including payments, billing, authentication, and email.

There are many tools that help with that. Here are my favorite ones:

#### 🔗 [Memberstack](https://www.memberstack.com/)

Memberstack is a simple tool you can use to implement membership plans, authentication, and making content private on your website.

You install it as a simple javascript snippet, and you manage members and content through a simple dashboard.

It is handy and easy to use.

For a usage example you can check [DesignJoy](https://www.designjoy.co/) — a design subscription business that makes $1M+ / year via Memberstack subscriptions, and is run by a solo-founder.

#### 🔗 [Outseta](https://www.outseta.com/?via=marco)

Outseta works in a similar fashion as Memberstack, but it is a more comprehensive suite meant to help you with everything you need for a recurring revenue business.

Other than memberships and auth, it also includes sophisticated billing, a CRM, transactional emails, and a surprisingly robust helpdesk.

Pricing is also totally reasonable.

You can find examples of Outseta in the wild at [SDR Nation](https://sdrnation.com/), [PathMatch](https://www.pathmatch.com/), and [Homeroom](https://homeroom.club/).

### 🛒 Ecommerce

Ecommerce is one of the most popular types of internet businesses. There are two tools that stand out as my absolute favourites, based on whether you plan to sell physical or digital products.

#### 🔗 [Shopify](https://shopify.com/)

If you are selling physical products, either with your own stock or by [dropshipping](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Drop_shipping), Shopify is the overwhelmingly right choice.

Some of the [largest shops in the world](https://webinopoly.com/blogs/news/top-100-most-successful-shopify-stores) use Shopify — including the likes of Decathlon, Allbirds, and ColourPop.

#### 🔗 [Gumroad](https://gumroad.com/)

Likewise, Gumroad is increasingly the best choice for digital goods like books, audio, design, or education items. That stands both if you are a small creator, or a brand with a vast portfolio of products.

Gumroad also allows to run recurring subscriptions, and plays nicely with other no-code tools.

Check out [here](https://www.makerpad.co/posts/selling-digital-goods-with-carrd-gumroad) how you can setup a website with a digital purchase, in 10 minutes, with Gumroad and Carrd.

### 🤝 Marketplaces

Marketplaces, with respect to ecommerces, are tricky because they are peer to peer — which generates tons of complexity both business and tech-wise.

However, some of the world’s most successful businesses are marketplaces, like Airbnb, Fiverr, or eBay. There is an old adage among entrepreneurs that says that marketplaces are *hard to build and hard to kill*.

Here are your best bets to build one 👇

#### 🔗 [Sharetribe](https://www.sharetribe.com/)

Sharetribe is the most popular marketplace tool I know of. It is fully managed, a-la-Shopify, and has everything you need to get started.

Here are a couple of success stories that I love:

- [FitMyTime](https://www.fitmytime.com/) — match yoga instructors with students, all over the world.

- [Swimmy](https://www.swimmy.com/) — rent a private pool anywhere in the US.

#### 🔗 [Kreezalid](https://kreezalid.com/)

Kreezalid is similar to Sharetribe, but it sports some more advanced features like multi-purchases, subscription-based marketplaces, customizable SEO elements, and more. 

It also comes at a steeper price.

As for case studies, [Gofreedly](https://www.gofreedly.com/) is a great business built on Kreezalid. It is a marketplace for tours and activities all over the world, in the same fashion of GetYourGuide or Viator. 

#### 🔗 [Bubble](https://bubble.io/)

In alternative to using some managed tool, you can build your marketplace from scratch with Bubble. 

Bubble is arguably the most powerful no-code tool out there. It can be used to build pretty much anything — and marketplaces are a pretty popular choice.

Bubble itself provides tutorials for re-creating two of the world’s most popular marketplaces: [Airbnb](https://bubble.io/blog/build-airbnb-no-code/) and [Fiverr](https://bubble.io/blog/build-fiverr-clone-no-code/).

#### DIY!

You can find even more creative ways for building marketplaces by duck-taping various tools together.

Here is a [good example](https://www.makerpad.co/posts/airbnb-clone), inspired by Airbnb, that uses Airtable, Webflow, Zapier, and Stripe.

These kinds of solutions aren’t really viable for the long term and do not necessarily provide the best UX, but they can be very good for prototyping a product in little time and almost zero cost.

### 📱 Mobile Apps

Mobile apps have long been out of reach for no-code tools, because of the high complexity and the need to be distributed via stores.

This isn’t true anymore, thanks to a new generation of tools 👇

#### 🔗 [Glide](https://www.glideapps.com/)

Glide was and still is my absolute favourite no-code tool. It allows you to create cross-platform apps what work and look great on smartphones, tablets and desktops, incredibly fast.

Apps are backed by data you have on Google Sheets or Airtable. The latter is especially powerful as it can leverage Airtable’s relationships, working as a true relational database.

The [Refactoring Library](https://refactoring.substack.com/p/library) is built with Glide and backed by Airtable.

#### 🔗 [Draftbit](https://draftbit.com/)

I have been tracking Draftbit for a while, and their progress it’s been impressive.

Draftbit provides a visual platform for building [React Native + Expo](https://expo.dev/) apps. They have low-level components you can assemble to build pretty much anything. Custom code can be injected anywhere and the full app’s code can be exported.

#### 🔗 [FlutterFlow](https://flutterflow.io/)

FlutterFlow has recently launched on ProductHunt, and is similar to Draftbit — but based on Flutter!

It has plenty of pre-built components, native Firebase integration, and allows you to export code.

### 🔌 Automation / Plumbing

Sometimes, coding is not about building a product from top to bottom. Sometimes we write code to remove some manual work; to perform a periodic routine; or two connect two processes that would be separate otherwise.

There are many tools that help you with these *under-the-hood* scenarios:

#### 🔗 [Zapier](https://zapier.com/)

The grandfather of no-code tools, it needs no introductions. Capable of connecting almost anything with anything else, it is a cornerstone of automation for most engineering teams.

#### 🔗 [Parabola](https://parabola.io/)

Parabola is similar to Zapier in many ways, but has a stronger focus on manipulating data and creating complex workflows. Also, it is a joy to use.

#### 🔗 [Segment](https://segment.com/)

Segment allows you to collect data and events on your product and send them automatically to other tools. It isn’t exactly a no-code tool — it definitely needs some code to make it work — but it enables so many automated workflows that it makes sense to be mentioned here.

Other tools, in fact, can trigger specific behaviour when they receive certain events, turning segment into a *control center* for other processes.

Here is how Nikita Logachev former CTO at Hospitable.com, used Segment and [Courier](https://www.courier.com/) to trigger automatic notifications 👇

> *At Hospitable.com, we ended up switching to Courier for our multi-channel transactional notification management. All the logic and templates lived in code before. We switched to sending (a lot of) Segment events that got consumed by Courier and linked to templates we set up visually in their UI. More low code than no code. Very scalable though, and removes engineering as a total blocker.*

#### 🔗 [YepCode](https://yepcode.io/)

YepCode lets you create data integration / automation workflows by combining several services, in a serverless environment.

You still have to write some code, but it has [*recipes*](https://yepcode.io/recipes/) that make it easier to do so. I tried it in the past and it nicely bridges the gap between no-code solutions and complex use cases you would need to build yourself otherwise.

[Marcos Muíño](https://www.linkedin.com/in/mmuino/?originalSubdomain=es), YepCode’s co-founder, tells me how they *dog-food* it internally for their sign up flow: 

> We ask user information with [Arengu](https://www.arengu.com/), and then a [YepCode webhook](https://docs.yepcode.io/executions/webhooks) is invoked.

The process that is started with that webhook does the following tasks, that we have been evolving without the need of any deployment 😎

– Validate user information showing any error to the user
– Create user account in our identity and access management solution (we use [Keycloak](https://www.keycloak.org/))
– Create a new database in our multitenant MongoDB
– Perform some needed GraphQL requests to create the user Team
– Import some samples in the user workspace
– Send one onboarding email
– Send a Segment event to register the sign up

All this implementation relies on the integrations we support out of the box.

It must be said that YepCode target user must have coding skills, but you may enjoy a quite agile development experience forgetting about all the devops hassle.

### Backend

What does *no-code backend* even mean? To me, it’s a platform that lets you model your data, create logic and expose APIs.

There aren’t many out there — here are my two favourites 👇

#### 🔗 [Xano](https://www.xano.com/)

Xano truly does it all. It lets you build a PostgresSQL database, creating the schema visually; it automatically generates CRUD endpoints + custom endpoints where you can write your own logic, and it plays nicely with plenty of external tools via integrations and webhooks.

If it seems too good to be true — go check it out.

#### 🔗 [Airtable](https://airtable.com/)

Can you use Airtable as your backend? In a surprisingly high number of cases, the answer is yes.

Airtable lets you:

- Create a fully-fledged relational database in the fastest way I know of 

- Add plenty of business logic through formulas and aggregations

- Expose CRUD API out of the box.

Airtable API has rigid throughput limits, so this might be viable only for internal tools (see more below), products that are mostly read-only, and MVPs. But still! When Airtable is a good fit, it is 100x faster than building things on your own.

### 🧑‍💻 Internal Tools

Internal tools are products and workflows that you adopt within your company, used (also) by non-technical people.

They are true products that can make a huge difference on productivity. In my experience, the best companies I have known always empowered people with the best internal tools.

Fortunately, no-code *thrives* on internal tools 👇

#### 🔗 [Airtable](https://airtable.com/)

If what I wrote above about Airtable isn’t enough, you should consider that:

- Airtable has a fantastic UX for any back-office / database / spreadsheet-like product. Chances are, if you model your data correctly, Airtable’s default UX already makes for a good internal tool by itself.

- Airtable sports many add-ons (e.g. charts, PDF designers, email, sms) that complement nicely the *data* features.

These days, if I have to build an internal tool, I try to build it with Airtable first, and only move to other solutions later.

#### 🔗 [Retool](https://retool.com/)

Speaking of when Airtable isn’t enough, here is Retool.

Retool let’s you build internal apps by connecting your own data sources, defining custom queries, and assembling pre-made frontend components.

It has plenty of templates, integrations, and has recently added the option to build mobile apps, too.

#### 🔗 [Appsmith](https://www.appsmith.com/)

Appsmith is very similar to Retool, but it’s open source and can be self-hosted. Similarly, features plenty interations and widgets you can combine.

#### 🔗 [Airplane](https://www.airplane.dev/)

Airplane transforms scripts into internal tools that anyone can use. It’s a very clever tool that will make you think: “*why has nobody thought about this before?*”

You can take APIs, SQL queries, and custom scripts, and turn them into small tools that can be run by anybody.

It has plenty of integrations; it can take data from your own database and sources, and supports multi-step and scheduled workflows.

### 💼 Agencies

Sometimes, it might be scary to build a complex product with no-code tools, especially if your team hasn’t much experience with them.

In this case, you can bootstrap the process by working with a specialized agency. Here are the best ones I know of, based on recommendations by readers and reviews on [Clutch](https://clutch.co/):

- [**8020**](https://www.8020.inc/)** **— US-based. One of the first no-code agencies to appear in the market. They specialize in Webflow development.

- [**9x**](https://www.9x.agency/) — Berlin-based agency that works with the likes of Bubble, Airtable, Zapier, and Retool. They also provide training to your people so they can maintain the product later, and build more in the future. 

- [**AirDev**](https://www.airdev.co/)** **— the most trusted no-code agency on Clutch. They use primarily Bubble to build products for startups, and internal tools. They also run a bootcamp to educate people about developing with Bubble. 

- [**Codemap**](https://codemap.io/) — marketplace that connects companies with vetted no-code experts. A good solution if you are looking to hire someone for the long-term, rather than working with an agency.

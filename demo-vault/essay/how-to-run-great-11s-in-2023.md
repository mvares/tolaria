# How to Run Great 1:1s in 2023

In a world where we strive to reduce meetings and push for async communication, there is at least one kind of meeting we need to keep and nurture: 1:1s.

This was the introduction I wrote in April 2021 when I published my first article on 1:1s. It turned out to be my most successful article so far, when Refactoring had just ~4,000 readers. Today, it remains one of my most popular pieces, and people often refer to it on social media and ask me questions about it.

Why do 1:1s draw so much attention? Because, if anything, their importance has only grown with time.

Just as handcrafted goods become more precious when the majority are mass-produced, 1:1s *stand out* in a world full of bad meetings and shallow Slack chats. In fact, we probably need more of them, and we need to get them right.

So, this article is meant to be a practical guide on having great 1:1s, updated for 2023.

It is one of my favourite topics and over the years I read almost anything I could find about it. I also learned a few things I didn't find on books and would love to share.

Here is what we will cover today:

- 📖 **What 1:1s are** — and what they are not.

- 🎯 **Why 1:1s** — let’s talk about safe spaces and open doors.

- 📋 **How to have good 1:1s** — the actual guide, including advice about time, topics, journaling, and how to follow up.

- ✨ **Templates** — my personal template for running and storing 1:1s on Notion.

- 📚 **Resources **— the best articles and resources to learn more.

So let's dive in!

### 📖 What 1:1s are

1:1s are private, periodic meetings between you and your direct reports, usually held weekly. In a 1:1, your report sets the agenda and is expected to talk about about anything they like.

They are mainly used to get honest feedback from your report, discuss personal development, and any issue that arose during the week. These are the meetings where you get to coach your people and find ways to resolve conflicts.

[https://refactoring.fm/p/how-to-give-feedback-](https://refactoring.fm/p/how-to-give-feedback-)

Such definition is important to avoid common misconceptions:

- **1:1s are not about status update** — even though occasionally this is a good conversation starter. There are other processes for that, which, most probably, shouldn’t rely on a meeting. Status in 1:1s is only useful as a means to talk about issues.

- **1:1s with peers are not real 1:1s** — I know many people who have recurring 1:1s with peers with no particular agenda: just to small talk and update each other. While it is good to build rapport intentionally, I have found that such meetings, set on a tight schedule (e.g. weekly), are often sneaky time wasters, especially when multiplied by many peers you may have. I have covered better ways to build rapport in remote teams in a previous article 👇

### 🎯 **Why** 1:1s?

I have found that the value of 1:1s is usually obvious in large companies, but way less so in small teams. Many leaders think they already know their people and talk with them more than enough, and there is no need to schedule weekly calls with them.

This is a mistake I have made for a long time as a founder.

You feel people can already reach out to you when they have an issue. Probably this is true, and they do to some extent.

This is also, however, the classic [open door fallacy](https://www.guygeltner.net/blog/1162020the-open-door-fallacy). An *open door* still puts the burden on the shoulders of your reports, who sometimes may not feel like raising an issue, or may go to great lengths to fix things themselves before reaching out for help.

1:1s, instead, are *intentional*.

They create a safe, recurring space where people are allowed to approach you with their needs. 

#### The Safe Space 🧘

For your reports, knowing they have this space changes the game completely.

They become more mindful about their issues, because they know they will be able to discuss them with you every week. They will note them down in anticipation of the meeting.

Nurturing this private space has three main side-effects:

- 💣 **It defuses issues before they become too big**. — without 1:1s, people have to win some friction to reach out to you and speak about problems. So they will naturally try to fix them by themselves first, with the result of speaking to you when it's too late.

- 🗓️ **It frees the rest of the week from this kind of talk **— "Let's talk in our 1:1" becomes a common way of scheduling a conversation about personal development, keeping the rest of the time focused on production.

- ❤️ **It builds true rapport** — between managers and reports. As a manager, these are your most important meetings. It’s where you get to coach your people and build long-term trust. I talked more about coaching, mentoring, and sponsoring people in this previous piece 👇

### 📋 How to have good 1:1s

This section is a practical guide on how to have good 1:1s. Here are three main areas I would like to cover:

1. ⏱️ **Time** — duration and scheduling.

1. **📖 Topics** — how to run the meeting and what to talk about.

1. ☀️ **Weather report **— a practical way to track mood and rate issues.

1. **✍️ Journaling** — taking notes, producing artifacts, and other useful tricks.

1. ➡️ **Following up **— acting on what comes up from 1:1s.

#### 1) Time ⏱️ 

Most advice here is about consistency and building the habit:

- **Have 1:1s every single week** — 1:1s are about issues and personal development. Whenever someone has an issue, they have to think they will be able to discuss it with you *soon*. The "soon" part is crucial to most 1:1s benefits, and "once every two weeks" is often not soon enough.

- **Have 1:1s always at the same day, same time.** This is important to build the habit, just like having breakfast or brushing your teeth. People need to prepare for 1:1s, and need to know when these will happen.

- **Schedule 30 minutes for them.** In my experience, 20-25 minutes might be enough, but you don't want to cut the meeting short in case they are not. Especially because long 1:1s are usually important 1:1s. 

- **Always always do them.** Finally, this might be trivial, but it's really important not to cancel 1:1s. 1:1s work because they are a reliable space. If they stop being reliable, they stop working.

![Weekly 1:1s are good at *defusing* issues before they become too big. Bi-weekly 1:1s...less so.](../assets/5adf75fbe08d40bf.png)

#### 2) Topics 📖

I always open the meeting with a softball question: "*How are you this week?*". This is because:

- **I want my report to run the meeting** — I don't want to start with something from my own agenda.

- **It is more *****specific*** than a simple "How are you" and it's less likely to receive a standard, mindless answer.

During the first minutes, it's inevitable sometimes to discuss project status. This can be useful to get into specific issues, but try to move away from it as quickly as possible. A few recommendations:

**1)** **Be a good listener** — keep the focus on your report and let them do 80% of the talking. Make the conversation move forward with questions that dig more into what they are saying. Do not underestimate how much concentration you need to do this effectively.

**2) Have some prepared points to fallback to** — If the conversation stagnates and it becomes clear there aren't many things to discuss, I fallback to a couple of topics I have prepared in advance. These are usually:

- **Follow-ups to past topics **— if you write down 1:1 notes (more on that later), you can ask about items that came up in the past to see it anything changed, or update your report about how you took action on something.

- **Feedback **— you may take advantage of the 1:1 to share some feedback about recent events. Most feedback should be shared on the *spot*, but 1:1s are a good spot for having more feedback conversations. More on giving good feedback 👇

[**How to Give Feedback 💛**](https://refactoring.fm/p/how-to-give-feedback-)

#### 3) Weather Report ☀️

At the end of the 1:1 I ask my report to *rate* the week, in the form of a weather report. They can chose between 5 levels, from clear sun to rain with thunders and lightning.

I also write down action items I can work on, based on the feedback I received. These items get the "weather rating" too:

- ☀️ ***This is good*** — *I am happy that [...]*

- 🌤️ ***This can be improved**** — I would like to [...]*

- ☁️ ***This is a problem**** — I am worried about [...]*

- 🌧️ ***This is a big problem**** — It really annoys me that [...]*

- ⛈️ ***This is a crucial problem**** — It threatens my stay at the company that [...]*

Such ratings are really helpful to put things in context — you may hear a lot of complaints in a 1:1 but you are not always able to understand how *good* or *bad* things are in general.

I have a fun story about this.

At my startup, I have had two directs for many years with whom I held weekly 1:1s. One of them was always positive-ish: during 1:1s he would tell me about the things he had done during the week, and ask me for feedback about this and that. 

The other one was complaining all the time. 

He always told me that we should do that better, this other thing is not working, and those specs sucked.

At the beginning I didn’t use the *weather rating*, and I was constantly worried the second developer would just quit at some point. I tried to act on most things he reported, and I had long walks with him dissecting all the feedback.

After a while, I started adopting the *weather report* as a way to prioritize stuff that came up in conversation.

To my surprise, the second developer would always give high overall rates (☀️ or 🌤️) to the weeks, while the first one, who seemed happier, would give *cloudier* ones.

So I asked them (individually) what was going on:

- The second developer, who always complained, told me he was actually happy — he just thought he should use 1:1s to report things we could do better.

- The first developer, who was secretly sad, instead, told me he didn’t report some of the issues because he didn’t think I could do anything about them.

So, I am not saying that sun or cloud emojis will magically solve your 1:1 problems. But you should always strive to have deep, authentic conversations in 1:1s where you get to the core of problems. If you turn 1:1s into simple routine where you go through the usual questions and call it a day, you will eventually miss out on how people truly feel.

#### 4) Journaling ✍️

I use a shared Notion document (I have put the template in the section below 👇) with each of my reports where I store 1:1 notes. For each 1:1, I store it with the date, weather rating, and main items.

Since each item also gets its own rating, during the week I can check at a glance which are the most important things I should work on, basically based on how bad they are.

The shared doc is also a space where reports can go on their own and add things they want to discuss during the next 1:1. It makes the process more asynchronous and interactive from both sides. 

Finally, sharing my notes makes sure I don't get anything wrong. If my notes do not represent what my report has said, they can tell me.

These notes, however, are not the only type of *journaling* I do about my reports. There are also more serendipitous notes I take about my impressions on work they did, feedback I want to give, and more. I keep those private, and they are kind of an *accomplishment journal*, but for about reports instead of myself 👇

[https://refactoring.fm/p/how-to-write-an-accomplishment-journal](https://refactoring.fm/p/how-to-write-an-accomplishment-journal)

My private notes are important in many circumstances:

- **Performance reviews** — I get back to them when it’s time to recollect how my reports performed during the period

- **Fallback topics for 1:1s** — as already said above, having some notes of your own is helpful to prepare a few points in advance of 1:1s for when the conversation stagnates.

[https://refactoring.fm/p/performance-reviews](https://refactoring.fm/p/performance-reviews)

#### 5) Following up ➡️

A crucial part of 1:1s acting on issues that arise.

If you don’t, people will gradually stop trusting 1:1s, will stop reporting issues, making the whole ceremony useless.

This is typically the problem when you have too many reports: you may have time for the actual 1:1s, but you won’t have time to act on issues. For this reason, I have always find hard to go past 6-7 reports and still be effective. Sure, I have known execs with 10-12 reports, but their relationship with them looked shallow, and those people looked to others to get help.

### ✍️ Post

---

A couple of months ago, Aditya Agarwal, former CTO at Dropbox, made a splash on Twitter with his take on engineering management 👇

[Embed](https://twitter.com/adityaag/status/1509196363019395081)

Aditya argues that modern tooling like Asana or Slack made management drastically easier. 

Today, teams of 100 can stay in sync at the same level as a team of 10, and you need way fewer managers than in the past — if any.

Quoting Aditya 👇

> *In my experience, you can have roughly 30-35 engineers without formal management. You will likely want some Tech Leads before then but that is way simpler than a management layer*

This take is clearly controversial and, unsurprisingly, spawned a hot debate both on social media and on the [Refactoring community](https://refactoring.substack.com/p/community).

**Do you really need managers anymore? Or, how far can you go without them?**

This week I tried to break this down in a way that is more nuanced than a Twitter thread. We will cover

- 3️⃣ **Management is three things (+1)** — 10,000 feet view of what a manager should do.

- 🔨 **How tools are helping **— are they?

- 👑 **Formal managers vs management** — do you need full-time, formal managers?

- 🔄 **Distributed management** — can you replace managers with processes?

Let’s dive in 👇

### 3️⃣ Management is three things (+1)

Paraphrasing Aditya himself, engineering managers’ responsibilities can be organized into three main areas (plus one):

- **Planning** — organising work into OKRs, sprints, weeks, or whatever process you use. It includes scoping, prioritising, allocating resources, and negotiating with stakeholders.

- **Alignment** — keeping people coordinated on what needs to be done. Removing obstacles. Adding what’s missing at any given time.

- **Health & Growth** — managing people and the overall team’s health. Taking care of feedback, careers, and recruiting.

- **Bonus: Tech Leadership** — this is largely a separate responsibility than EM, but let’s face it, many companies don’t have the headcount to separate the two, so these roles may conflate on the same person.

How are tools helping with these areas? Well, it’s complicated.

Let’s see them one by one.

![image](../assets/137576c9710e42fd.png)

#### 🗺️ Planning

**Tools impact: medium **🟨

Modern tools like [Linear](https://linear.app/), [Shortcut](https://shortcut.com/), or (*inhales*) Jira, undeniably simplify **creating and maintaining plans**. 

They give you views at different levels of granularity, from high-level timelines down to the single tasks, so that, in healthy teams, engineers can manage their own tickets and simplify the work of managers.

However, tools are less helpful when it comes to scoping, allocating resources, and all tasks where you need to **negotiate with stakeholders**. These are inherently managerial duties. To be effective you need your influence and judgement — with or without tools.

Now, the actual effort on this largely depends on the stage of your company / product.

In a small, cohesive team that works on a **single product**, like an early stage startup, you are likely not spending much time negotiating or allocating resources. So — I agree with Aditya — you can probably distribute most planning duties among engineers, and leverage tools for tracking.

Things get trickier when you start having **parallel work** streams, and people fighting for resources.

Shiva Ramagopal, Engineering Manager, weighs on this 👇

> *The critical point can vary between organizations depending on the product, market, team maturity and the culture. 

For some, it can be when they hit a count of engineers or the number of parallel streams of work that the entire org is handling at any given point of time.

My personal experience has been that when there are multiple work streams all competing for engineering bandwidth, engineering management of some form is needed to for resolving/aligning priorities, documenting stuff, hiring to increase the engineering bandwidth, mentoring and so on.*

So, the tipping point might not be much about engineering headcount, but rather about how *diverse *the work to be done is, and how much negotiation it requires.

In that respect, a product-focused, 25-engineers team, might require less management than a 10-engineers web agency scattered across multiple projects.

#### 🔀 Alignment

**Tools impact: high **🟩

Alignment is about running ongoing projects, keeping people on the same page, and removing obstacles. 

Here, tools really help a lot.

The remote shift brought a renewed focus on async + written communication, to the point where most operational meetings can now be replaced by good, async alternatives.

Here are common tasks where you can use async tools instead of meetings: 

- **Create rich requirements** → [Notion](https://notion.so/), [Coda](https://coda.io/)

- **Take decisions** → [Threads](https://threads.com/), [Twist](https://twist.com/)

- **Run stand-ups** → [StatusHero](https://statushero.com/), [Range](https://www.range.co/)

- **Run status updates** → [Linear](https://linear.app/), [Shortcut](https://shortcut.com/), [StatusHero](https://statushero.com/)

#### ❤️ Health & Growth

**Tools impact: low **🟥

This is where you take care of feedback, careers, and recruiting. As a manager, it is your most personal role, and there isn’t much that tools can do to make it easier.

Is it always so? Many believe that top requires less management effort — so if you keep hiring **A-players**, you won’t need managers.

This is a fallacy.

While it is true that great engineers may help more on planning or may stay aligned more easily, they require just as much work (if not more) on the **growth** side.

A-players want to grow and have an impact like all other engineers. Helping on this is a key managerial duty, and likely the main reason you may introduce managers in the first place.

[Pamela Gotti](https://www.linkedin.com/in/pamelagotti/), CTO at Credimi, reported her experience on this 👇

> W*e felt the need of EM as soon as the team became big and "old" - in our case big was about 15 people and "old" means that we had been working together for more than 3 years.

I think that we worked well for 3 years because there was plenty to do in the early stages of a startup. But as soon as we calmed down a bit, everybody felt the need of having a clear career path in front of them, and the CTO could not possibly take care of the growth of 15 people alone while working on his daily job.

In Credimi now I have 3 eng manager for roughly the same team size, their role is to make the team grow (sizing, hiring, defining career paths) while working with the product manager to make things happen. […]

I'm really not sure how I would manage the team without EMs, as I would spend the whole week in 1:1s.*

#### 💻 Bonus: Tech Leadership

It is common in many teams to have senior devs taking both tech leadership and some managerial duties. 

This may happen when 1) your headcount is small, and 2) you have people who can pull this off — yes, they do exist.

However, the more you grow, the harder it gets to keep such roles overlapped. For two main reasons:

- **Growth requires work** — fast growth brings extra burden on managers when it comes to hiring, onboarding, designing [goals for new hires](https://refactoring.fm/p/30-60-90?s=w), and more.

- **Need for career paths **— the larger your team grows, the more you will need a structured career path that separates managerial and IC roles. This is important both for people’s expectations on their own growth, and to align your company to the broader tech market when it comes to hiring.

I wrote more about these two roles in a previous Refactoring article 

- [https://refactoring.fm/p/the-role-of-tech-leads-and-engineering?s=w](https://refactoring.fm/p/the-role-of-tech-leads-and-engineering?s=w)-

#### 📌 Final breakdown

We have listed the three main management areas, but do they weigh the same? How much of your time goes into each of them?

Aditya is right about the fact that, historically, *a ton *of time is spent on alignment. If good tools and processes can bring this down, it is a massive time saver for managers.

So, for sure, you need fewer managers than in the past. This is natural, as it is just the secular trend about technology augmenting humans.

As **Yurii Mykytyn**, Director of Engineering at Rebbix, noted:

> *We need fewer managers and clerks compared to 50 yrs ago, probably to 20 yrs ago, and perhaps a bit less than 10 yrs ago.*

If there is a *threshold* after which you need full-time managers, that threshold is definitely higher than in the past.

But does management equates to *full-time managers*? Is this a 0→1 transition in which one day you have a happy utopia of engineers, and all of a sudden you introduce ✨ Management ™* *✨ in the form of full-fledged EMs?

Of course not — and this is the crucial point 👇

### 👑 Formal Managers vs Management

Management is first and foremost a *behavior*.

Take the three main areas we discussed above. It doesn’t really matters whether you have formal managers or not — it matters that they are covered, in a way or another.

Because while full-time managers *can* wait, you *can’t* wait for such behaviours. You need them from day one.

- Don’t wait for managers to **have 1:1s**

- Don’t wait for managers to **create plans**

- Don’t wait for managers to **give feedback** to people 

To avoid having formal managers, here are your best bets:

- **Distribute responsibilities **— across the members of your team

- **Invest in processes** — that replace human decisions

Let’s look at both 👇

#### Distribute responsibilities

When a company is small, most managerial tasks are performed by the founders. 

Founders, though, get easily overwhelmed, and their role is a continuous pendulum between [doing things that don’t scale](http://paulgraham.com/ds.html), and replacing themselves by delegating these very things. 

Delegating managerial tasks doesn’t necessarily mean hiring (or appointing) managers. You can go a long way by:

1. Acknowledging something that has to be done

1. Distributing it across the team by allocating official time.

Hiring makes for a good example. You can “recruit” part-time hiring managers among your engineers, to perform hiring duties along-side their regular work.

This is healthy as long as you are upfront about it and expectations are clear on all sides.

You can be even more creative. [Nicolas Zermati](https://twitter.com/nicoolas25) works at Alan, an 80+ engineers startup with no managers. They have rotating coaches to take care of 1:1s and the growth of more junior peers 👇

> *To complement the processes that are "impersonal", we have another role: "****The Coach****", that every employee can take. It is about helping more junior peers grow, adapt to their context better, and overcome their challenges. 

Engineers' coaches are mostly other engineers, and they handle the 1:1. Depending on the coachees' challenges, we can redirect them to other persons or advise them to bring some topics to the "community" level, publicly…*

#### Invest in processes

Many managerial tasks can be documented and turned into processes, thus requiring little human intervention.

Think of salary grids, career paths, but also day-to-day things like prioritizing bugs.

Whenever you make a decision, write down your thought process to make it repeatable. That is key for delegation.

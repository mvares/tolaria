### ✍️ Post

---

The startup scene here in Italy is pretty small. This is most often a problem, but it also means that the community is tight-knit and we all know each other.

Since I founded my startup, in 2012, I have often been involved in mentoring other early-stage founders and CTOs. I still do it today.

So, the most recurring question I get from CTOs is: ***what should I focus on?***** **Or, if you prefer — *how should I spend my time?*

Answering this *directly* is close to impossible, because the CTO role depends on many factors. I have found that the three most significant ones are:

- 📱 **Product / Business **— the nature of your product deeply affects your role. If your tech is commodity (e.g. you are building yet another SaaS), your main concern might be going fast and staying lean, without reinventing the wheel. If your product involves a strong technical challenge, instead, […]  

- 🏢 **Size / stage of your company **— like all C-level roles, being a CTO is a *shapeshifting* duty that changes with the scale of your team.

- 👑 **Executive team **— much of your *scope* is shaped by the qualities and gaps of people around you. What are your co-founders / peers good at? The best teams fill each other’s gaps.

Nevertheless, there are some common traits about the work of the best CTOs I have known, especially early-stage ones.

With startups, it is useful to organize responsibilities in three main areas: **Tech**, **Product**, and **Marketing**.

![image](../assets/31d1f65202514713.png)

Such areas are not clear-cut and have often big overlaps. For example:

- *SEO* is a marketing / distribution topic that requires a strong technical work.

- *Member-get-member* strategies are both non-trivial product features *and* distribution channels.

- A good *technical strategy* often *informs* the product of what can be built. Famously, Steve Jobs decided to build the iPhone *after* he saw a prototype of a multi-touch screen in the lab.

The biggest overlap of all, though, lies at the center, and is about **process **— that is, how we work together.

Out of the various leadership roles, CTOs are the ones most likely to take care of process. This is probably because of their engineering mindset — they are just more likely to see the company as a system, and act accordingly.

So, this article covers what you should focus on as a CTO when it comes to your **process **and** tech**. We will cover some principles to build your tech, and how to organize your team.

I will cover these in three different stages of a Startup life:

- 🌱 **Pre-seed** — where you learn more about the problem / solution space.

- 🪴 **Seed** — where you look for product-market-fit.

- 🌳 **Series A** — where you scale your product and business for the first time.

Sure, there are more stages! But these are the ones I am the most familiar with — and are also where the most action happens.

Let’s go 👇

### 🌱 Pre-seed

The pre-seed stage is an **exploratory** stage. You may start with some assumptions about the problem you are going to solve and how, but you better approach this with an open mind.

Some of these assumptions will hold, but most won’t. Does this problem exist? How can I solve it for people? Are people willing to pay? Who are my ideal customers? How do I reach them?

Let’s see how you can organize your team to answer these questions.

#### Team

At the beginning it’s just about you, your co-founders, and maybe a couple of friends.

As long as your team is smaller than ~10 people, your best bet is to hire **smart generalists**, rather than people who are big on some specific tech. For many reasons:

- You may have no idea about what tech to use — and even if you do, such idea is likely to change.

- Generalists are usually open and happy to use whatever is needed to deliver the product. Specialists, instead, are more likely to feel the urge to use whatever they know better, even if it is the suboptimal choice.

As a CTO, you should assemble a founding engineering team that is flexible, passionate, and capable of growing together with your company.

More about generalists 👇

[https://refactoring.substack.com/p/generalists](https://refactoring.substack.com/p/generalists) 

#### Process

At this scale, communication is easy and there is no need to setup fancy processes. Just focus on creating a basic **work loop**. This is what it may look like:

- 🔨 **Weekly dev cycle **— you may have a lightweight planning at the beginning, and a demo for the whole team at the end. You still want to [ship things ASAP](https://refactoring.fm/p/how-shipping-fast-changes-your-life?s=w), likely multiple times a day. The ceremonies help with pacing and with keeping everyone on the same page.  

- 🎯 **Monthly goals** — a longer timeframe cycle is useful to anchor things you don’t want to do every week. You may do some product strategy, set targets for your KPIs, and have [retrospectives](https://refactoring.fm/p/retrospectives)

- ☀️ **Standups** — I am a big fan of [async standups](https://refactoring.fm/p/standups). They take 5 mins of your time, force you to reflect on your day, and solve 80% of the blockers / coordination problems that may arise.

This is not a one-size-fits-all recipe. You should experiment and stick with what works for you. We used to run Scrum — over time we changed the [parts we didn’t like](https://refactoring.fm/p/scrum-2022?s=w) and invented our own variations until we were happy with the result.

#### Tech

When you think about tech at this stage, there are two mental models that I find to be the most useful:

1. **Be fast **— optimize for [shipping fast and often](https://refactoring.fm/p/how-shipping-fast-changes-your-life?s=w). Even if all your initial assumptions are wrong, if you keep talking with customers and iterating on the product, you will eventually get things right. The faster you build, the more time you can *buy* to become successful. 

1. **Expect to scrap everything **— because sooner or later it will happen. At this stage, there is no way to prevent tech debt coming from the ever changing landscape. So do not fixate on long-term tech choices, because nobody knows what long-term looks like.

When you combine *being fast* with *expecting to scrap everything*, the most logical solution is to write as least code as possible.

To deliver a product feature, writing code is by far the most expensive activity you can perform, when you combine 1) the cost of engineers, 2) the time it takes to write code, and 2) the [cost of future ownership](https://refactoring.fm/p/the-operating-cost-of-new-features).

At this stage, no-code and low-code tools are your friends, and you can [build pretty much anything with them](https://refactoring.fm/p/the-best-no-code-tools-you-should). Seriously — if you can’t prototype your MVP with low-code tools, chances are you should reduce its scope.

### 🪴 Seed

At *seed *stage, you have likely understood your problem better, built a prototype solution for it, and you are now looking for product-market fit.

You have your first hires in, and, based on where you are, your team may stretch to >10 people.

As a CTO, when it comes to process, you have two main goals now:

1. Create a basic team structure

1. Organize leadership and management responsibilities

Let’s see them both.

#### Team structure

There are two main ways to organize people and make them work together:

**Functional teams** are based on an area of expertise (e.g. frontend, backend). When you have to deliver a feature, you can assemble temporary *feature teams* on the fly, with everyone who needs to be involved.

**Cross-functional teams **work independently on some product area, and contain all the skills they need to do so.

At this stage, cross-functional teams are a largely bad fit for startups, for two reasons:

- **Headcount** — You don’t have the headcount to split people in truly independent teams. You likely still have many *singletons*—people who are the only ones to perform their function—who should be shared across many teams, breaking the independence and making planning harder.

- **Stability** — It is rare that you can identify stable product areas on which to allocate a given amount of resources for a long time. 

Conversely, **functional teams **are a good choice at this stage because they let you retain the agility to plan work just in time. They also help you build *practices *around the functions, ensuring coherence and quality. 

![image](../assets/fedb18138575450b.png)

#### Leadership / Management responsibilities

As long as you could count engineers on one hand, as a CTO you would probably take all *non-coding* responsibilities on yourself.

At this point, instead, you should start scaling back your role to 1) avoid the team getting bottlenecked, and 2) create growth opportunities for your best people.

There are two main responsibilities you should look at:

- **Technical leadership **— guiding technical decisions, training younger folks, nurturing best practices.

- **Engineering management **— take care of people’s well-being, productivity, hiring, and growth.

There are infinite setups to make sure these needs are met. You may start by understanding your people’s inclinations: is there anyone who has already exhibited some leadership or management traits? Are they interested in taking them in a formal role?

You may have engineers performing part-time as managers, and retaining their IC role for the rest of their time. Or you may discover that you have good tech leaders but no one is really interested in management, so you settle with you acting as an EM, while delegating more of the technical direction.

In the end, only two things matter: 1) people perform in a role that fits them, and 2) all responsibilities are taken care of.

This process is often non-linear, and you will swing between different roles as your company grows and priorities shift.

[Nikita Logachev](https://www.linkedin.com/in/nikita-logachev/?originalSubdomain=cz), who has several years of experience as CTO and VP of Engineering at different startups, told me how he had to put different hats and jump between them over time 👇

> *The role is both evolving and dynamic, particularly for an early-to-mid stage startup. […]*

[Andrzej Kopera](https://www.linkedin.com/in/andrzej-kopera/?originalSubdomain=pl), with his work at Idego, has interviewed several CTOs about their role and needs:

> *From my conversations with CTOs, it appears that the duties depend on the organization's size, at what stage it is, and what kinds of projects it is doing.

In small companies, the CTO is usually the ****lead engineer turned CTO****. It's someone who knows the system from the beginning, the entire history of the project, all the problems that appeared, and the background of the decisions taken […]

In addition, the CTO will be responsible for ****managing the engineering team****, developing people, and ensuring the coherence of the technologies used in the project so as not to create unnecessary technological debt. He liaises with the product department and also acts as an interface between the dev team and the rest of the company.*

#### Process

At this stage, your dev process might not change much. You may create more formal goals (e.g. [with OKRs](https://refactoring.fm/p/how-to-create-good-okrs-)) or just run on the same monthly planning.

You should, however, put in place basic managerial processes:

- Put **reporting** in place between devs and EMs (or someone acting as EM)

- Setup weekly **1:1s** with reports

- Assign **hiring** responsibilities

- Have monthly / quarterly **retrospectives** with individual people, which act as MVP for future performance reviews.

#### Tech

If in the previous stage you couldn’t trust anything to hold in the future, this is now the time to build some **foundations**.

What should be the **core tech assets** of your company? What is unique to you and generates a competitive advantage? You should build yourself those few things, and make **aggressive buy** vs make decisions on everything else.

Your app might look like a patchwork of custom pieces and managed services, and that’s ok!

Let’s say you are building a SaaS. You may build your main features yourself, with custom code, while at the same time running the landing page on [Webflow](https://webflow.com/), and memberships on [Outseta](https://www.outseta.com/). 

### 🌳 Series A

At this stage you have found something that works and you are growing your team to **scale the business**.

How does this change your processes and tech?

#### Process

The larger headcount and the increased stability make convenient to create more **durable teams** around the staples of your product.

![image](../assets/a22fbc0c48054091.png)

Feature teams, in fact, are flexible, but in the long run they pose some crucial problems:

- **Overhead **— the larger your team, the heavier it gets to assign work in *just-in-time* style. People fight for resources and waste energy on negotiation. At some point, it becomes more convenient to have stable allocations around some key areas.

- **Little ownership** — constantly rotating people across product areas doesn’t build strong ownership, that is important for long-term strategy. Feature teams are by definition not owners of what they do, and rely on some decision making from outside (e.g. executives).

More about these tradeoffs 👇

[https://refactoring.fm/p/feature-teams-vs-durable-teams-](https://refactoring.fm/p/feature-teams-vs-durable-teams-)

With durable, cross-functional teams, you trade some flexibility in allocating resources, for higher execution speed and stronger ownership.

This is not a trivial trade-off, because flexibility is valuable. Creating stable teams is a big investment that, just like with code, you should only make for things that deserve it. Also, this transition is not all-or-nothing: you may start with just one or two stable teams, and keep other people rotating on the other tasks. Check how things work for a while, and stay open to going back or double down on the new process.

#### Tech

As you scale your tech, you will gradually replace out-of-the-shelf solutions with custom ones. You will also have to choose the main pieces of your tech stack. I have a bias for choosing **boring technology **— because it is predictable and it mostly fails in *known* ways.

![image](../assets/1841f73886694aef.png)

I am a fan of the [*innovation tokens*](https://mcfunley.com/choose-boring-technology#:~:text=gets%20about%20three-,innovation%20tokens,-.%20You%20can%20spend) model by [Mcfunley](https://twitter.com/mcfunley). It basically says: you can’t innovate on everything, so limit yourself to what is worthy. So, if your product / business is already unproven, better to couple it with proven technology.

More on this 👇

[https://refactoring.fm/p/how-to-choose-between-boring-and](https://refactoring.fm/p/how-to-choose-between-boring-and)

### 🔄 The CTO Cycle

All in all, I have found that the CTO role follows a continuous cycle:

1. **📚 Learn** — you figure out what to do and how.

1. **🔨 Do** — you do things yourself first, with your own hands.

1. 🔀** Replace **— you replace yourself, either by *hiring *or *automation.*

The paradox, as a CTO, and even more as a founder, is that you are on a quest to continuously replace yourself. To make yourself irrelevant.

To do your job well, you should figure out what makes you valuable, find a way to empower your team to do it in your place, and move on.

Move on to what? To the next thing where you can bring unique value.

Have fun, it’s a hell of a ride!

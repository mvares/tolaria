# The Startup Checklist

### ✍️ Post

---

As a former founder, I am in touch with many other founders, and they make for a sizeable part of my network.

With the ones I am the closest with we have periodic check-ins, where I just try to be a sounding board and coach them through any hardships — which startups always have plenty of.

These chats, though, tend to be all over the place. Founders often get deep into their problems, losing part of the big picture: they see the trees but not the forest anymore.

Which is, by the way, one of the benefits of talking with external people: that they don’t have such a baggage, so they can look at things with fresh eyes.

But what things?

Over time I created a small list of the most important areas I believe you need to get right in your team. It’s a checklist I kind of go through, mentally, whenever I speak with founders.

Think of it as a health check for your team. You can run it periodically and figure out how you are doing.

So, this article goes through all the items, explains the reasoning, and what good looks like.

Here are the macro items:

1. 📈 **Growth process**

1. 🔍 **Learning process**

1. 🎯 **Mission + Goals + Planning**

1. ⏱️ **Time management**

1. 🔨 **Product development**

1. 🏦 **Financial management**

1. ❤️ **Co-founder relationship**

We will look at every single item in detail, but the premise is: you need to get *all* of them right. This is not a “*most of them*” situation: if you fail badly at any one, that’s probably enough to screw up the entire company.

So let’s dive in!

---

### 📈 Growth process is in place

Startups need to grow — in fact, high-growth is the very definition of startup 👇

> *A startup is a company designed to grow fast. Being newly founded does not in itself make a company a startup. Nor is it necessary for a startup to work on technology, or take venture funding, or have some sort of "exit." The only essential thing is growth. Everything else we associate with startups follows from growth.*

— Paul Graham

At any given time, you may have not figured out *how* to grow just yet, but you need to have a **growth process** in place. This means two things:

- [ ] **You know what growth looks like** — KPIs are well defined and everybody knows them**.**

- [ ] **You do check-ins at an appropriate cadence** — e.g. weekly.

---

### 🔍 Learning process is in place

The #1 reason why startups fail is that they build things that customers don’t want. 

This may happen either at a macro level—you don’t find product-market fit—or micro level—when a product hits a plateau and doesn’t improve anymore.

To counter this, your team needs to become a **learning machine**, and work with the *scientific *method: you observe, make experiments, measure, repeat. You need to make sure that you keep *learning*, and that your knowledge of the problem-solution space never gets stale.

Your team is a learning machine when:

- [ ] **You have a process for regularly talking with customers and getting feedback from them.**

- [ ] **You ship experiments backed by data, measure how they perform, and adjust.**

---

### 🎯 Goals & planning are clear

I am a strong believer in setting goals and creating plans, even in uncertain scenarios, for three main reasons:

1. There is always something you want to achieve: the next round of financing, ramen-profitability, launching a new market, you name it. 

1. Plans are not always useful, but planning almost always is. The act of planning itself makes you better prepared […]

1. It creates alignment and momentum in your team. Goals and plans rally people towards them.

So, force yourself to think about what you need to do to achieve the next step, and write it down for your whole team to know. At any given time, you should have:

- [ ] **Monthly or quarterly goals in place and visible to everyone **—** **anything that is longer than quarterly (not broken down into anything else) is sketchy.

- [ ] **Tracking & check-ins at appropriate cadence **—** **Do you have monthly goals? Check weekly with your team. Quarterly? Check bi-weekly.

### ⏱️ High-leverage time management is in place

You need to be intentional, at a company level, about how people spend their time, and apply what I call **high-leverage time management**.

Here is what you should pay attention to:

#### 1) Minimize waiting

Minimize the time people spend waiting for others to do stuff.

1. More waiting leads to more context switch — people move to other tasks in the meantime

1. More context switch leads to more work in progress — which is detrimental to quality, productivity, and morale.

![Biggest productivity offenders, based on a 2017 survey by Pluralsight](../assets/613df8b1e6b74bbd.png)

But why do people wait for others in the first place? In my experience, it’s mostly two things:

- 🤝 **Collaboration **— things that need contribution from multiple people, like a design doc.

- **🏅 Approvals **— things that need to be reviewed and *signed off *by somebody, like a PR.

To reduce waitings, you can try two things:

- **Make high-bandwidth activities *****sync***** **— for creative tasks that require a lot of back and forth, consider doing them *synchronously* in a meeting, instead of passing them around on Slack or a doc. E.g. create that design doc all together with your team, or do pair programming on that thorny issue.

- **Switch from an *****approval***** workflow, to a *****revert***** one** — default to trust your co-workers to go ahead with their decisions, and review only *after* the fact, not to block them. This does wonders for both productivity and accountability. For example, you can do so with code reviews: see the [ship/show/ask](https://refactoring.fm/i/45862551/consider-the-ship-show-ask-framework).

#### 2) Meetings are short & few

Meetings are the *easy default* to get things done, but easily turn into a *low-leverage* way of spending your time.

I have written about meetings many times, including a full guide about how to reduce them: 

[https://refactoring.fm/p/how-to-remove-meetings](https://refactoring.fm/p/how-to-remove-meetings)

A good rule of thumb for when to use a meeting is to follow the ☕ **CUP Rule**. Use meetings when the matter is:

- **Complex** — we need to brainstorm and figure this out together.

- **Personal** — improve bonding, 1:1s

- **Urgent** — there is an incident happening *right now*

Truth is very few things happen *regularly* that meet any of these criteria. So, be especially suspicious of recurring meetings.

#### 3) You apply continuous delegation

An investor once told me that as a founder I had to continuously replace myself. 

Once I understood how to do something, my job was to delegate or automate such work.

I have found this to be a good mindset for everybody, not just founders. You can ask yourself continuously:

1. **Can I remove this? **—** **Is this really needed?** **You would be surprised by how much work can be safely *removed* and not be replaced by anything. This should always be the first option. 

1. **Can I automate this? **— Automate aggressively any manual work that is not high-leverage. These days, you have plenty of ways to do so: chatOps, internal tools, and workflows are easy to build even without code. 

1. **Can I delegate this? **— Are you the best person to do this?** **Sometimes, what looks like a chore for you, is an opportunity for a younger co-worker to prove themselves. 

Also, the order matters. No work that can be remove should be automated, and no work that can be automated should be delegated.

### 🔨 Product development has high velocity

The best teams ship fast and often, we all know that. The *degree* of such speed may vary with your product stage, company size, and more, but here is an aspirational checklist:

- [ ] **You ship daily** — deploy code to production everyday. It may be a small feature, or an incomplete one gated behind a [feature flag](https://refactoring.fm/p/feature-flags), but do it everyday.

- [ ] **Progress is observed weekly **— something meaningful is shipped every week, and e.g. you have demo time on Friday. Bi-weekly is ok, but weekly is better.

- [ ] **There is little work in progress **— people work best when they do one thing at a time

- [ ] **Engineering prizes speed and value** — your team values cutting down scope, shipping things fast, and delivering value to the customer, as opposed to building tech for its own sake.  

I wrote a full guide about what good continuous delivery looks like 👇

[https://refactoring.fm/p/how-to-create-a-good-release-process](https://refactoring.fm/p/how-to-create-a-good-release-process)

### 🏦 Minimum viable financial mgmt is in place

I have rarely met founders who *liked* financials. Yet, there are some basics you need to get right:

- [ ] **Financial model is in place** — with growth + profit + expense hypotheses.

- [ ] **Plan says you will reach your goal** — this seems obvious, but you will be surprised by how many plans I saw that literally said “*we are going to die*”. So, if your goal is to raise the next round of funding, the plan needs to say you’ll get there. If the goal is to become profitable, you’ll get there as well. 

- [ ] **Check-ins are in place at appropriate cadence **— you track numbers monthly against the plan and ensure you are on track.

- [ ] **Hiring is last resort** — this may be opinionated, but all the best teams I have known consider hiring at the very last option for doing anything. Founders learn how to do the thing first, and replace themselves later (see continuous delegation) to increase quality and unlock their time.

---

### ❤️ Co-founder relationship is good

The relationship between co-founders is special and needs to be nurtured. This is another area that you need to be intentional about to avoid gradual erosion, which can literally kill your company.

- [ ] **Transparency + trust are in place** — you give each other direct, honest feedback, and you talk about everything.

- [ ] **Dedicated time is spent to build trust** — that includes non-work activities, like having dinner, or drinks, together every once in a while. It’s important to spend *non-work* time together to decompress a build a human relationship.

# Interview with Rebecca Murphey

Today's guest is [**Rebecca Murphey**](https://www.linkedin.com/in/rmurphey/)! Rebecca is Field CTO at Swarmia, former manager at Stripe and Indeed, and the author of Build: Elements of an Effective Software Organization.

With Rebecca, we talked about her journey through Indeed, Stripe and Swarmia and the three pillars of her book: business outcomes, developer productivity, and developer experience.

### 🏢 A Developer Productivity Journey ([01:16](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=-cRZOSfE4yA&t=76s))

Rebecca has had a distinguished career, a lot of which was focused on developer productivity. 

Her interest in making processes more efficient started quite early, and became a central theme during her time at Indeed and Stripe:

- **Indeed** — at Indeed, Rebecca was instrumental in creating a team focused on frontend engineering productivity. Her team achieved remarkable gains in delivery efficiency by streamlining processes and building effective tooling to support engineering teams.

- **Stripe** — at Stripe, Rebecca built a JavaScript infrastructure team from the ground up. Later, her role evolved to encompass broader productivity efforts across the company, implementing strategies to reduce bottlenecks and improve the development lifecycle.

---

### 📈 What does a Field CTO do? ([05:49](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=-cRZOSfE4yA&t=349s))

In January 2023, Rebecca joined Swarmia as *Field CTO*, which is a pretty unique role. 

In fact, she focuses more on external engagement and **understanding industry challenges** than on internal technical decisions. She leverages her extensive experience to bridge the gap between Swarmia’s product offering and the real-world needs of engineering teams.

As Field CTO, Rebecca's primary responsibility involves speaking with users and industry leaders, to gather insights. She brings these insights back to Swarmia's product, marketing, and sales teams to ensure their solutions address the real challenges faced by engineering leaders.

This role allows her to apply her technical credibility and extensive background to influence product direction and strategy.

While this makes for an unusual role, it also makes a lot of sense to me, especially for a companies that build developer tools.

### 📚 Writing a book on effective engineering teams ([07:26](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=-cRZOSfE4yA&t=446s))

Rebecca authored the book "Build", which encapsulates her experiences and insights about creating effective software organizations. 

The book focuses on three pillars:

#### 1) **Business Outcomes **🏆

Engineering work should always be aligned with business goals, and engineering leaders should understand what value they are creating for the company.

Rebecca explains that, without clear business objectives, technical work can become misaligned, leading to inefficiencies and wasted resources. That’s what happens in many companies she sees: engineers often work in silos, disconnected from the business value their work creates.

#### 2) **Developer Productivity **🔨

This involves measuring and improving how work *moves* through the system. 

Effective developer productivity metrics help evaluate and enhance the development process, without targeting individual performance. Rebecca explains that focusing on system-level metrics rather than individual performance helps create a healthier, more productive work environment.

#### 3) **Developer Experience **❤️ 

Developer experience focuses on the qualitative aspects of a developer's work environment. 

Rebecca highlights the importance of understanding and addressing engineers’ pain points to improve overall efficiency. These pains often do not surface through *telemetry*, but rather through surveys and good conversations.

### Conway’s Law 🏗️ ([22:57](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=-cRZOSfE4yA&t=1377s))

Rebecca is a fan of the Conway’s Law, which states that organizations produce software systems whose design invariably *mirrors *their teams’ communication design.

Therefore, to improve systems, we should work on communication first.

![image](../assets/6c5e944b184e404f.png)

During her time at Indeed and Stripe, Rebecca worked directly on re-architecting systems + re-organizing teams, which led to significant improvements in productivity and software quality.

### 🛠️ Continuous Improvement ([32:17](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=-cRZOSfE4yA&t=1937s))

Rebecca advocates for using metrics to continuously improve processes.

Setting achievable goals and gradually working towards them helps maintain progress without overwhelming the team.

She underscores the importance of *iterative improvements*, where small, incremental changes lead to substantial long-term benefits. This is akin to what James Clear advocates for in Atomic Habits, which we reviewed earlier this year 👇

[https://refactoring.fm/p/atomic-habits](https://refactoring.fm/p/atomic-habits)

Small changes, backed by real-world feedback and performance data, give teams room to evolve sustainably.

---

### 🧑‍💻 Developer Experience vs Developer Productivity ([38:33](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=-cRZOSfE4yA&t=2313s))

While developer productivity and experience are closely linked, they address different aspects of the development process:

- **Productivity** — focuses on system efficiency, measuring how work moves through the development pipeline. 

- **Experience** — centers on the qualitative aspects that impact a developer's daily work, such as tooling, environment, and overall job satisfaction. 

Rebecca highlights that improving developer experience often leads to enhanced productivity, as happier developers are typically more engaged and effective.

### **1) 🧠 Coaching vs. mentoring (**[**05:22**](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=jTmIxd4EMmM&t=322s)**)**

Joel begins by making an important distinction between mentoring and coaching – two approaches often confused but fundamentally different in their philosophy and execution.

Mentoring is based on learning from someone with different experiences than yours, leveraging their knowledge to accelerate your learning. In contrast, coaching starts from the assumption that you're already capable and resourceful.

> ***"Coaching comes from a place of assuming that you're a capable and resourceful person. And therefore, most of the answers to your challenges are already in your head somewhere. They just need help getting out."***

As a coach, Joel creates a thinking partnership to help CTOs become clearer about themselves and their situations, challenge self-limiting beliefs, and examine available options for change.

- **🤝 Trust-based partnership** — Unlike mentoring, coaching isn't about giving advice but helping clients discover their own answers.

- **🔍 Self-discovery process** — The coach guides clients to uncover solutions that are already within them, making outcomes more sustainable.

- **🌱 External perspective** — Senior leaders like CTOs often lack peers within their organization who can provide unbiased support without evaluation.

### **2) 👥 Individual vs. group coaching (**[**10:10**](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=jTmIxd4EMmM&t=610s)**)**

Joel outlines the key differences between one-on-one coaching and group coaching, each offering distinct benefits for CTOs. One-on-one coaching provides personalized attention and deep focus on individual needs:

> ***"One-to-one coaching where you have your own coach and you have individual sessions with that coach on a regular basis, enable you to focus on what you need at the pace you need."***

Group coaching, like the masterminds Joel facilitates for Refactoring, offers different advantages:

- **🔄 Shared experiences** — Regular meetings with peers remind CTOs they're not alone in their challenges.

- **🌐 Multiple perspectives** — Group members bring diverse insights that wouldn't emerge in one-on-one sessions.

- **💰 Resource efficiency** — Group coaching requires less budget with a different but valuable intensity level.

Joel typically runs groups of 6-7 CTOs who meet monthly for a year, with each session focusing on challenges presented by individual members. His role shifts to that of a facilitator rather than a direct coach, helping the group have productive coaching conversations with one another.

### **3) 🏢 The CTO's relationship with the executive team (**[**21:44**](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=jTmIxd4EMmM&t=1304s)**)**

One of the biggest challenges Joel sees CTOs face is understanding their role within the executive team. Many early-career CTOs struggle with a fundamental mindset shift: seeing themselves primarily as members of the executive team rather than as representatives of the engineering team.

> ***"It's not that I'm, you know, my primary team is the engineering team and I'm there to support them and fight their battles in the leadership team. It's more that I'm a member of the leadership team. My engineering or technology team is my secondary team."***

This shift transforms how CTOs approach their relationships with other executives:

- 🏢** Organization-first mindset** — Successful CTOs prioritize company success over engineering team advocacy.

- 🤝** Build executive relationships** — They invest time in understanding each executive's challenges and priorities.

- 🌱** Cultivate trust** — They demonstrate reliability, competence, and sincere care about the company's overall success.

When this shift doesn't happen, the effects ripple through the organization: teams begin mirroring executive conflicts, silos form, and the "us vs. them" mentality prevents effective collaboration across departments.

### **4) 💬 Advocating for technical work (**[**25:44**](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=jTmIxd4EMmM&t=1544s)**)**

Advocating for technical investments like addressing technical debt is a perennial challenge for CTOs. Joel shares insights on how to approach these difficult conversations with other executives.

The foundation for successful advocacy is building trust with the executive team:

> ***"I think this is something I underestimated when I was early in my career. I thought if I bring the right logical arguments, everyone will agree with me and will make the decision I need. But it's very hard for people to listen to you if they don't trust you."***

Once trust is established, successful advocacy requires:

- **🔍 Priority framing** — Rather than presenting "good ideas," focus on why this work needs to happen now.

- **👓 CEO perspective** — Ask yourself, "If I were the CEO, why would I choose to prioritize this over other initiatives?"

- **📈 ROI articulation** — Frame technical debt work in terms of future acceleration rather than developer happiness.

Joel emphasizes being selective about what you bring to the executive team. Many small technical improvements don't need executive buy-in if trust has already been established. The key is recognizing which decisions truly require leadership involvement.

### **5) 🔄 Delegation and team development (**[**39:14**](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=jTmIxd4EMmM&t=2354s)**)**

As a CTO, there's always more work than time available. Joel offers practical advice on delegation and team development.

First, accept that you'll never complete everything – you will inevitably drop things every week. The important part is consciously choosing what gets dropped rather than letting circumstances decide.

> ***"What is it that you've got that your team don't have that means you're the only person who can do this? And how are you going to bridge that gap? How are you going to support them to make that change?"***

Joel shares key principles for effective delegation:

- **🎯 Intentional prioritization** — Deliberately choose what to keep, delegate, or drop, rather than letting circumstances decide.

- **🌱 Development mindset** — View delegation as an opportunity to grow your team for the future, not just manage your workload.

- **📊 Task-relevant matching** — Apply Andy Grove's concept of "task-relevant maturity" to provide appropriate support based on experience with specific tasks.

Through careful delegation and intentional team development, CTOs can both manage their overwhelming workload and build a stronger organization for the future.

Software development continuously reinvents itself. Practices, languages and frameworks all change at a fast pace.

This is less true, historically, for the development cycle itself. When it comes to how we work together, there hasn’t been much progress over classic frameworks like Scrum or Kanban, which are now 20+ years old.

In the past couple of years, though, something has changed. 

The shift to remote shuffled the cards of how engineering teams work, suddenly turning it into a prime-time topic for discussion. Managers are questioning what makes their teams productive and are willing to experiment.

There is a feeling of reinvention and *nothing is sacred anymore*, which led many teams to reflect on their practices, like:

- *Do we really need daily stand-ups?*

- *Are two weeks cycles long enough to deliver value?*

- *Should we work in cycles — at all?*

This article tries to bridge the old with the new. We will address common criticism to traditional agile practices and discuss popular, sometimes wild, but always interesting, alternatives. Finally, we will figure out how to evaluate all this advice to create a process that fits your team.

We will cover:

- 🔄 **The role of processes** — why do we even bother?

- ⚖️ **Criticism of Scrum** — the good, the bad, and the ugly.

- 🚪 **Alternatives to Scrum** — classic, new, and wild ones.

- 🔨 **How to create your process** — mental models to make the right calls.

- 📚 **Readings** — case studies and resources to learn more.

Let’s dive in 👇

### 🔄 The role of processes

A process is a series of actions or steps taken in order to achieve a particular end.

In the context of an engineering team, the dev process is meant to enforce a way of working that guarantees some qualities.

Processes can only go so far, though.

As Cate Hudson wrote in her excellent piece about [process cultures](https://cate.blog/2022/02/28/low-process-culture-high-process-culture/), processes only guarantee **adequacy**, rather than excellence. They are a mechanism of standardization and enforcement, and there is no way to enforce **greatness**.

We can just enforce adequacy, and we should be cognizant of this.

True greatness comes from people — as Will Larson points out: “*With the right people, any process works, and with the wrong people, no process works*”.

So, the best processes get two things right:

1. **Enforce the basics** — to guarantee **adequacy*** *and relieve people from decision fatigue.

1. **Stay out of the way** — to let people shine and create **greatness**.

How does Scrum fares on this? Well, it’s complicated 👇

### ⚖️ Criticism to Scrum

Scrum gets a lot of flak for many things these days. Most of it, though, revolves around **overhead**.

To many, Scrum feels too *constraining*, so teams either give up parts of it, or move to a different process entirely. Basically, while it successfully *enforces the basics*, it fails at *staying out of the way*.

Why is it so? 

Scrum is designed around a single, powerful idea: work in short cycles. **Sprints*** *are the central unit of work, so all events and ceremonies are scheduled on a Sprint basis. And there are plenty of them:

- **Planning** → to lay out the work to be performed during the Sprint. At the beginning of each Sprint.

- **Review** → to inspect the Sprint outcome and sign it off. At the end of each Sprint

- **Retrospective** → to plan ways to improve the process. At the end of each Sprint.

- **Daily Scrum** → for coordination and to inspect progress. Everyday.

These are all legit activities. Every team needs to perform them in a way or another, whatever framework they choose. Anchoring them to the Sprint duration, though, doesn’t always feel right.

Whatever Sprint duration you choose, you may want to do something *less often, *and something *more often *than that.

Let’s take a bi-weekly Sprint as an example. Here is some common criticism from startups 👇

- **Planning needs more time **— “*to deliver valuable product features we need longer cycles of planning*”.

- **Review should not happen at the end** — “*we review work continuously and need to release multiple times a day*”.

- **Retrospective is shallow **— “*two weeks are not enough to discover meaningful insights about the process*”.

- **Daily Scrum is a burden** — “*most of the times coordination is easy and the ceremony feels like wasted energy”*

These are not absolute pitfalls: any of these may or may not apply to you.

The central point, though, is that for many teams Scrum manages to be *too much* and *not enough* at the same time.

So, what’s the alternative?

### 🚪 Alternatives to Scrum

Popular alternatives to Scrum often involve a 1) longer cycle for planning and retrospective, and 2) a less prescriptive process for the day-to-day operations. 

Here are some examples 👇

#### 🎯 OKRs + Frequent check-ins

The main planning happens on a 2-3 months timespan (e.g. quarterly) with OKRs or similar methods. There are *check-ins *on shorter timeframes, but the dev process is fluid and doesn’t revolve around isolated batches of work that happen within a sprint.

OKRs are compatible with Scrum — but many decide to go lighter with the weekly ceremonies after you do a great job on the wider planning. 

[Kishan Sampat](https://www.linkedin.com/in/kishan-sampat/), Engineering Manager at Dimagi, runs quarterly OKRs and monthly check-ins 👇

> *We observed that two-week sprints were no longer relevant. We needed more time for an output. [...]

We run a monthly “sprint”, but the sprint tasks are not engraved in stone for the month. We usually have a lot of tasks that are carried over into the next month (sprint). Instead of using the sprint as a hard deadline to finish a task by, we use the cycle as a measure of a check-in.

At the end of the quarter, we evaluate by scoring the OKRs. OKRs are driven by strategic objectives set at a larger level.*

#### 🧱 Shape Up

[Shape up](https://basecamp.com/shapeup/webbook) is a framework created by the Basecamp team that revolves around 6-week cycles for product dev, followed by 2 weeks of cool-down for planning and maintenance.

This is how [Michael Genesini](https://www.linkedin.com/in/michaelgenesini), Engineering Manager at ProntoPro, has been working with Shape up for 2 years 👇

> *– we work in *[*6 weeks building cycles*](https://basecamp.com/shapeup/2.2-chapter-08#six-week-cycles)***
****– every building cycle is preceded by a *[*shaping cycle*](https://basecamp.com/shapeup/1.1-chapter-02)* where PM and Leads/Seniors prepare the projects
– at the end of every cycle, we have the *[*circuit breaker*](https://basecamp.com/shapeup/2.2-chapter-08#the-circuit-breaker)* (this enforce to close every project, even in an MVP sort of way)
– we have ad-hoc cross-functional squads that EMs assemble every end of the cycle (fostering knowledge sharing and team collaboration)
– we have champions for every activity, the champion is one engineer that takes ownership of guiding the feature during the cycle
– we have only 3 sizes for projects: tiny bite (1 week), small bite (3 weeks), and big bite (6 weeks). This helps measure the workload when we assemble the ad-hoc squads
– every cool-down (2 weeks after the 6 weeks) we run retrospectives for each squad
– during the cycles, the squads are autonomous also in the process to follow*

#### ✈️ Flights

[Flights](https://simonhoiberg.medium.com/the-flight-manual-9e1aedd04fbf) is a *ceremoniless* framework proposed by Simon Høiberg. It is a *continuous *framework, rather than *iterative*, and it is based on the notion of single *flights *(i.e. deliverables).

Each flight is created with a set of properties that ensure it can be delivered safely.

#### 🧟 Zombie Scrum

I made up this term for all those teams who take Scrum and use ~70% of it, adapting the rest to their workflow.

Some common tweaks are:

- Replace **daily scrum** with something lighter / async. We [did this ourselves](https://refactoring.fm/p/standups?s=w).

- **Release** work continuously instead than only at the end of the Sprint.

- Have fewer **retrospectives** — e.g. every quarter instead than every Sprint.

Sometimes the opposite happens too: people *believe* they hate Scrum and resort to implement their own process, but end up basically reimplementing Scrum. I call this **undercover Scrum**.

Yurii Mykytyn, Director of Engineering at Rebbix, also commented on this 👇

> *Many people who try to do Scrum didn't even bother to read the *[*Scrum guide*](https://scrumguides.org/scrum-guide.html)*, and when they read it they find that the guide itself doesn't tell them to do things which people usually hate Scrum for.*

### 🔨 How to create your own process

There isn’t a one-size-fits-all answer about the right process for your team and I tend to be skeptical even of broad generalizations like: “*X is better for startups while Y is better for large corps*”.

Here are a few mental models you may adopt to reflect on your own process 👇

#### ⚖️ **ROI of ceremonies**

Try to figure out the return on investment of your ceremonies. As [Louis Bennett](https://twitter.com/louisbennett) from Reforge says 👇

> *If a team’s ceremonies create more value than the opportunity cost of its attendees’ time, then the ceremonies are arguably not overhead. And… the converse is also true.*

In my experience this can be hard to assess, because in most ceremonies the cost component is constant, while value is not.

Example: I may find that daily standups are 80% of the times not worth the hassle. But roughly once a week you may have one where you discover a crucial roadblock, and that possibly repays the cost of all the other standups.

Same goes with retros, 1:1s and other processes that look unnecessary until they don't.

As humans we are bad at assessing situations where the value distribution is basically a power law. We only get better with experience.

![image](../assets/57ce6ca91244481a.png)

#### 🎽 **Team’s stage**

Bruce Tuckman described [the four stages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tuckman%27s_stages_of_group_development) of team development, over which a team gradually performs better.

The best process looks different based on your team stage. Teams in earlier stages may need a more prescriptive approach, while those in later stages may be kept more autonomous.

#### ⏱️ **Change over time**

The best approach changes over time. Other than the team’s stage above, processes are influenced by the evolution of your product, the size of your company, personal qualities of people in the team, and more.

Instead of creating an endless list of recipes, focus on setting up a feedback loop that allows you to figure out what works, what doesn’t, and adjust. 

#### ➰ **Cycles are good**

If you think cycles bring too much overhead, probably you are just working with cycles of the wrong duration.

Cycles enable improvement because allow to create feedback loops — they lead to retrospectives, celebrations, and all kinds of healthy habits. 

Recurring ceremonies are also crucial for managers to have reliable spots in which to take action. Without them, you would either 1) end up not working enough on problems, or 2) need to be on the alert every time, which is taxing.

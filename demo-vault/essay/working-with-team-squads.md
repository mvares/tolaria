# Working with Team Squads 🪖

As a team or department leader, one of the most gratifying experiences is watching your team grow. What starts as leading a handful of people can, almost imperceptibly, evolve into managing multiple expanding teams. But with growth comes a unique set of challenges.

Over the past decade, I've navigated this journey firsthand. From restructuring teams and implementing new processes to balancing innovation with maintenance, I've experienced the full spectrum of scaling pains. Eventually, I found myself at the helm of an entire department, proud of our established culture and processes that consistently delivered high performance and great products.

However, success breeds its own challenges. As our existing products required more maintenance, we faced a common dilemma: how to balance upkeep with innovation.

The natural inclination is to prioritize maintaining what works, but this approach comes with two significant downsides:

1. 🏃‍♂️ In the fast-paced world of technology, staying current and responsive to market changes is crucial.

1. 🎨 High-performing, product-oriented teams often lose motivation when innovation takes a backseat to maintenance.

About a year ago, I found myself at this exact crossroads.

We had a great team, both culturally and performance-wise, with successful products used by thousands of customers. But we were spending most of our time on maintenance, leaving little room for new features or products.

I knew it was time to shake things up.

I'd always been intrigued by the Spotify Squad model, and at the beginning of 2024, I decided to implement it with my teams. The results were impressive, and I'm excited to share our experience with you.

In this article, we'll explore:

- **🎵 The Spotify Squad model** — How it works and what are the main blocks

- **♻️ Benefit and Challenges** — What are the pros and cons of this approach

- **🚀 Getting started with Squads** — Essential prerequisites

- **🔧 How to work with Team Squads** — Implementing the model effectively

Let's dive in and discover how the Squad model can revitalize your team and reignite innovation!

### 🎵 The Spotify Model

Team squads are a concept made popular by Spotify due to their innovative approach to agile development.

This approach was first detailed in two videos published on the Spotify Engineering Blog in 2014 ([part 1](https://engineering.atspotify.com/2014/03/spotify-engineering-culture-part-1/), [part 2](https://engineering.atspotify.com/2014/09/spotify-engineering-culture-part-2/)).

I still remember being amazed by many aspects of this culture when I watched them. However, even though I drew much inspiration from their cultural practices, I never had the chance to experiment with the model firsthand.

The idea was to create mini-teams, each with the autonomy to manage their own projects as if they were a self-contained startup.

#### 1) Squads 🎽

A Squad is the basic unit in this model, similar to a small startup team.

![image](../assets/10abdf02815c8061.png)

Each squad usually has the following characteristics:

- 👥 **Optimal-sized** — typically, squads consist of less than 8 people, a size that supports agility and effective collaboration without becoming unmanageable.

- 🎯 **Focused **— each squad operates with a clear, focused mission, aligning their efforts to specific company goals or product areas.

- 🤝 **Cross-functional** — squads are composed of members from various disciplines like development, design, product management, and QA, enabling them to operate independently.

- 🚀 **Autonomous** — squads have the autonomy to decide how they work, which technologies to use, and how to achieve their objectives best.

- 👑 **Accountable** — each squad owns the results of their work, fostering a sense of responsibility and accountability among its members.

- ⏱ **Fast** — the small size and self-sufficiency of squads allow for quick decision-making and rapid execution of tasks.

Over the years, Spotify's number of squads has consistently grown, leading to the introduction of more forms of structure. While these substructures were irrelevant to my experiment, I will briefly describe them for the sake of completeness.

#### 2) Tribes 👥

A Tribe is a collection of squads working in related areas, often within the same business domain or feature set.

The primary role of a tribe is to provide support and facilitate collaboration among its squads.

![image](../assets/754023b3f04f4031.png)

Tribes help maintain alignment across squads so that they do not drift into conflicting directions even though each operates independently.

A tribe is led by a Tribe Lead, who focuses on providing the best environment for squads to succeed, rather than managing the specifics of projects.

#### 3) Chapters 📙

Within tribes, individuals from different squads who perform similar roles (such as backend developers, testers, or product managers) are grouped into Chapters.

A chapter is a way to organize the competency areas across the squads.

![image](../assets/f9292cc05aa5450d.png)

Each chapter has a leader, usually an experienced individual in the specific field, who is responsible for the growth and development of the members, even though they work in different squads.

#### 4) Guilds 🪄

Guilds are interest groups anyone in the company can join, regardless of their tribe or squad affiliation.

Guilds span the whole company and are organized around topics like web development, user experience, or data science.

![image](../assets/10abdf02815c80c3.png)

They are informal communities for sharing knowledge, tools, and practices and initiating wider discussions about these topics.

### ♻️ Squads benefits and challenges

The Spotify model has been extensively discussed, including [some significant criticisms](https://www.jeremiahlee.com/posts/failed-squad-goals/) from former employees.

Based on my research, conversations with individuals who have firsthand experience with this model, and my own recent experience, I believe this approach can be a valuable asset if used wisely, particularly for projects that require a high degree of focus.

Some of the key benefits I have observed firsthand include:

- 🚀 **Enhanced Autonomy** — One of the standout advantages is the increased autonomy it provides. Squads are empowered with decision-making authority and ownership, which not only fosters a sense of responsibility but also enhances engagement.

- 🐝 **Cross-pollination** — Another benefit is the cross-pollination of ideas and knowledge. By bringing together people from different backgrounds and functions, the model facilitates both personal and professional growth.

- 🗣️ **Soft-skills Growth** — Finally, the structure naturally promotes the development of soft skills. Small team environments encourage individuals to improve their interpersonal abilities, from communication to conflict resolution.

Although I haven’t encountered any major issues during my year of experimenting with squads, there are some aspects you should be mindful of if you decide to adopt this approach:

- 🛡️ **Dependence on strong leadership** — The success of the model heavily depends on leadership. It requires leaders who can guide and coach squads effectively without resorting to micromanagement, which can be a delicate balance to maintain.

- 🚫 **Cultural fit** — Additionally, the model’s success relies on a strong cultural fit. While it works well in some organizations, it requires a supportive company culture and may not align with every workplace.

- 🛠️ **Challenges in implementation** — Another challenge lies in the implementation. While the concept sounds appealing, the lack of strict guidelines can make it difficult to execute (more on that later!), particularly in more traditional companies that are used to rigid structures

### 🚀 Getting Started with Squads

Before diving into how to practically work with a squad, I believe there are some requirements that your team must satisfy.

Over the past year, I've reflected extensively on these and summarized them in a 8-item checklist. I believe this checklist could be valuable if you're willing to experiment with the squad model.

Before starting to experiment, I suggest assessing these requirements objectively. If you feel your team isn't adequately prepared in some areas, focus on improving those aspects first.

#### 1) Independence 🦅

Squads thrive on autonomy.

Your team members should be capable of working independently, making decisions, and taking ownership of their tasks. This doesn't mean they work in isolation, but rather that they can function effectively without constant supervision.

**TIPS 💡**

- Implement a "trust by default" policy where team members are given autonomy from the start

- Encourage decision-making at the lowest possible level within the squad

- Provide clear guidelines and boundaries within which squads can operate independently

#### 2) Ownership 🏆

Each squad member should feel a sense of ownership over their work and the squad's mission. This ownership mentality drives motivation and encourages proactive problem-solving.

**TIPS 💡**

- Assign end-to-end responsibility for features or products to squads

- Implement a "you build it, you run it" mentality for all squad deliverables

- Recognize and reward squad members who demonstrate strong ownership behaviors

#### 3) Product-minded team 🧠

Your team should have a strong product orientation. They should understand not just how to build features, but why those features matter to the end-user.

**TIPS 💡**

- Conduct regular user research sessions involving all squad members

- Use techniques like "Jobs to be Done" to help squads understand user needs

- Encourage squad members to use the product regularly and provide feedback

#### 4) Cross-functional skills 🛠️

Ideally, your squad should have all the skills necessary to take an idea from concept to production. This might include developers, designers, QA specialists, and a product owner.

**TIPS 💡**

- Ensure each squad has a mix of developers, designers, QA specialists, and a product people

- Provide opportunities for skill-sharing within the squad to increase versatility

- Consider rotating roles within the squad to build empathy and understanding

#### 5) Agile mindset 🔄

The squad model works best when team members are comfortable with agile methodologies. They should be open to iterative development, frequent feedback, and continuous improvement.

**TIPS 💡**

- Provide training on agile principles and methodologies to all squad members

- Use the Agile Fluency Model to assess and improve your team's agile capabilities

- Encourage squads to experiment with different agile practices and find what works best for them

#### 6) Shared understanding of goals 🥅

All squad members should have a clear understanding of the squad's mission and how it aligns with broader organizational objectives.

**TIPS 💡**

- Use the OKR framework to align squad goals with company objectives

- Conduct regular squad meetings to discuss and reinforce the squad's mission

- Create visual representations of squad goals and display them prominently

#### 7) Psychological safety 🛡️

Squad members need to feel safe to take risks, voice their opinions, and make mistakes. This is crucial for fostering innovation and continuous improvement.

**TIPS 💡**

- Implement a "no blame" culture where failures are seen as learning opportunities

- Encourage open and honest feedback within the squad

- Conduct regular anonymous surveys to gauge the level of psychological safety

#### 8) Leadership support 👥

The transition to a squad model requires strong support from leadership. Leaders need to be willing to delegate authority and trust in the squad's ability to deliver.

**TIPS 💡**

- Provide leadership training on how to support and enable autonomous squads

- Encourage leaders to act as servant leaders, removing obstacles for squads

- Ensure leaders regularly communicate the vision and importance of the squad model

### 🔧 How to work with team squads

Having all the prerequisites mentioned above is already a big step into succeeding with the squad model, but starting to adopt this model for real projects or production features is a different thing.

Taking inspiration from big techs like Spotify can be beneficial, but it's always important to understand your context and try to adapt existing methodologies to it.

For instance, it's probably unlikely that you will need to recreate the same structure described before. Unless you are trying to apply this model to an entire company, you won't probably need to think about tribes, chapters, and guilds.

Moreover, working with squads could be a huge change for your team, so my suggestion is to approach it safely, trying with small objectives and then, if it works, extend it to wider scopes.

So let's see now all the steps to start using this model effectively.

#### 1) Define clear missions 🎯

Each squad needs a clear, focused mission that aligns with broader organizational goals. This mission should be specific enough to guide day-to-day work, but broad enough to allow for creativity and innovation.

If it's the first time you experiment with the squads model, I suggest you start from something easy. A small feature for your product, a POC, or something where you can limit the impact in case of failure.

When I first experimented with squads this year, I dedicated a small group of people to a small feature, while the rest of the team continued with the more traditional approach to product development.

#### 2) Nominate a squad leader 👑

While the squad should be a small and flat team, it's important to have a single point of contact when you need information about the progress and also to have someone coordinating all the activities related to the squad, leading rituals, etc.

From my direct experience, this is a great opportunity for people in your team who want to try the leadership/management experience, which usually is not an easy thing in smaller teams.

#### 3) Build the squad 🧩

Together with the newly nominated squad leader, it's crucial to have the right skills to progress and succeed with the project the squad is assigned to.

For this reason, before everything you need to assess the project you want to assign the squad to and define the skills needed. Also, always ensure someone from product is present in the squad.

Something I learned using the squad approach is that skills required for the project are not everything. For example, you might want to assign someone to a specific squad because you want this person to improve with some skills or align them with the base knowledge.

#### 4) Define squad rules 📜

After the squad has been formed and a leader has been chosen, it's important to define squad rules. While you can have some guidelines, in my opinion it's important to leave the squad to set their own rules.

Every squad could have different rules and a different way of working, but it would be beneficial to document everything so future squads could take inspiration from the previous ones.

#### 5) Establish squad rituals 🔄

While every squad has its own rules, it's important to establish regular touchpoints for squads to sync, plan, and reflect.

These might include:

- Daily stand-ups

- Sprint planning sessions

- Retrospectives

- Demo days

Especially if, as described before, you adopted a hybrid model where you only dedicated a small group to a project, having regular demo days is a great way to keep the rest of the team involved and in touch with the squad.

#### 6) Establish communication channels 📢

A squad can go at different speeds compared to the rest of the team, so effective communication is crucial for its success and to ensure alignment.

Establish clear channels for intra-squad communication and inter-squad collaboration:

- Set up dedicated communication channels (e.g., Slack) for each squad

- Implement a shared knowledge base for documentation and best practices

- Schedule regular sync-ups to ensure alignment and knowledge sharing

#### 7) Do regular health checks, but don't micro-manage 🩺

Especially at the beginning of your journey with the squad model, it's important to have regular health checks to assess the health of your squads.

Are they still aligned with their mission? Are they facing any obstacles?

Pay attention not to try to micro-manage the squad.

#### 8) Adapt and evolve 🐛

Remember, the squad model is not a one-size-fits-all solution. Be prepared to adapt it to your organization's unique needs and culture. In my experience, this flexibility is crucial for long-term success.

When I first implemented the squad model, I quickly realized that what works for Spotify or other tech giants might not work perfectly for us. Our team had its own dynamics, strengths, and challenges that required a tailored approach.

Here are some ways you can adapt and evolve your squad model:

- 🔄 **Regular retrospectives **— Hold monthly or quarterly sessions to discuss what's working and what isn't. Be open to feedback from all squad members.

- 🧩 **Experiment with squad size **— I found that while Spotify recommends 6-8 members, our most effective squads sometimes had 4-5 people. Don't be afraid to adjust based on project needs and team dynamics.

- 🎭 **Flexible roles —** Encourage squad members to step outside their comfort zones. In one of our squads, a backend developer volunteered to help with frontend tasks, not only solving our resource crunch but also expanding their skill set. This flexibility can lead to more well-rounded team members and smoother project execution.

- ⏰ **Adapt rituals **— Some of our squads preferred daily standups, while others worked better with less frequent check-ins. Allow each squad to find its rhythm.

---

As I've experienced firsthand, following these steps and continuously refining your approach can help you harness the full potential of the squad model. In my journey, I've seen it drive innovation, boost productivity, and create a more engaging work environment for my teams.
Remember, transitioning to a squad model is a journey, not a destination - I'm still learning and adapting every day. It requires patience, flexibility, and a commitment to continuous improvement. But I can tell you from personal experience, with the right approach, it can truly transform your organization. I've watched my own workplace become more agile, innovative, and employee-centric, and it's been incredibly rewarding.
I encourage you to give it a try. You might face challenges along the way, but the benefits are well worth it. Good luck on your squad journey!

# Salary Reviews

### ✍️ Post

If I had to name one of the most challenging things I had to deal with in my career as an Engineering Manager and Leader, I'd probably say **talking about money**.

That includes:

- Negotiating your yearly budget

- Deciding how much to pay people

- Regularly review compensations

In fact, if not handled well, all these processes can have a tremendous impact on your team productivity, morale, retention, and of course, on your career as an EM.

While these three processes are all connected one to another, in today’s article I'd like to focus on compensations, and in particular on **how to deal with salary reviews**.

That’s because, despite its importance, discussing money is often still a taboo, or at least a delicate topic, in many engineering teams. 

Conversely, in my experience, bringing transparency to salary discussions is incredibly helpful to fostering a positive and healthy work environment.

So, in today's article, here is what we are going to cover:

- 🙋‍♂️ **What is a salary review**

- 🔬 **How to prepare for a salary review**

- 🔧 **How to perform a salary review**

- 📚 **How other companies do salary reviews**

Let's dive in!

### 🙋‍♂️ What is a Salary Review?

A salary review is a structured process to evaluate and **adjust employee compensation**.

The main purposes of salary reviews are:

- 📈 **Aligning with Market **— ensuring that employee compensation is competitive with industry standards and market conditions.

- 🌟 **Rewarding Performance** — recognizing and financially rewarding employees for their achievements, hard work, and contributions to the organization.

- ⚖️ **Ensuring internal equity** — maintaining fairness and consistency in pay among team members to promote a sense of equity and prevent dissatisfaction.

The ownership of the salary review process can vary depending on the organization’s size, structure, and culture.

However, the following stakeholders are typically involved in the salary review process:

#### **1) Human Resources (HR) Department **🏢

- **Ownership** — HR usually oversees the whole process. They establish guidelines, provide market salary data, and ensure the process aligns with company policies and legal requirements.

- **Coordination** — HR coordinates with various departments to gather necessary data and ensures consistency and fairness in salary adjustments across the organization.

#### 2) **Engineering Managers and Team Leads **🧑‍💻

- **Implementation **— EMs and team leads play a critical role in *implementing* the salary review process. They gather *performance data*, provide input on individual contributions, and make recommendations for salary adjustments.

- **Communication** — EMs are responsible for communicating the outcomes of the salary reviews to their team members, providing feedback, and addressing any concerns.

#### 3) **Senior Management and Executives **🧑‍💼

- **Approval** — senior management and executives often have the *final approval* on budget allocations for salary adjustments. They ensure that the salary review process aligns with the company's strategic goals and financial constraints.

- **Policy Setting** — they may also set high-level policies and objectives for the salary review process, such as target compensation percentiles and overall compensation strategy.

#### 4) **Finance Department **💰

- **Budget Management** — finally, the finance department ensures that the salary adjustments fit within the overall budget. They provide financial oversight and help balance compensation increases with other financial priorities.

### 🔬 Prepare for the Salary Review

You might think that a salary review is just a *negotiation* between you and your team members to decide if and how they should have their salary increased. 

Instead, there's much more involved.

Before you even start negotiating, there's a good amount of work you should do, which you can divide into three main phases:

- 🔍 **Market Research**

- 📊 **Performance Data Collection**

- 🧮 **Budget Planning**

Let's see all these step more in detail.

#### 1) Market Research 🔍

A critical starting point for salary reviews is understanding what constitutes a fair salary for your team members. This can change based on many factors and one of the most important, is market.

Market is constantly moving and based on the demand of specific roles, average salary can change year over year. That's why it becomes crucial to have regular benchmarks to ensure your salaries are competitive enough to retain people.

Depending on your company’s size and structure, this research can be performed by:

- 👥 **The HR Team** — conducts comprehensive and continuous market salary surveys and benchmarking to ensure competitive compensation.

- 🧑‍💼 **The Engineering Manager** — while it's a time-consuming activity, if the company structure and size don't include an organized HR department, you might have to gather industry data and insights specific to your team to make informed salary decisions.

In case you have to take care of market research, these are some of the best websites to gather salary data:

- [Payscale](https://www.payscale.com/research/)

- [Levels.fyi](https://www.levels.fyi/)

- [Glassdoor](https://www.glassdoor.com/Salary/)

All these sites can give you a good range that you can use as a base when you negotiate salary raises with the upper management or the finance team.

#### 2) Performance Data Collection 📊

Over the past decade, there has been ongoing debate about the connection between performance reviews and salary raises. 

Some advocate that performance should be the sole driver for salary increases, while others believe performance reviews and salary raises should remain separate.

[Aidan Curley](https://www.linkedin.com/in/aidan-c-2359a1203/), Engineering Manager, shared his perspective on the question, "What elements do you consider when negotiating salaries with your team members?"

> *Performance. Of course, affordability is the controlling factor of the size of your team’s allocation, but performance is the driver of what % of the allocation each IC receives.*

As is often the case, there is no one-size-fits-all solution, and decoupling performance from salary in my opinion can be challenging. The most crucial aspect is how performance is measured and the clarity provided around the overall process.

I usually rely on the following principles:

- 💰 **Consistent Raises** — if the budget allows, I aim to give a minimum yearly raise to every team member, including those with average performance. Of course, there may be years when this minimum raise cannot be met.

- 🎯 **Encouraging Improvement** — if minimum performance standards are not met, I may delay or forgo the salary raise. However, I avoid using this as a punishment and prefer to use raises as an incentive for improvement.

- 🌟 **Rewarding Excellence** — for exceptional performers, I aim to provide a bonus in addition to the minimum yearly raise.

- 🗣️ **Seeking Feedback** — I never rely solely on my judgment. I seek feedback from colleagues who have worked closely with the person being reviewed.

By following these principles, I strive to create a fair and motivating environment for my team.

#### 3) Budget Planning 🧮

Once you have a clear understanding of fair compensation for your team, it’s time to plan your budget. 

This step varies based on your company’s size, reporting structure, and your specific role, but you will likely need to develop a plan that outlines all the raises you intend to give in the next year.

In this phase, consider the following important factors:

- 💰 **Financial Constraints** — understand the overall budget limitations and financial health of the company.

- 🔄 **Competition **—** ** keep up with industry salary trends to ensure competitiveness.

- 💡 **Strategic Priorities** — consider the company’s strategic goals and how salary adjustments can support these objectives.

- 🌟 **Employee Retention** — factor in the importance of retaining top talent and how competitive salaries can aid in this effort.

- 🏢 **Departmental Needs** — evaluate the specific needs and contributions of different departments to allocate raises appropriately.

Another thing I usually do when I prepare my yearly budget is to be very specific and include the reasons behind my planned salary raises. This helps significantly when negotiating the budget with upper management and the finance department.

### 🔧 Perform the Review

Once you have gathered all the necessary data and completed your preparatory work, you are ready to move forward with the actual review process.

This phase is crucial as it directly impacts employee satisfaction, retention, and overall team morale.

So let’s look at what you should take into account:

#### 1) Frequency ⏱️

The first thing to establish is the frequency of salary reviews.

Most companies, including mine, conduct salary reviews **once a year**. However, in larger organizations, this process can occur even *twice a year*.

Setting a regular cadence helps ensure consistency and allows employees to anticipate and prepare for their reviews.

#### 2) How much should you raise? 📈

This is one of the most frequent questions I receive, and the truth is that there's no one-size-fits-all answer. The appropriate raise depends on many factors, many of which are part of the preparation work:

- 💰 **Budget** — the overall financial constraints and allowances negotiated with upper management.

- 📊 **Market** — current market salary trends and benchmarks for similar roles. This may vary, and sometimes aligning with market rates is essential to retain your talent.

- 🌟 **Performance** — based on the data and feedback collected, rewarding high-performing team members is crucial.

- 📈 **Role and Level** — salaries cannot grow exponentially, especially for higher-level roles where it becomes challenging to apply the same percentile increases.

- 🎯 **Strategic Value** — depending on the strategic importance of certain team members, increasing their salaries might be necessary to ensure retention.

Each year, I define a minimum raise percentile and, based on these elements, determine the appropriate raise for each team member.

#### 3) Communication 💬

Beyond the numbers, one of the most critical aspects of the salary review process is communication. 

In my experience, the biggest cause of frustration is not unmet expectations regarding raises, but rather a **lack of communication**.

Effective communication in the salary review process includes:

- 📄 **Documentation** — clearly documenting the review process to provide transparency and clarity.

- 🔄 **Process** — communicating the entire process to your team, ensuring they are aware of all phases and what to expect.

- 🗣️ **Motivations** — explaining the reasons behind salary decisions, helping team members understand the rationale.

- ⚠️ **Issues** — transparently addressing any issues that could result in below-expected or no raises, such as performance or financial constraints.

By focusing on clear and consistent communication, you can manage expectations and reduce frustration during the salary review process.

### 📚 What Other Companies are Doing

As you might imagine, finding information about salaries and how they are reviewed in larger companies is a challenging task due to their confidential nature.

However, after some research, I was able to find some information that could be inspiring.

#### 1) GitLab

GitLab is renowned for its transparent and inclusive [salary review process](https://handbook.gitlab.com/handbook/total-rewards/compensation/compensation-review-cycle/), detailed in their GitLab Handbook.

- **Base Budget** — at the time of writing, they have a funded budget of 4% of overall payroll.

- **Merit** — they have some recommendations based on various levels of performance (developing, performing, exceeding).

- **Open Salary Calculator **— they provide a public salary calculator that allows employees to see how their pay is determined.

- **Comprehensive Guidelines **— the GitLab Handbook includes extensive guidelines on compensation, ensuring employees understand the factors influencing their salaries.

- **Remote Work Considerations **— they adjust salaries based on geographic location, ensuring fair compensation for remote employees.

#### 2) Google

Google's compensation review process is designed to be transparent, fair, and performance-driven, focusing on three main components: salary, bonus, and equity.

- **Market-Based Salary Adjustments **— Salaries are determined based on role, location, and market conditions, ensuring competitiveness.

- **Performance Bonuses **— bonuses are awarded based on individual performance and contributions, with different plans for various roles.

- **Equity Awards **— new employees receive restricted stock units (GSUs), and ongoing equity refresh grants are provided based on performance.

- **Annual Pay Equity Review **— Google conducts a rigorous pay equity analysis annually to ensure fairness across gender and racial lines ([Levels FYI](https://www.levels.fyi/blog/understanding-googles-compensation.html)) ([blog.google](https://blog.google/inside-google/company-announcements/ensuring-we-pay-fairly-and-equitably/)) ([blog.google](https://blog.google/inside-google/life-at-google/our-annual-pay-equity-review/))

#### 3) Facebook

Facebook (Meta) combines rigorous performance reviews with a compensation structure that includes salary, bonuses, and stock refreshers.

- **Performance Reviews **— Conducted semi-annually, involving self-assessment, peer reviews, and manager evaluations.

- **Calibration Process **— managers meet to ensure fair ratings across teams, with performance bonuses and stock refreshers determined based on these reviews.

- **Stock Refreshers **— additional stock grants are awarded to high performers, promoting long-term retention ([RSUs simplified](https://candor.co/articles/tech-careers/performance-reviews-bonuses-at-facebook)) ([Robots.net](https://robots.net/ai/when-does-facebook-pay-your-performance-bonus/)).

#### 4) Netflix

Netflix emphasizes transparency and market alignment in its compensation practices.

- **Market-Based Pay **— they pay top of market salaries.

- **No Bonus Philosophy **— having salaries reflecting top of market value, they eliminate the need for bonuses.

- **The "**[**Keeper Test**](https://jobs.netflix.com/culture)**" **— managers decide if they would fight to keep an employee, with those not making the cut offered a severance package.

What works for other companies, particularly large tech firms, may not necessarily apply to your situation, especially regarding sensitive topics like salaries.

My primary advice is to communicate openly with your teams. Listen to their thoughts and work towards finding a mutually beneficial solution that aligns with your company's goals and their satisfaction.

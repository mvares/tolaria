# Interview with Henry Poydar

Today's guest is Henry Poydar

Henry is CEO and founder at Steady, with more than 25 years of experience in tech leadership roles.

With Henry, we talked about continuous coordination, an open source framework he co-created, providing a set of principles and practices for running modern, effective engineering teams.

### **1) 🏗️ Henry’s Journey (**[**02:15**](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=19MvvYsvlgw&t=135s)**)**

Henry shared his journey in tech, which spans over 25 years. 

His experience ranges from being a developer to holding leadership roles like VP of Engineering and CTO in various companies, including startups and public corps.

A pivotal moment in Henry's career came during his time at Constant Contact, where he faced the challenge of integrating a startup's technology into a large, legacy system. 

This experience highlighted the complexity of communication in larger orgs and inspired him to tackle this problem.

After leaving Constant Contact, Henry created Status Hero, a simple tool for team check-ins. Status Hero became extremely successful, especially during the pandemic, but it only solved part of the coordination problem. This eventually led to the development of [Steady](https://steady.space/), a more comprehensive solution built around the principles of Continuous Coordination 👇

### **2) 🔄 Continuous Coordination Principles (**[**13:41**](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=19MvvYsvlgw&t=821s)**)**

[Continuous Coordination](https://continuouscoordination.org/) is an open-source framework providing principles and practices for running modern engineering teams. There are seven key principles, and we discussed three of them on the show:

#### **Keeping a steady beat **🥁

This principle is about adding structure to asynchronous coordination. It involves two loops:

- **Small loop** — creating a small, frequent loop for team-level communication (similar to async stand-ups)

- **Big loop** — establishing a larger loop for updates on objectives or goals

A successful steady beat avoids the "alignment trap" of either too many meetings or complete absence of synchronization.

![image](../assets/cc75c67a5b9c4dbf.png)

#### **Telling the future **🔮

Inspired by David Marquette's book "[Turn the Ship Around](https://www.goodreads.com/book/show/16158601-turn-the-ship-around)", this principle encourages team members to communicate their intentions and plans. This approach:

1. Allows for course correction before misalignment occurs

1. Maintains autonomy while providing opportunities for input from leadership

#### **Leading with context **📋

Managers and leaders should provide necessary context upfront, ensuring team members have the information they need to make autonomous decisions and contribute effectively.

By the way, this is also one of Netflix core principles, as we explored in No Rules Rules 👇

### **3) 👥 Implementing Continuous Coordination (**[**30:00**](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=19MvvYsvlgw&t=1800s)**)**

I asked Henry how to implement CC in practice, and he proposed a four-step plan:

1. **Lead by example **— managers should embody the principles they want their team to adopt, participating actively in the coordination loops they establish.

1. **Emphasize benefits for contributors **— highlight how async coordination respects team members' time and optimizes for value creation in meetings.

1. **Set expectations with stakeholders **— establish regular update cadences for larger goals, providing stakeholders with insights into progress and opportunities for input.

1. **Start small **— begin with one project or team, setting up structured communication loops and demonstrating their effectiveness before expanding.

### **4) 🚀 Trends in engineering and product teams (**[**37:55**](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=19MvvYsvlgw&t=2275s)**)**

Henry lived through what feels like different eras of software engineering, and is now at the forefront of how modern teams work.

I asked him what trends he can see today, and he identified many:

1. **Changing work constants **— time and place, once fixed aspects of work, have become variable, requiring new approaches to coordination.

1. **Legacy of pre-pandemic tools **— many current tools were designed before the pandemic, not fully addressing the needs of today's distributed work environment.

1. **Over-engineering of processes **— there's a tendency to create overly complex tools and processes, making it challenging for developers to make decisions effectively.

1. **Hybrid and remote work challenges **— even as some companies returned to office, the lessons and habits formed during remote work persist, requiring intentional strategies to make things work.

### **5) 🤖 AI's impact on software development (**[**47:24**](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=19MvvYsvlgw&t=2844s)**)**

Finally, we discussed the growing role of AI in software development. Henry gave three main pieces of advice:

1. **Have realistic expectations **— while AI tools like GitHub Copilot can boost productivity, their impact is limited. Henry notes that coding typically occupies only about 15% of a developer's time, setting a ceiling on AI's potential gains.

1. **Use AI for synthesis and analysis **— Henry sees AI's greatest potential in synthesizing information from various sources, providing insights, and assisting with tasks like summarization and context-gathering.

1. **Importance of human skills is here to stay **— as AI handles more routine coding tasks, there will likely be a greater emphasis on skills like architecture, system design, and understanding the bigger picture of software engineering, which are still *human* domains.

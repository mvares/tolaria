# Daniel Vassallo – Small Bets for Engineers

### 🪴 Daniel’s Story

Daniel spent more than 8 years at AWS as a Software Engineer. He was paid well, got promoted often, and had apparently the job of his dreams.

However, the more he progressed in his career, the more he felt he was spending time on bureaucracy, purposeless stuff, and working on other people’s terms.

By the end of 2018, he was on track to becoming Principal Engineer and was paid around $500K/year in total compensation (salary + stock), but he decided to quit and work on his own terms.

He started a bunch of things:

#### Userbase.com

He started a software project and went all-in on it. Worked hard, invested a lot of savings, and even hired another developer.

He got 1st on Product Hunt, on the front page of Hacker News, and got a lot of positive feedback.

Eventually, though, the project was making $2000/mo, and Daniel had already wiped out 1/4th of his savings, so he did other things 👇

#### Freelancing

Started fractional freelancing to get some peace of mind about finances. He talked with friends and past co-workers and easily found some gigs to start with.

#### The Good Parts of AWS

He had a thread about AWS that went viral, where we went through what he called *the good parts* out of the hundreds of services available.

He expanded that thread into an ebook of ~70 pages and started selling it on Gumroad via Twitter.

He made $5K in one day which gave him motivation for more projects

#### Twitter Course

By sharing his journey since he left AWS, he had grown a 10K+ audience on Twitter very fast, so he made a small course about the lessons he learned about it.

The course took mere 16 hours to put together, and as of today it has made more than $300K.

#### Small Bets community

Fast forward today, Daniel runs the [Small Bets](https://smallbets.com/) community, which counts more than 5000 members, including engineers, entrepreneurs, and creators of various kinds.

### 💰 Small bets vs All-in

After Daniel quit his full-time job, he quickly got addicted to the new lifestyle where he could spend more time with his family, and work on his own terms.

So his goal became to **keep the lifestyle**.

To do so, he initially did what he knew best: go all-in on one thing (userbase.com), but this proved unsuccessful, leaving him with a big financial downside.

The lesson he learned is that with internet projects, success is a power law: vast majority of projects fail, and a few get an incredible upside. So he resorted to trying many things, **minimizing the downside** of each project (start small & cheap), and doubling down on what works at any given time.

He called this mindset the *small bets* approach, which is kind of a survivalist approach: the first goal of the game is to keep playing the game.

---

### 🏡 Lifestyle-first Mindset

This switch to a *lifestyle* mindset, though, didn’t come easy.

Daniel came from a career where he seeked impact and prestige. Over time, though, he realized he had less and less time to spend with his family, kids, friends, and hobbies. Also, many of his friends, who earned considerably less than him, seemed to have a comparable or better lifestyle: they owned a house, a car, but worked less hours and had no pager.

Moving to his new lifestyle required changing his *identity — *the way he saw himself. Today, he doesn’t attach labels to himself: he doesn’t see himself as a creator, entrepreneur, or engineer. He just does things.

He [keeps his identity small](https://paulgraham.com/identity.html), which gives him more optionality about what to go after.

---

### 🗓️ How Daniel works right now

Daniel doesn’t work on a regular schedule. He optimizes his projects to be low-maintenance, so he can do work driven by opportunities and excitement, rather than chores.

He admits there are long stretches of time in which he does next to nothing, followed by bursts of high intensity in which he delivers major projects and improvements.

During these high-intensity periods, he sets hard constraints in terms of output and deadlines, because he feels such pressure helps him being productive.

However, he always strives to work on many things, even if for little time, as opposed to going all-in on one. He believes working on diverse projects enhances creativity and **avoids tunnel vision**.

### 🚀 How to get started?

Daniel’s journey is not for everybody, as self-employment is not for everybody. Some people genuinely prefer structure and consistency, rather than uncertainty and having to pave your own path all the time.

For those who want to dip their toes in the water, though, Daniel suggests to start small:

#### Make $1000 with a small project

Start with a small project that you can make $1000 with. It should be a low hanging fruit, like:

- Create an ebook about something you know well

- Solve a problem for yourself and a group of friends.

- Sell something small to 10 strangers

Small things create momentum and lead to bigger things.

#### Set expectations

Set a monetary expectation about how much you want to make with your project. This is important for many reasons:

- Allows you do design practical steps to achieve it

- Prevents a project from ballooning uncontrollably

- Develops gratitude when you reach your goal — it doesn’t prevent you to go further but it keeps you grounded.

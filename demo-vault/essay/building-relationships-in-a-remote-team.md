### ✍️ Post

When I talk with people about the switch to remote, the #1 drawback they mention is the lack of human connection with co-workers.

Creating meaningful relationships at work is important. Relationships make us happier, create a sense of belonging, and, ultimately, make us more productive too.

While it’s hard to find anyone who disagrees with this, it’s equally hard to find managers who do real, practical work to nurture such relationships in a remote environment. 

The result is teams where work turns into more of a *transactional *pursuit. Turnover increases, people feel a personal disconnection from their work, and they ultimately just follow the money.

It doesn’t have to be like this.

The best managers I know *design processes* to help people create better relationships with co-workers. They are *intentional* about it, just like they are when it comes to productivity.

This article collects the best strategies and processes I know to make your remote engineering team better by fostering human connection.

It talks of 👇

- 🔍 Why **serendipitous** encounters at the office aren’t really *serendipitous*.

- 🤝 Providing **structure** and **permission **for personal moments to happen.

- 💡 **Practical ideas** you can apply every day, every week, and every quarter

It includes strategies from managers at great companies like Netflix, Netlify, Amazon, Microsoft, and more. It is also the result of conversations we had in the [Refactoring Community](https://refactoring.fm/p/community).

Let’s dive in 👇

### About structure and serendipity

I suspect that the reason why intentional work about remote relationships is so rare, is that it is counter-intuitive to many. Back at the office everything seemed so *effortless*, right? You didn’t *design *for personal moments — they were just **serendipitous**.

Or were they?

Think at the coffee machine in that corner — beside the sofa, maybe. *Someone* consciously decided to put a coffee machine, and a sofa, there, so you could have coffee breaks with others. Do you have a space for lunch? With some appliances maybe, and a few tables? This is *by design*, too.

The coffee machine, the microwave, the sofa, the ping pong table, they all create **structure **for certain things to happen. This structure in turn signals to people they have the **permission** to do these things. The ping pong table makes people know they *are allowed *to play ping pong at work. Otherwise, of course, they wouldn’t know.

[Embed](https://twitter.com/mar15sa/status/1356462774466002944)

This kind of judgment seems trivial in the physical world — either there is a ping pong table, or there isn’t — but it is trickier when you work remotely. 

Are you allowed to play a quick online game with a co-worker? Did the company make this game available to you, just like it did with the ping pong table? Are you allowed to talk about puppies at work, if there isn’t a Slack channel to talk about puppies?

Let’s see how you can create such structure and embed it into processes that happen **every day**, **every week**, or **every quarter**.

For each of these timeframes, I have collected the best ideas from brilliant managers at great companies.

### Everyday

#### 👯 Pair programming

Pair programming is incredibly helpful in a remote environment. Counter-intuitively, managers report it being even *more *helpful remotely than in presence, because it allows to build work relationships with your peers in a way that is not possible otherwise.

> “*Interestingly, in a remote environment, pair programming is one of the best things that you can do with two programmers*” – [Jason Warner](https://deepcollaboration.simplecast.com/episodes/jason-warner-cto-github), former CTO at Github

Also, tooling for remote pair programming is now so advanced that makes the experience in many ways better than just having two people sitting in front of a screen and taking turns.

![People pair programming on Pop.com](../assets/35cb3a98cf744ea1.png)

Check out my [**previous article**](https://refactoring.fm/p/pair-programming) about pair programming to learn more.

#### 🗣️ Small talk in meetings

[Jason Lengstorf](https://twitter.com/jlengstorf), VP of Developer Experience at** **Netlify recommends intentionally spending 10-15% of every meeting on small talk 👇

> *If you join a meeting with Netlify’s Developer Experience team, you might get the impression that we waste a lot of time. The first five to ten minutes of most of our meetings is spent telling stories, trying to make each other laugh, and (lovingly) making fun of *[*Phil Hawksworth*](https://twitter.com/philhawksworth)*.

10–15% of every meeting ‘wasted’ on goofing around might seem like a productivity nightmare, but I’d argue that it’s a secret weapon for us as a team.

Up until recently, I hadn’t met the majority of my team in person. Despite this, we’ve created inside jokes and been vulnerable with each other in these unstructured minutes. As a result, I’d go to battle for everyone on my team and trust them to make decisions on my behalf.*

You can check out Jason’s full article [**here**](https://leaddev.com/managing-distributed-teams/run-better-meetings-build-community-your-remote-team?utm_source=ActiveCampaign&utm_medium=email&utm_content=Run%20better%20meetings%20to%20build%20community%20in%20your%20remote%20team&utm_campaign=Originals%20-%20Issue%2061%20(November%2018)).

#### ☕ Watercooler meetings

[Molly Struve](https://twitter.com/molly_struve), Senior SRE at Netflix, organizes multiple *watercooler meetings* over the week.

She does so with a well defined process:

- **Each meeting has a topic **— you share that in advance, so people know it and it feels like a real event.

- **Process has an advocate **— who always join and proposes topics

- **The meeting lasts 15 minutes** — it might last longer, but this way if people are busy they have an excuse to leave. At the same time, 15 mins is the sweet spot so you can do it everyday and it doesn’t make people feel guilty about pausing from work.

![image](../assets/80bd33ac5ea8437b.png)

You can check out Molly’s full talk [**here**](https://leaddev.com/leaddev-live/eight-timezones-one-cohesive-team).

#### 🎽 Clubs around interests

In any company you will be able to find clusters of people with similar interests, like pets, videogames, or cooking.

You can create **dedicated spaces** to make people share content about such interests. This can be as simple as a slack channel, and if people are serious about that it can easily lead to recurring events, and more.

These *clubs* work best when there is a **core behaviour** that drives people engagement. Example: in a club around movies, the core behaviour might be members posting their review everytime they see a movie, with others leaving their comments below.

### Weekly / Bi-Weekly

#### 💬 Office Hours

Many successful companies I know hold recurring events where people can jump in and talk freely about their work.

These events have typically no agenda — people can discuss anything that happened over the week, also throwing in personal bits.

[Zach Wolfe](https://www.linkedin.com/in/zdwolfe/), Senior Engineer at Amazon, shared some details in our community about how they do it in his team 👇  

> *If the team is in agreeable time zones, as tech lead I've seen significant benefit from holding "office hours" weekly (done both scheduled and impromptu), calling them "work and chats", "coffee chats" or "jam sessions" to be more approachable.

Lots of questions that otherwise go unasked are brought up in these small group calls. It builds rapport between teammates as you solve problems together and chat.

The sweet spot is ~5 people before a call gets too noisy. Usually everyone gets in a little small talk, brings up their current task or problems, and the other members / tech lead offers help.*

#### Mob programming

[Mob programming](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mob_programming) is like pair programming, but with all of your team. People work together at a same problem, and take turns at the keyboard.

This is great to make people get familiar with how others in their team think about problems and code. You might do it with real problems from work, or even fictional challenges.

**Michael Genesini**, Frontend Engineering Manager at ProntoPro, shared in the community how they do it 👇 

> *Another thing that helps us is running a mob-programming session every 2 weeks (on Friday) [...] 

The topics are sometimes small coding challenges we find on the internet, other times small refactoring for reducing tech debt. I also expect we work on some tiny product feature but it hasn't happened yet. 

I discuss the possible topic with the frontend lead couple of days before to prepare the session. The tech stack is the same for the whole frontend so everyone is aware of what we are doing*.

*The session is 1h with 10min timebox (with a real pomodoro app) after that we change the driver (the person that is only writing what the others say). [...]

What is important is to stay all together for 1h sharing the way we think and code).*

#### 🕹️ Fun events

Having fun, non-work experiences is admittedly easier in a non-distributed team, where you can just have a beer or go out for dinner together. Nevertheless, creative alternatives exist for remote teams that are no less fun. Here are a couple that I like:

**1) Remote cocktails **—** **[Darren Dillon](https://devinterrupted.com/podcast/management-and-the-future-of-ai-with-azures-cto/), Azure’s CTO, has online cocktail parties with his team:

> *We found a company that hosted cocktail parties online, so they shipped out the drinks, all the ingredients. And then there was a zoom call where a cocktail bartender took us through how to build some drinks.*

**2) Games on Around **— [Around.co](https://www.around.co/) is a new conferencing tool that has a number of additional features, including playing games with your team. You can have a session as a fun way to close the week. I don’t get paid to promote it — I just love what they are doing.

### Quarterly

#### 🔄 Rotate teams

If you have durable teams that address different parts of the product, consider rotating people periodically to allow them to work with everyone. 

Doing this quarterly may be convenient so you can attach it to the general planning.

Michael shared more insights about how they do it at ProntoPro 👇

> *We use 6+2 weeks cycles and so every 8 weeks we basically re-assemble the squads based on the projects we will work on. One of my duties as EM is to balance the expertise/knowledge, the needs/preferences, and the relations of our frontend engineers.
*

#### 🏕️ Retreats

Offsites / retreats used to be niche initiatives. Up to a couple of years ago, offsites would be usually dedicated to executive teams, while those for the full company were pretty rare.

Of course the pandemic changed everything, and retreats are now a vital habit of distributed teams to allow people spend quality time together. Forward-looking companies like [Buffer](https://www.surfoffice.com/blog/how-buffer-organizes-company-retreats) and [Zapier](https://zapier.com/learn/remote-work/how-run-company-retreat-remote-team/) have been organizing them for years, while the rest of us is catching up just now — and the [demand is soaring](https://www.businessinsider.com/moniker-companies-corporate-retreats-office-spending-budgets-remote-working-2021-7?r=US&IR=T).

Here are two great articles you can check about why and how to organize retreats:

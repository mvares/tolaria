As a creator, I am a believer in **building in public**. I like to share not only my Refactoring articles, but also my working process and everything that is behind the emails I send out every Thursday.

I believe it's a win-win:

- It helps me to reflect on how I work ([writing is thinking](https://twitter.com/david_perell/status/1260793045806493696?lang=en)) and to get feedback from readers.

- It helps other people who want to start something similar.

In the past I already published [How I Write Refactoring](https://refactoring.fm/p/how-i-write-refactoring-), and it turned out to be one of my most popular articles ever.

Recently Refactoring got to [10K subscribers](https://refactoring.fm/p/august) and many people reached out to ask how I make it grow. I thought it would be useful to share my process here, for anyone who is going to write an engineering blog / newsletter, for their company or for themselves.

So let's dive in 👇

![The Refactoring Flywheel](../assets/51d1e1eb5ac94715.png)

### 🔢 The Refactoring Flywheel

When it comes to growth, I have been doing basically the same things for the past 6+ months. 

I am happy they stayed the same, because I wanted to find a process that would scale nicely with subscribers. It did so far.

Also, what I do is pretty generic and can be applied to basically any domain, not just Engineering. The only caveat is the assumption that articles are *essays*, or they are about *ideas* anyway. They are not, say, collections of links. But even in that case, you can probably adapt it to that use.

My process is based on four parts:

1. 📑 **Notes**

1. 🐦 **Twitter**

1. 💼 **Linkedin**

1. 🤗 **Communities**

### 📑 Notes

Note-taking is at the foundation of everything I do, from thinking to writing to growing the newsletter. 

I keep notes in a [Notion](https://notion.so/) database, and I have two kinds of notes:

- 👓 **Reading Notes**

- 🌱 **Evergreen Notes**

#### 👓 Reading Notes

Reading notes are about, you guessed it, what I read. 

I have a [process for reading online](https://refactoring.fm/p/how-to-read-online) and I read nearly everything on Instapaper. For each article I highlight the most relevant passages and these are sent to my Notion automatically by an app called [Readwise](https://readwise.io/).

Readwise creates a note for each article I have read and highlighted, and each note only contains my highlights. This is all done automatically.

![Instapaper to Notion workflow](../assets/fbf363264e5240be.jpg)

When I have to decide what to write next, most of the times I just go through my notes and get inspiration from there.

When I think I am going to use a note, I read it again and highlight it for further *compression*. I took this [progressive summarization](https://fortelabs.co/blog/progressive-summarization-a-practical-technique-for-designing-discoverable-notes/#:~:text=Progressive%20Summarization%20focuses%20therefore%20on,little%20as%20the%20information%20deserves.) approach from [Tiago Forte](https://twitter.com/fortelabs).

All the highlighting and summarization require more effort than regular reading, but they improve reuse and retention, which is what I need to write articles.

#### 🌱 Evergreen Notes

The most important notes I have are the **Evergreen Notes**. These are notes about individual ideas and insights.

These ideas need to be:

- **Short** — ideally the size of a couple of tweets.

- **Independent** — they need to make sense in isolation, without much context.

- **Eternal** — they should stay relevant indefinitely.

Sometimes I create them straight from reading notes, but mostly they come from the articles I write. I am usually able to create 3-4 of them for each article.

![image](../assets/9f47d3a3910b491a.png)

These notes are related and linked to each other and they build up a *network *of knowledge that I can refer to whenever I need. 

This is a lightweight version of the [Zettelkasten](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zettelkasten) method explained in the [How to Take Smart Notes](https://www.goodreads.com/en/book/show/34507927-how-to-take-smart-notes) book, which is probably the book I mentioned the most in Refactoring articles!

![Example of an Evergreen Note](../assets/5e873a58313c4080.png)

### 🐦 Twitter

Twitter is the social network I use the most to spread my ideas. I do basically two things on it:

- 💡 **Tweets** — I tweet about articles and ideas.

- 📈 **Ads **— I run some Twitter Ads.

#### Tweets

Every week I write a long thread where I re-write most of the latest article. These threads end up being my most successful tweets.

I also tweet 🌱 **evergreen notes **separately, as they fit very well with the twitter format.

All in all I write about 5-6 tweets per week + the main thread, and I plan most of this work in advance on [Hypefury](https://hypefury.com/), usually in around half a day.

Hypefury also has this concept of [*evergreen posts*](https://hypefury.crisp.help/en/article/evergreen-posts-dcjfwt/)* *and allows me to retweet them automatically every once in a while. I use this with tweets coming from my evergreen notes, and it seems to work well so far!

#### Twitter Ads

I have been running twitter ads for several months now. I keep a budget of €10 / day and I usually bring in new subscribers at ~€0.25-€0.40 / subscriber.

Ads are relatively cheap and allow you to **target your audience** very precisely so I definitely recommend them, whatever your domain is.

Although there isn't a visible difference in engagement between organic subscribers and those coming from ads, I try to keep a balance and make sure at least 50% of growth comes from organic subscribers.

### 💼 Linkedin

I use Linkedin to **connect with subscribers.** I add them as contacts there — every single one.

This is not an automatic trick, it is literally me taking the list of the latest subscribers every once in a while, and adding them one by one (if I haven't connected with you yet, [feel free](https://www.linkedin.com/in/lucaronin/)).

And I **engage with everyone** who writes me: I ask for feedback, give advice if anyone asks, make intros, and so on. Basically, I try to behave like a human being 😄

People often ask me if I am worried that adding subscribers as contacts makes my feed worse, or that then I can't separate real-life connections from newsletter ones.

I don't see it like that.

Readers of Refactoring know an awful lot about me, my beliefs, and my experience, because I basically write everything in my articles. I know much less about most of my coworkers than my readers usually know about me.

Connecting with readers creates genuine opportunities for everyone: work opportunities, advice opportunities, or just friendships. 

Being able to connect and make friends with like-minded people is one of the internet superpowers — let's use it!

### 🤗 Communities

Finally, I am member of a few communities where I know I can find other people interested in what I write. I try to engage there and participate in other discussions whenever I can, and I also post my articles there from time to time (I don't want to be spammy).

Here are the communities I like the most: [The Generalist](https://www.readthegeneralist.com/), [Lenny's Newsletter](https://lennysnewsletter.com/), [Dev Interrupted](http://devinterrupted.com/), [Blogging for Devs](https://bloggingfordevs.com/).

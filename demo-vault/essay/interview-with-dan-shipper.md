# Interview with Dan Shipper

### **1) 🏗️ From Newsletter to Media Empire (**[**03:31**](https://youtu.be/jGWZUC1WU3Y?t=211)**)**

Dan Shipper's journey to building [Every](https://every.to/) began with a simple productivity newsletter called "Super Organizers" — focused on how smart people organize their work lives. 

What's fascinating is that his original goal wasn't to build a media company but rather to conduct customer interviews for a potential tools-for-thought product:

> ***"I started Super Organizers because I really wanted to build a tools-for-thought software company — like Notion or Roam. I needed a way to get smart people on the phone with me, so I decided to start a newsletter and interview them for it."***

From these humble beginnings, Every evolved through several phases. 

First, it became a bundle of newsletters during the early creator economy boom, creating a space where multiple writers could come together under one subscription. Then, Dan and his team began developing **small software tools to complement their writing** – a natural extension of their interest in products.

The watershed moment came when [Nathan Baschez](https://www.linkedin.com/in/nbashaw/) (Dan's co-founder) built [Lex](https://lex.page/), an AI document writer that went viral and was later spun out as a separate company. This success, combined with [Brandon Gell](https://www.linkedin.com/in/brandongell) joining to run their studio, accelerated their experiments in blending content and software.

Today, Every has transformed into what Dan calls a "multi-modal media company" publishing essays, podcasts, videos, and software – all bundled into one subscription. What makes Every unique is how it embraces the blurring line between writing and building in the age of AI.

### **2) 🔄 The Creator CEO Journey (**[**13:07**](https://youtu.be/jGWZUC1WU3Y?t=787)**)**

Dan's evolution as a CEO has followed what he describes as a "*horseshoe*" pattern. Initially, he was primarily a writer, then tried to become a "*real CEO*" who doesn't write, before finally returning to writing as a core part of his identity and role.

> ***"I made a decision to be like, 'I'm a writer, I want to write.' That's a core part of making this business work. So now I spend a lot of time writing... And that has actually been very good for the business."***

This journey has shaped how Dan structures his workday:

- **🌅 Morning focus** — He aims to spend mornings with his phone off, fully dedicated to writing.

- **🧩 Afternoon management** — Afternoons are for meetings, strategy, and team collaboration.

- **📝 Weekly rhythm** — For the past year, he's published every Friday, creating a productive deadline.

Dan believes many creators burn out because they follow traditional business advice that pulls them away from their core creative work. Instead, he suggests a different model:

> ***"To be a creator, you start creating, and if you have product-market fit with the stuff you're making, you just keep going and hire people to do everything else."***

This approach directly challenges the Silicon Valley startup playbook where founders typically step away from doing the core work as the company grows. 

For Dan, admitting that he wanted to spend half his day writing was initially embarrassing but ultimately made him a better CEO by forcing him to build systems and empower his team.

### **3) 🔮 Building for the Long Game (**[**24:30**](https://youtu.be/jGWZUC1WU3Y?t=1470)**)**

Sustainable success in creative work is fundamentally tied to playing the long game. This means creating systems and routines that you can maintain for years, not just months:

> ***"It's very underrated how much duration matters for any kind of thing like this—whether you're a creator, running a startup, or maybe both. Doing something for a really long time is really, really important."***

Dan emphasizes that this doesn't mean avoiding challenges or negative emotions. 

In fact, he believes the entrepreneurial journey naturally involves experiencing "*the full palette of emotions*." The key is preventing those challenges from becoming so overwhelming that they cause you to quit altogether.

He shared a framework for making career decisions that resonates deeply with both creators and entrepreneurs:

- **🎯 Play to your strengths** — Find opportunities that showcase your existing skills

- **🌱 Pursue growth** — Choose paths that teach you new skills you want to learn

- **💼 Create value** — Ensure your work helps the organization or business succeed

Finding the sweet spot between these three elements naturally attracts resources and opportunities. Most importantly, Dan recommends "*chasing growth of options instead of promotions*" — viewing your career as a portfolio of experiences rather than a traditional ladder.

### **4) 🤖 Living with AI Tools (**[**26:37**](https://youtu.be/jGWZUC1WU3Y?t=1597)**)**

AI has become thoroughly embedded in Dan's daily workflow, transforming how he thinks, writes, and manages his business. He uses generative AI constantly across different domains:

- **📚 Reading enhancement** — Taking pictures of physical books and asking 01 (Anthropic's Claude) to explain concepts

- **✍️ Writing assistance** — Generating metaphors, explaining complex concepts, or suggesting directions for essays

- **👥 Team management** — Analyzing meeting transcripts to improve his leadership approach

- **🧠 Note-taking** — Using Cursor with AI to organize notes, build custom tools, and find connections across ideas

> ***"I use Cursor for notes... you can throw tons of notes into it and have Cursor composer, which is an agent, research through your notes and answer questions. Because it's used for programming, you can have it build tools for you—like one that splits a big note into smaller atomic notes or hosts my notes on a web page."***

When it comes to software development at Every, Dan estimates that **80-90% of their code is written by AI**. 

This doesn't mean humans don't understand the code—rather, engineers focus on planning and architecture while AI handles implementation details. The result is dramatically increased productivity with smaller teams.

Dan observes that people fall into three categories regarding AI adoption:

1. The 10% who are natural early adopters (like himself)

1. Another 10% who actively resist the technology

1. The 80% in the middle who just want to know if it will help them do their job better

He believes this middle group will gradually adopt AI tools as they become more reliable and accessible, but it requires both curiosity and a willingness to experiment with new approaches.

### **5) 🧠 The Future of Work & Intelligence (**[**40:23**](https://youtu.be/jGWZUC1WU3Y?t=2423)**)**

Looking toward the future, Dan sees a fundamental shift in how we think about knowledge work. 

He describes this as moving from a "knowledge economy" to an "allocation economy":

> ***"In a knowledge economy, you're compensated based on what you know. In an allocation economy, you're compensated based on how well you can allocate the resources of intelligence."***

This shift will require everyone to develop skills traditionally associated with management:

- **🎯 Resource allocation** — Knowing which tools to use for which tasks

- **⏱️ Estimation** — Understanding how long things should take

- **🔍 Quality control** — Recognizing when AI outputs are off-track

- **📋 Planning** — Creating roadmaps and strategies that leverage AI capabilities

Dan believes this future will particularly favor generalists—people who can work across multiple domains and understand how different pieces fit together. 

Every itself demonstrates this with just 10 people running a daily newsletter, weekly podcast, multiple software products, and a consultancy.

When asked about his bold predictions for AI's future impact, Dan's most provocative view concerns how AI might transform science itself:

> ***"I think it'll fundamentally change how we do science and how we think about where science is valuable. Science tries to abstract phenomena into generalized theories where you can say X causes Y. What machine learning does is find thousands of little correlations that together, applied in context, can predict what might come next."***

He suggests we may need to become more comfortable with correlation-based prediction models rather than insisting on causal understanding in many domains—a perspective that challenges centuries of scientific tradition.

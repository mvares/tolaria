What does an Engineering Manager do?

This is an impossible question to answer because the EM role is a *fluid* one, where different teams call for different approaches.

The best managers, as we [discussed with Thiago](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=vO-EX0HHaB8), *shape-shift* to fill the specific gaps of their teams, be these about tech, product, process, or else.

That said, in recent years, we can see a decisive trend in the industry where:

- **Managers are getting more technical** —  they are expected to give a stronger technical contribute than before.

- **Managers are fewer** — per number of engineers. Orgs are also getting *flatter*, with less middle management and fewer levels overall.

Big tech made the headlines about this multiple times, from the Twitter + Elon stories, to Meta asking [managers to go back to ICs](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2023-02-07/meta-to-ask-many-managers-to-become-individual-contributors-or-leave?srnd=premium).

You can see a flavor of this in startups as well, where the ratio of engineers-to-pretty-much-anybody-else is skyrocketing:

- [Telegram has 30 engineers and the CEO is the only PM](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=1Ut6RouSs0w&t=2263s)

- [Perplexity has 50 engineers and 2 PMs](https://www.lennysnewsletter.com/p/how-perplexity-builds-product)

Today we’ll try to unpack how the Engineering Manager role is changing today, and why. Here is the agenda:

- 🏋️‍♀️ **Empowered engineers** — how engineers do more today thanks to better tooling & AI.

- 💨 **Coordination headwinds **— how companies are learning from big tech struggles.

- 🪤 **Technical EMs traps **— are technical EMs the solution to all? Not so fast.

- 💬 **Community Stories **— insights and real-world experience from managers in the Refactoring community.

### 🏋️‍♀️ Empowered engineers

The first reason why ICs are sought after is that they are more productive than ever.

You might think this is obvious: tooling *naturally *only gets better, so productivity is anytime at an all-time-high, just like any new iPhone is the most powerful iPhone ever.

This is kind of true, but there are also very specific trends that are helping with this:

- **AI coding** — there are several reports now on the effect of Copilot (and the likes) on productivity, which is immense. AI not only helps engineers do more of the same, but it helps them become more independent, as they get basic proficiency across the whole stack.

- **Full-stack **— as opposed to 10 years ago, it is just more common for the same language to stay the same across the whole stack. From the popularity of JS frameworks, to the [resurgence of Ruby on Rails](https://devops.com/the-ruby-on-rails-resurgence/), engineers are more and more full-stack these days.

- **And***** more***** full-stack **— *full-stack* doesn’t stop at coding. Good tools today help with UI, infra, project mgmt, and all kinds of things for which you needed a full team before. Now, you might still do for big projects, but not for a lot of small stuff.

So, engineers today are just doing more: 1) more of what they were already doing, and 2) more of what other people used to do.

### 💨 Coordination headwinds

These trends are bound to have a strong impact on how teams are run.

An obvious effect is, of course, that you can accomplish more with fewer people, but it doesn’t stop here. Other than being more productive, engineers are more *autonomous*: they can own full slices of the product, drastically reducing the need for coordination, layering, and work’s work.

So, a team of 30 engineers today can be run way differently than even just 5 years ago. With the same headcount, you get 1) more output, and 2) less need for management.

Is this bad for engineering managers? Not necessarily! 👇

### 🎽 What does an EM do?

We said an EM can do many things, depending on what the team needs the most help with. Taking a page from the fantastic [Pat Kua’s article](https://www.patkua.com/blog/5-engineering-manager-archetypes/) about EM archetypes, EMs may help with four areas:

1. **🔨 Tech** — they drive technical decisions, spearhead quality, guide design, and more.

1. 🪴 **Team** — they coach team members, help with careers, foster a healthy culture, and do hiring.

1. 🚚 **Process** — they tune the dev process to make it lean and effective. They remove obstacles, maintain alignment, but also scope work and allocate resources.

1. 📱 **Product** — they liase with stakeholders to shape the product + technical strategy. They align technical direction with what’s needed by the business.

No EM does all of this, of course! 

How you allocate your time depends on many things, like company size, stage, and especially your team’s strengths and weaknesses: have strong technical leadership already? You may take a back seat on that. Are you in high-growth? More work on hiring. And so on.

At any given time, these duties may not need full-time managers, and might be performed by other roles over a fraction of their time.

![The inflection point that makes tech leads separate from EMs looks waaay further than it used to be.](../assets/2739261493ae4bb5.png)

Traditional hybrid roles, like Tech Lead, or ([the infamous?](https://lethain.com/tech-lead-managers/)) Tech Lead Manager, are based on the idea that you stick with your IC work most of the time, but also help with some managerial stuff.

As teams grow, hybrid roles naturally split into full-time IC and full-time manager roles (and tracks), but here is the thing: as a consequence of the productivity and autonomy trends, this inflection point seems to be way further than it used to be.

In other words, teams are sticking with hybrid roles (or no managers at all) for longer than ever.

All of this had been already [spotted by Aditya Agarwal](https://twitter.com/adityaag/status/1509196363019395081), ex-CTO at Dropbox, two years ago 👇

> *How far can you go without formal engineering management? This might surprise some but the answer is: pretty damn far. […]*

So, is all management impacted the same way? Not really 👇

### 📊 The Five EM Archetypes

Following Pat Kua’s piece, you have five main archetypes of engineering managers (+1, the *manager of managers*), each roughly focusing on one of the four pillars:

![image](../assets/2f23da153a164dd5.png)

In my experience, and based on the conversation I am having with readers and in the community, some managers are struggling, while others are absolutely thriving.

It all depends on what they do:

#### 🔨 Tech Lead EMs ➕➕

Technical managers look like the #1 winners of this transition. People who can manage *and *are still able to have a strong technical impact are simply invaluable on the market today.

Still, this role is not all roses and can be extremely challenging to navigate — more on that later.

#### 🪴 Team Lead EMs ➖

With teams getting smaller and processes getting more leaner and distributed, EMs primarily focused on the team naturally see less work.

In fact, while many of the *team* duties are evergreen and will never go away — think of coaching, careers, hiring — it is also rarer that these make for a full-time job these days.

#### 🚚 Delivery EM ➖

Delivery EMs focus on process. I feel like this archetype also lost some ground — coordination is simpler, and, while good dev process matters more than ever, it is also go easier to put in place.

#### 📱 Product EM ➕

Product EMs are a sneaky winner here, often replacing full PMs.

The hybrid PM+EM role has long being a weird one, but it is slowly but surely graduating into mainstream.

#### 👑 Lead of Leads EM ➖➖

Managers of managers are probably the biggest losers of the draw.

Those who are the most removed from product and technical work are looked at suspiciously — especially those who sit in the middle between head/director and line manager roles.

This means that leads of leads can still thrive, of course, but are given less leeway and need to convincingly prove their impact

### 🪤 Technical Managers Traps

In a way, it feels like the archetype of the modern engineering manager is someone who can — more than ever — wear multiple hats.

E.g. run 1:1s and career conversations + do meaningful coding. Guide technical strategy + talk with customers and get product insights.

If this feels familiar for startups, it also brings… familiar risks.

People who wear many hats are exposed to all kinds of problems:

- **Being spread too thin** — it is hard to figure out what you should focus on at any given time, with the risk of *half-assing* many thing instead of *whole-assing* a few good ones.

- **Management neglect **— when there is too much to do and people are overwhelmed, they often fall back on what they know best, which for fresh managers is typically… coding. This is how management gets neglected, even under the best intentions.

- **Harder to hire from outside **— when a manager needs both tech and team context to be successful, because they are expected to work on both, it gets harder to hire folks from outside. There is just too much to onboard people on.

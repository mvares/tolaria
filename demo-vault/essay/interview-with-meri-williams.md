### 1) 🚀 Meri's Unique Career Journey ([01:55](https://youtu.be/MfXaVKC9odg&t=115s))

Meri's path into tech began with hardware hacking as a teenager, including the remarkable experience of helping build part of South Africa's first satellite at age 16. After studying computer science in the UK, she spent her first decade at Procter & Gamble — an unusual start that gave her early exposure to corporate leadership development.

Her transition to modern tech came through an unexpected opportunity. Tom Loosemore called her with a proposition: "Hey, we're going to fix the government. Come help us do that. This is a once in a lifetime opportunity."

> *"The most exciting start-up in London right now is in the government." — Tim O'Reilly, referring to the Government Digital Service*

At GDS, Meri built the team that created [Gov.UK](http://gov.uk/), experiencing rapid scaling from 30 to 300 people in just nine months. This unconventional journey — starting corporate, then moving to government, and finally to scale-ups and startups — gave her a unique perspective on leadership.

- 💼 **Early commercial exposure** — learned finance, marketing, and business operations at 23

- 👥 **Management experience** — managed people older than her father from a young age

- 🏛️ **Government innovation** — proved that rapid iteration could work in traditional institutions

This reverse trajectory provided Meri with leadership and commercial skills that most engineers don't develop until much later in their careers, if at all.

### 2) 🌍 The Case for Diversity: Data, Challenges & Solutions ([12:16](https://youtu.be/MfXaVKC9odg&t=736s))

When confronting skeptics of diversity initiatives, Meri leads with data: diverse teams are more innovative, profitable, creative, and likely to succeed. But she goes deeper into why this matters:

> *"If you are a woman in the tech industry and you have gotten to senior software engineer, it is at least twice as hard for you as it was for anybody else... They're almost worth more than somebody else in the same role."*

Meri reframes the meritocracy argument: if someone from an underrepresented group has reached a senior level, they've overcome additional barriers that others haven't faced. This suggests higher capability, not lower.

#### The Three Critical Questions

People considering whether to join or stay at a company ask themselves:

- **Am I expected here?** — Is someone like me supposed to be in this role?

- **Am I respected here?** — Are my differences seen as a bug or a feature?

- **Can I be myself and be successful?** — The most important question

#### Getting the Order Right

Most organizations approach diversity backwards. Instead of starting with recruitment marketing, Meri recommends:

1. **Create inclusive practices first** — ensure everyone can be successful being themselves

1. **Address unconscious bias** — train people to recognize discriminatory patterns

1. **Fix external messaging** — only then worry about how you look to potential candidates

> *"Part of why diversity leads to better results is because it's more difficult rather than less difficult. They have to rely on data more and they have to actually collaborate and come up with a good solution that they all believe is the right thing to do."*

The key insight: diversity isn't magical — it works because it forces better decision-making processes through increased collaboration and data reliance.

### 3) 🎤 Creating Memorable Talks & Conferences ([23:14](https://youtu.be/MfXaVKC9odg&t=1394s))

After 10+ years of curating LeadDev conferences, Meri has identified what makes talks truly memorable and actionable.

#### The Three Elements of Great Talks

- 📖 **Personal story or case study** — real experience that grounds the content

- 🧠 **Abstracted framework** — theory that can be applied broadly

- ⚡ **Practical application** — clear takeaways that audiences can implement

#### The Selection Process

LeadDev employs a rigorous, blind review process:

- **Anonymous scoring** based only on talk descriptions and takeaways

- **Pre-review committee** filters thousands of submissions to top 200-250

- **Theme analysis** to understand what topics are trending in the industry

- **Audience research** to match content with attendee needs

This year, AI was everywhere in submissions, requiring careful curation to avoid becoming "just an AI conference."

#### Conference Experience Beyond Talks

Modern LeadDev events have evolved into "festivals of engineering leadership" featuring:

- 🛠️ **Interactive workshops** — hands-on learning experiences

- 💬 **Ask Me Anything sessions** — direct access to industry leaders

- 🎯 **Career panels** — guidance on paths to staff engineer or VP roles

- 🤝 **Quality sponsor interactions** — genuine conversations, not just swag collection

The shift from single-track lecture format to multi-track, multi-engagement experiences serves different learning preferences and creates more networking opportunities.

### 4) 🤖 Developing Engineers in the Age of AI ([33:19](https://youtu.be/MfXaVKC9odg&t=1999s))

Meri addresses one of the industry's most pressing concerns: how AI is changing the junior engineer experience and what we need to do about it.

#### The Fundamental Shift

Previously, engineers had an accidentally perfect learning progression: write small pieces of code, gradually take on bigger tasks, and slowly build expertise. AI disrupts this natural flow:

> *"A lot of entry level engineers don't review much code. They're not yet trusted to do PR reviews... But with AI, they're reviewing code all the time."*

Junior engineers now spend more time reviewing AI-generated code than writing it themselves — but they lack the experience to spot issues, security vulnerabilities, or architectural problems.

#### The Skills Gap Challenge

The statistics are alarming: **75% of entry-level jobs disappeared in the last year**. This creates a dangerous trend — if we stop training junior engineers now, we'll have a massive skills gap in five years.

#### What Needs to Change

- 🔍 **Earlier code review training** — teach juniors to spot problems they've never encountered

- 🏗️ **Foundation skills focus** — security, performance, scalability, maintainability

- 💡 **Architectural thinking** — help juniors use AI for structural problem-solving, not just coding

- 👥 **More intentional mentorship** — pair programming and design sessions become crucial

#### The Opportunity Hidden in the Challenge

Some junior engineers are using AI brilliantly — they talk to ChatGPT about problem structure before coding, use Claude to think through architecture, and treat AI as an "endlessly patient mentor."

> *"Every junior I spoke to said that ChatGPT is now an endlessly patient mentor, that they can ask the same dumb question over and over again and never worry about annoying them."*

Senior engineers, while more skeptical and better at spotting AI errors, often miss these innovative uses of the technology.

#### The Long-Term Vision

AI will likely make foundational skills more important, not less. Just as architects moved from hand-drafting to CAD to 3D modeling — each transition requiring deeper understanding of structural principles — software engineers will need stronger foundations as AI handles routine tasks.

The industry may need to formalize training similar to architecture's three-degree system, ensuring engineers understand not just how to build, but how to build securely, maintainably, and at scale.

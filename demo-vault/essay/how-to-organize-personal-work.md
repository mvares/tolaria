# How to Organize Personal Work

Exactly one year ago, I got married. And right after that, I went to honeymoon, for three weeks.

This means that for three weeks I shut ~95% of work off. I consider this a major accomplishment, because, in the previous two years, I had never been able to stay away from Refactoring for more than ~4-5 days.

It didn’t come easy, though. It required a lot of prep, and that, combined with everything else going on in our lives, made those last few months *extreme*.

In fact, other than the day-to-day work, we organized the wedding (which in Italy is a big deal), we relocated, and I anticipated stuff for honeymoon, that is, I wrote 6 newsletter editions in advance.

All of this meant fitting ~2x the regular work into an already packed schedule.

Everything turned out fine, and, in retrospect, this period taught me a lot about myself, productivity, and knowledge work in general.

Going from Italy to Polynesia is about 30 hours travel time, including a non-stop 12-hour flight to Los Angeles. During that flight I drafted this article. 

Today I want to tell you about my framework to organize knowledge work and maximize your personal output. It draws from the experience of the past few months, combined with that of two years of solo writing work, and 10+ of engineering / management one.

Here is what we will cover today:

- 🪨 **Rocks, pebbles, and sand** — a familiar metaphor, borrowed for managing work.

- 🧺 **Work basket** — what should go into your basket? How should you spend your time?

- ⏱️ **Managing time **— scheduling work and handling interruptions.

- ⚖️ **Managers vs engineers** — how this applies to managers?

Let’s dive in!

### 🪨 Rocks, pebbles, and sand

There is a famous story, that you have probably heard, about a professor who teaches a life lesson to his students by means of a simple metaphor.

This metaphor is about a jar, that first gets filled with rocks, then pebbles are added, and finally sand. If you want to make room for everything, the order in which you put stuff in the jar matters: rocks leave room for pebbles, which in turn leave room for sand. The opposite is not true: if you put pebbles first then you can’t fit the rocks, and so on.

In the original story, the metaphor is about important things in your life. Today, I *borrow* this to talk about work.

Like the items in the story, not all work is created equal. If the jar is your time, or, better, your energy, the *strategy* you use to fill it matters.

So, let’s organize your work into three buckets 👇

#### 🔴 Rocks — High Intensity

Rock work is your most important work: it is the one that requires the most of your **energy** and **creativity**. It produces an original output and gives you a sense of fulfilment. 

Rock work is why you do your job.

Examples of rock work for various tech roles might be: system design, creating good design docs / PRDs, coding complex stuff, running good 1:1s, or, in my case, writing newsletter editions.

It is the work that makes you unique, and the hardest to delegate.

#### 🟡 Pebbles — Medium Intensity

Pebbles aren’t necessarily *smaller* tasks than rocks, but they require less of your energy per unit of time. While you need to perform rocks at your very best, for pebbles you just need to clear some bar, or keep a standard.

A litmus test to identify pebbles, for me (may not be the same for others) is whether I can do them with music on.

For example, as a writer, I can’t really write essays while listening to music, but I can reply emails, or do some research for new articles. As a manager, I could go through status updates, check-in on things, and unblock small decisions. As an engineer, I could code small changes, squash some bugs or add missing tests.

#### 🟢 Sand — Low Intensity

Sand work is basically **chores** — you do them because you have to, but there isn’t a lot of intelligence involved.

Examples of my personal chores are moving notes between various tools, publish interviews on youtube, update spreadsheets, or sending some email sequences.

Infamous engineering chores are: going through CI/CD, updating dependencies, or attending meetings where you never speak (let’s not pretend they don’t exist).

### 🧺 Work Basket

In my experience, you should aim to spend ~50% of your day on *rocks —* high-intensity work.

In fact, there is a balance to be found: you can’t do intense work all day long, as it is too draining and you can’t keep up in the long run. At the same time, spending most of your day on small things points to lack of meaning, impact, and personal growth.

 So, how do you optimize your basket? Here are a few strategies, based on the type of tasks:

- 🪚 **Break Rocks** — for hard, intense work, break it into smaller tasks and turn some “rock parts” into pebbles and sand. To simplify writing, create templates. To simplify processes, create checklists. To simplify coding, improve platform.

- ↪️ **Delegate Pebbles **— simpler tasks are easier to delegate. They also make for opportunities for younger co-workers to prove themselves. I wrote a full article about good delegation: [https://refactoring.fm/p/how-to-delegate](https://refactoring.fm/p/how-to-delegate)

- 🤖 **Automate Sand **— chores should be simply automated. For example, it is easy these days to create workflows and integrate various tools by using AI and low-code tools. I wrote more about this here: [https://refactoring.fm/p/how-to-automate-work-with-low-code](https://refactoring.fm/p/how-to-automate-work-with-low-code).

This strategy is continuous: you take work that is hard, try to make it easier, and keep shifting your attention to stay focused on hard work.

![image](../assets/ceaf283672c34659.png)

This is like turning art into engineering:

- 🎨 **Art **— no clear rules, you apply your judgment in an instinctive way based on your experience.

- 🔨 **Engineering **— you follow well-defined rules to achieve an output.

All tasks we do are part-art, part-engineering. Doing great work, to me, looks like 1) doing great art, plus 2) continously turning art into engineering.

So, once you are happy with your tasks, how do fill your jar in a way that fits the most things?

Let’s talk of the things that have the most impact to me: **interruptions** and **time management** 👇

### 🔀 Interruptions

This essay was challenging to write, because it sums up a lot of thoughts that I collected over the years. So, it is no coincidence that I drafted this on a plane from Munich to Los Angeles: 12 hours, no internet, zero interruptions. It was probably most productive time of my life.

Interruptions are damaging for all work, but especially creative work. I have found that there are three main interruption *environments *you may find yourself in: 

1. 🙋‍♂️ You **will** be interrupted — e.g. there is a meeting later, or you need to check email frequently.

1. 🤷‍♂️ You **may** be interrupted — e.g. if someone writes you, you may need to reply.

1. 🙅‍♂️ You **can’t** be interrupted — e.g. you shut all notifications, you are on a plane, etc

I have found the difference of productivity between these modes to be immense. The mere idea that you *may* be interrupted by something makes you decidedly less productive than working in an environment that is guaranteed to be interruption-free.

The good news is there are easy things we can do to improve:

1. **Turn *****will***** into *****may**** *— e.g. move meetings where they do not interrupt work (end of the day, right before/after lunch), check email only twice a day at well-defined times.

1. **Turn *****may***** into *****can’t**** — *e.g.* *shut notifications and block distracting websites when doing focus work.

### ⏱️ Managing time

It is no secret that I love timeboxing my tasks. I wrote about this multiple times, and especially in this edition about time management 👇

[https://refactoring.fm/p/how-to-manage-your-time](https://refactoring.fm/p/how-to-manage-your-time)

Here is what I do:

1. 🗓️ **Timebox** — I create various *slots* of work during the day, and I put individual tasks on the calendar in those slots. Then, I religiously adhere to the schedule.

1. 🍅 **Pomodoros** — I work in sessions of 25 mins, followed by 5 mins of rest. I call these *pomodoros* because of the [Pomodoro Technique](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pomodoro_Technique). During a session, all notifications are off and I don’t allow interruptions. 

Pomodoros have also become a unit of work to me: I estimate tasks in number of pomodoros, and I know quite precisely how I much I can get done over a day, and a week. On a good day without calls, I can get ~12 pomodoros under my belt. In a week, I can reliably do 50.

I organize work and pomodoros in a schedule that stays pretty much the same everyday:

#### 1) 🌅 Morning (8:30 → 12:30)

- 1x Planning / Triage — I look at tasks and email, and put stuff in the calendar

- 4x Primary Task (Rock) — I spend 4 pomodoros on the main thing I have to do that day, that is usually advancing the weekly essay. 

- 1x Rest — I take a longer rest of ~30 mins at half morning. 

- 2x Secondary Task (Pebbles / Sand) — I spend the rest of the morning doing less intensive work, like replying to people on the community, social media, and emails.

#### 2) 🍔 Lunch Break (12:30 → 14:00)

I have lunch at ~12:30 and I don’t get back to work until 14-ish. Over lunch I usually watch videos from youtube channels I am subscribed to. Shout out to some of my favorites:

- [Dave](https://www.youtube.com/@DaveCamYT) and [Dan](https://www.youtube.com/@DanSuzuki) for sim racing

- [Hikaru](https://www.youtube.com/@GMHikaru) for chess

- [Real Life Lore](https://www.youtube.com/@RealLifeLore) for geopolitics

#### 3) 🌄 Afternoon (14:00 -> 18:00)

- 4x Primary task (Rock) — I spend two hours on another big task, which is usually not writing. It may be something to improve the library, or the community, or else. In general, it is often about “building stuff”, because I have found that it balances well with the other main task being writing.

- 1x Rest — like in the morning, I take a break around 16:00-16:30.

- 2x Secondary Task + Review — I close off the day with some small, misc tasks.

Of course this schedule doesn’t run exactly the same everyday. I have calls sometimes, and I try to schedule them in secondary task slots. Same goes for gym and personal/family duties.

My goal for the day is always to get the two primary tasks done. 

### ⚖️ Managers vs engineers

At this point many people will say: *ok may apply well to engineers, but what about managers?? They spend most of their time inside meetings*.

Well, they shouldn’t!

I believe that today, in 2023, managers should use plenty of *maker time* to improve processes, in turn reducing meetings. Managers can still see themselves as *makers *— just not makers of apps, but makers of work systems.

### 📌 Bottom line

So that’s it for today! The main takeaways from this piece are:

- **⚖️ Not all work is created equal **— when you are too tired to do creative work, you may still have some bandwidth for minor stuff. By accounting for that, you can get more done during your day.

- **🔋 Follow your energy** — create a schedule that matches what you want to achieve, and the level of energy you usually have during the day. At any given time, do the type of work that fits your energy.

- **🎨 Pursue creative work **— make sure a big chunk of your day goes into doing intense, creative work. That’s best for your motivation, your growth, and overall sense of fulfillment.

- **✂️ Split tasks **— break daunting work into multiple tasks, so you can delegate / automate some of them.

- **🙅‍♂️ Remove interruptions **— to get more done, put yourself in a situation where you *can’t* be interrupted. Work in small batches and take pauses (e.g. 5 mins every 30) so you 1) don’t get tunnel vision, and 2) may catch up with anything that needs your attention.

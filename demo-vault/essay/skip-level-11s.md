### ✍️ Post

Skip level 1:1s are, in my experience, one of the most underrated practices in engineering management. They are often added very late to the *arsenal* of a team’s ceremonies, while some companies do without them altogether.

Today I will talk about this, and I will do so by experimenting with a style of article that is a bit different than usual.

In fact, I am starting with a story—a real world one—and will use it as an anchor to cover why and how you should have skip level 1:1s on your team.

We will talk of:

- 📔 **Matt, Dave, and Jane** — our case study.

- 🔍 **What are skip level 1:1s **— definitions first.

- 🧑‍💻 **For reports **— goals and benefits for reports. 

- 👑 **For managers **— goals and benefits for managers.

- 📌 **How to have good skip level 1:1s **— frequency, agenda, how to act on items, and more.

Let’s go!

### 📔 Matt, Dave, and Jane

Many years ago, one of my best friends — let’s call him Matt — got his [M.Sc](http://m.sc/) in computer science with a focus on machine learning. Right after university, Matt joined a well known local company as a ML Engineer, with the promise he would work on the flagship company's product.

However, he was soon assigned to work on other, unrelated areas. He created simple internal tools (CRUD apps), refactored parts of the company website, and hammered bugs in legacy code he had little knowledge of.

After the first few months—where, he thought, he was just onboarding—Matt started inquiring his manager, Dave, about his path. Dave’s answers were unconvincing and Matt couldn’t figure out neither when he would start working with ML, nor the more general company roadmap.

After more months, he finally asked for a meeting with his manager’s manager — Jane. 

Jane was blissfully unaware of the whole situation, while Matt was already stressed and a bit disengaged. Jane seemed to sincerely care about that and said Matt could probably contribute to a ML project that was currently being reworked, starting next quarter.

Eventually, Matt was indeed assigned to some of that project’s tasks, but he still remained swamped with the rest of the internal tooling work, which Dave seemed to care more about. 

Over the following months Matt tried to juggle between both lanes of work. To do so he often overworked, while his relationship with Dave degraded considerably.

After a little more than a year since Matt had joined the company, he reached out to Jane again to tell her he was deeply unhappy about his job, his expectations weren’t met, and he couldn’t get along with his manager. He was about to quit.

Jane acted swiftly — after less than two weeks she moved Matt to a different team, under a different manager. Meanwhile, Matt took a long summer break.

After the break, Matt sat down with his new manager—an upbeat, warm guy—who told him about the great ML work they would do together, starting that day.

Matt smiled, thanked him for the opportunity, and resigned the same day.

Aside from the redacted names, this is a 100% true story.

Let’s review everything that went wrong with Matt’s experience, and let’s see why and how skip level 1:1s are related to this 👇

---

### 🔍 What are skip level 1:1s

Skip level 1:1s are recurring meetings between an employee and their manager’s manager.

While there is a ton of literature on traditional 1:1s, there isn’t nearly as much on skip levels. In fact, you may encounter wildly different scenarios:

- **Some companies don’t have them at all** — like in Matt’s case.

- **How companies have them varies wildly** — frequency, goal, structure, etc.

But why should you set them up in the first place? Let’s see what they are useful for, both from the reports and the managers’ angle.

### Utility for reports

As a report, a 1:1 with your manager’s manager acts as a *fallback* for any topic you can’t properly address—for any reason—with your actual manager.

That means, if the relationship with your manager is healthy, your skip level 1:1s may just feel like nice-to-have conversations. They may revolve around high level topics like company strategy or industry trends, and not have a big impact on your work.

I suspect this is why many small to mid-size companies feel that skip levels are a waste of time. Things seem just fine without them.

But things are fine until they aren’t.

The situation changes if you have **issues with your manager** that you are not able to sort out with them. For the better or worse, your manager is a wall — it may be hard to pass information through it if you don’t have a dedicated process to do so.

To put that in context let’s go back to our story 👇

#### Matt’s case

Matt has issues with his manager Dave, but he doesn’t have a recurring 1:1 with Jane — Dave’s manager.

Of course, Matt can reach out to Jane anytime, but he is not sure about it. Since he has no *official* relationship with Jane, and no official* *1:1, is that the right way of solving his problems? Maybe he should sort things out on his own? Maybe that is what is expected of him? Or maybe talking with Jane would be seen as *complaining*?

Also, what if that makes his relationship with Dave even *worse*? Dave will surely find out and confront him openly.

So, Matt is unsure, and delays talking with Jane until he can’t stand the situation anymore.

At the end of the story, Matt has talked with Jane twice: she acted rapidly both times, but in the end it was too late. Matt was burned out and quit anyway.

Now, talking with Jane is not just about the relationship with Dave. Matt has no clue about the company roadmap and whether what he is interested in—namely, machine learning work—is going to happen anytime soon, or is already happening elsewhere at the company. Dave is unclear on this and, to the best of Matt’s knowledge, he might genuinely not know it either.

So, Matt’s relationship with Jane is also important to fill this information gap.

Sometimes Matt may want to inquire about things which fly over Dave’s head, for whatever reason*.* Having a reliable channel with Jane is an efficient way to do so. 

### Utility for managers

For managers, the usefulness of regular skip level 1:1s stems directly from situations like Matt’s. 

Here is what Jane would have obtained by scheduling, for example, a quarterly or bi-monthly 1:1 with Matt:

- **Feedback loop about Matt’s wellbeing** — which would have allowed her to put out the fire before it spreads.

- **Creating alignment on company strategy **— by sharing roadmap information that Matt was not aware of. That may also trigger conversations on how to better trickle down such info in the future.

- **Feedback about Dave’s work** — Dave is Jane’s direct report, and Matt’s feedback is important (while not conclusive) for evaluating Dave’s work. Even more so if Jane can combine it with that of Dave’s other reports, whom she can talk with in other skip level 1:1s.  

- **General surveying **— so far this all came from Matt’s initiative, but Jane can also drive part of the conversation herself. She may ask for feedback on recent company initiatives, overall direction, or ask for precious bottom-up feedback on how to do things better.

If I managed to sell you on the usefulness of skip level 1:1s, let’s talk now about how to have them, in practice 👇

### How to have good skip level 1:1s

Let’s break down advice into some main areas:

- ⏱️ **Frequency **— how frequently you want to have these.

- 📌 **Agenda** — topics and questions to drive the conversation

- 🏷️ **Introducing them **— how to prepare people for having them.

- 🔨 **Acting on things **— how should you handle things? The relationship between you, your report, and your report’s report.

#### ⏱️ Frequency

This varies based on how many reports you have, with a range that goes from once every two months, to twice a year. A good sweet spot that works for most is **once a quarter **— so may attach the skip levels to other quarterly stuff, like OKRs.

Frequency for skip levels is particularly tricky, in both directions:

- **Do not have them too frequently** — even if you have time for, say, having monthly skip levels, these easily become unhealthy, because they tend to chip away at the manager's ownership. Monthly meetings give the report the impression you might take action even on small stuff, which is not what you should do. Quarterly ones, instead, are clearly for feedback, which is the main goal. However, chances are you wouldn’t have time to do them too frequently anyway. Skip levels pile up *fast*: say you have 4 reports, who in turn have 5 reports each. That is already 20 skip levels, which held monthly becomes ~1 / day.

- **Do not have them too infrequently **— say, once a year. That may still work in large companies for general surveying, but it basically defeats all other purposes, including, most importantly, defusing problems before they get too big. 

#### 📌 Agenda

With 1:1s, the general advice for leaders is always to *talk less* *and listen more. *However, there are  times where you want to drive the conversation towards specific topics, especially when things stagnate otherwise.

To do, here are the four main areas you want to cover, with questions to do so:

- 🗺️ **Strategy** — what is the least clear to you, in terms of our strategy and goals?

- 🪜 **Career** — what goals would you like to accomplish in the next 6-12 months? How can we help you with that?

- ❤️ **Wellbeing** — are you happy with your role? What could make it better for you?

- 🗳️ **Feedback** — what’s one thing we should start, stop and continue doing as a company?

#### 🏷️ Introducing skip levels

If you are introducing skip levels for the first time, be prepared for people to be confused about them. Both your reports (managers), and reports of your reports.

In both cases, you want to tell them exactly why you are having those.

For people you are having skip levels with, you may refer to the areas above. 

For your direct reports, other than reinstating the above, it makes sense to clarify that you are not going to overstep your boundaries and take away ownership from them. Skip levels are 90% about feedback — while most of the action stays on the manager’s side.

Which leads to acting on things 👇

#### 🔨 Acting on things

As the one *holding *skip level 1:1s, how should you act on things that come up in conversation? 

It may seem weird, but 80% of the times the best answer to anything you hear is "**have you talked about this to your manager?**"

What follows is most always useful, and you want to learn more about it before you take any action. A “yes” may imply the manager didn’t act on this, while a “no” may hint at some communication/interpersonal problem.

The trickiest part of skip levels is being helpful—so that meetings feel valuable and the report keeps coming with good ideas / feedback—while not stepping onto the managers’ toes. 

It's kind of an art, even more than regular 1:1s.

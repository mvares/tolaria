### Impact and influence

📹 [**Video chapter**](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=6wRavIuI-AQ&t=750s)

What do you need to grow your career?

While some specific skills may depend on your role, at a high level, most people get promoted by displaying **impact**. This is widely acknowledged for managers, but it is largely the same for individual contributors, too. Your tech skills are only useful as long as they create value for the business.

Having good impact expands your *influence*. In turn, influence and trust enable you to take on bigger responsibilities, which in turn enable higher impact, in a virtuous cycle.

![image](../assets/ceb3dfc516a24e9e.png)

I believe that chasing impact is a healthy mental model for your career. For two main reasons:

- **It challenges your comfort zone **— higher impact usually means more ambitious initiatives. As long as you go after bigger things, it is less likely to stagnate.

- **It treats your skills as a means to an end **— skills are meant to increase your leverage. As long as you frame them this way, you will likely make the right calls about what you want to learn. E.g. you will likely embrace AI (to chase higher leverage) instead of being afraid of it.

So, it’s great to wanting to improve some skills, but always ask yourself: how does this help to create more value? That is, what skills enable impact? 👇

### ↔️ Get broad first

📹 [**Video chapter**](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=6wRavIuI-AQ&t=525s)

Maybe this is a bias from my time as a founder, but I strongly believe in chasing ample **autonomy** in any job you have. To me, high autonomy creates **ownership**, which in turn amplifies impact.

I have found that *autonomy *is enabled by two factors:

- **Adequate skills **— e.g. as a developer, being proficient on the whole stack so that you can own features from top to bottom.

- **Healthy work environment **— a working process where people are given the opportunity to own meaningful tasks.

So, I am a believer in chasing *full-stack *experiences, especially early in your career. When I say *full-stack,* it’s not just about the tech — it’s about doing *more:*

- Making meaningful decisions about the product

- Talking with customers

- Being on-call for your code

- Collaborating with designers

- …

Going *wide* early in your career has some strong benefits:

- 📈 **Efficiency **— for most skills, Pareto applies: you can go from 0 to 80 in just a few years, and then it takes 10+ years to get from 80 to 100. So it is a better deal to spend some initial time to build a few 80s, before committing to 100%-ing one.

- ➡️ **Direction **— getting in touch with different things helps you figure out what you really enjoy and want to get better at. You develop a more balanced perspective and avoid bias 👇

- 🔑 **No lock-in **— when you specialize too early in something, you risk to get cornered into a *local optimum* that is difficult to escape. You have all your eggs in one basket, which also makes you less resilient to changes in the industry.

> *I got stuck for a long time as a PHP contract developer because the market was so hot and in the UK the tax treatment very favourable for contract vs permanent, but I always knew I wanted to get back into management, having led a team at a startup back in 2011-12. But it was never financially worthwhile! It was only Brexit + BoJo's November election + IR35 changes that killed the contract market dead long enough I felt I had to get a perm job. I'm not sure what the lesson is here; I was definitely stagnating in my last couple of dev jobs and it really turned out to be a boon to be forced to change.

— *Kendrick Curtis — Lead EM at Codacy

So, if to you this seems like an ode to generalists, it kind of is, especially in your early years. 

To build your own T *shape*, it is more effective to go after the horizontal leg *before* the vertical one, which it also informs you on *which* vertical leg to build later.

- [Untitled](https://www.notion.so/ac0c1de77643483eb07c7af244291f92)

So what jobs should you chase for this?

### 💼 Choosing jobs

There is common advice that says:

- **Go with startups** — if you want to have more responsibility and a generalist experience.

- **Go with big tech** — if you want to specialize into something and earn more.

There is some truth in this but it is hard to generalize — especially because neither startups nor big tech are cohesive categories that always behave the same way.

So, when people ask me for advice about choosing jobs, I tell them to stay open and judge more based on the conversations they have during interviews, with people from their future team, rather than the overall company *reputation*.

Things vary a lot, also within the same company. There are lean teams inside big orgs that own high-growth products from top to bottom, operating like a startup, and then there are convoluted, [bureaucratic experiences](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=6wRavIuI-AQ&t=1540s) with long chains of commands in order to release any small change.

Also, not all startups make for good experiences. Some are messy, excellence is nowhere to be found, and the only thing you will learn is how to burn out.

So, my advice is to spend considerable time *talking* with your prospective employers, and *asking a ton of questions*:

- *How does the team work? Tell me about more the product dev process*

- *What are some ambitious projects you are working on?*

- *Are you raising money? What are the plans for the company?*

- *Is the company growing? Are you hiring for other roles?*

Think like an investor in the company, because you kind of are. You are investing your time in it and you are betting that it will be good for your growth and career.

### 🔀 Changing jobs

📹 [**Video chapter**](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=6wRavIuI-AQ&t=380s)

If your early years are for broad *learning*, then you will probably benefit from changing jobs often.

We covered this during the interview and labelled it as *divisive* advice, but it is just common sense: if you want to see different things—from a technical, product, and organizational point of view—you just need different companies.

How long you should stay depends on how much you can learn. A couple of years are usually a good amount of time to have good impact and learn some new tricks. In healthy, high-growth contexts, you can benefit from staying more, while you can stay less when things are not going the right way.

As a hard and fast rule, I would always stay at least 18 months in any position that is not extremely pointless or toxic. Less than that, the onboarding tax you pay is just too high and cuts into the value you can create for the company and for yourself. Also, a long string of shorts experiences doesn’t make for a good impression with future employers.

More advice on when to quit your job 👇

Later in your career, when you get into senior positions, I would look instead for environments where you can stay longer and have a long term impact. Leadership and upper management positions, in fact: 

- ⏳ **Need more time to create value** — initiatives are bigger, planning cycles are longer, and you just need a longer period of time to accomplish anything.

- 🤝 **Are built on strong trust** — every time you change jobs you lose all the trust you built in your previous experience, which is crucial to advance to leadership roles. In that respect, changing jobs too often can even *slow down* your career. Many companies do not even hire for e.g. VP or Director roles, because it’s too hard to vet candidates at that level. They hire for a level below and possibly promote the candidate fast.

### 🚪 Being in the right room

So, growing your career is a matter of always putting yourself in the *right room*.

There is a famous saying that goes: *if you are the smartest person in the room, you are in the wrong room. *But you can go a bit farther and ask yourself: what does the *ideal *room look like? Who should be there?

My answer is:

1. Someone you deeply admire for their **skills** and from whom you can **learn from**.

1. Someone who is receptive to your needs, sponsors your work and **gives you the opportunity** to shine.

1. Peers you **get along well** and with a diverse set of skills with respect to yours.

As long as your work has all three, you are set!

### 🍀 The Four Ps

Thiago created a small framework that you can use as an *engine* for growing your career, either in management or technical tracks.

It is called **the four Ps **which are the areas you should look at to positively impact the team/company you are working in: 

1. **Platform **— it is the hard tech. The codebase, infra, CI/CD, and engineering practices. You should master those and build your contribution on top of them.

1. **Product **— it is the application from the user perspective. Their experience and what they are able to accomplish. Focusing on the product means delivering value to the final users.

1. **Process **— it is the *how*. It is the culture, rituals, systems, and metrics. You impact these by optimizing and *strenghtening *the process.

1. **People **— this is about *nurturing *your team and your relationships via mentorship (giving advice), coaching (supporting personal development), and sponsorship (giving opportunities).

While the most successful engineers and managers excel at all four, you should probably focus on them at different times:

- **At lower levels** — *Platform* and *Product*** **are the primary drivers for growth and promotions. You want to look for impact on specific areas of the product and of the codebase.

- **At higher levels** — *Process* and *People*** **is where you should focus your attention. In fact, your role becomes a *multiplier* whose impact is primarily measured in how well you *enable* others to do their best work.

### ⚖️ Learning more about yourself

Everyone has **strengths and weaknesses**, things they like and don’t like.

Sometimes, especially early in your career, it is hard to separate those from what you are just more or less expert in. But over time, if you pay attention, you will just know what are the things you can *improve*, but will never be your *forte*, and those for which you are more of a natural.

I will tell you a couple of mine:

- I tend to be a *people-pleaser* and I am not comfortable with *conflict* — I can handle it, I can receive and deliver tough feedback, I am ok with hard conversations, but these all cost me a lot energy. So, I don’t really shine in environments where the culture is extremely direct, or people are challenged too often.

- I am good at picking up new things and owning something from top to bottom — I have been a founder for 10+ years so it’s just how I am wired. I am not scared by things I don’t know and if you give me something to do, I will just find a way to do it. So I thrive in flexible environments where I am not *pidgeon-holed* into one narrow function, and I can be creative.

When you play to your *strengths*, your energy is multiplied, and so is your progress.

> *Accepting promotions without being aware of this can land you in a position where you’re doing more of something you aren’t good at, or something you don’t enjoy.*

### 🗺️ Making a plan

So, just like with your skills, your initial years are best used to figure out what these strengths and weaknesses are, so you can later double down on the former.

And *doubling down* includes making a plan about where you want to go.

I know that some people are particularly *allergic* to making plans for their lives. The answer I can give, based on what I learned about myself, is that the actual *plans* turn out to be crap sometimes (but you get better over time), but planning is almost always useful.

At the beginning, it is useful as an act of introspection about what you want to achieve. And at the end, it is useful for a retrospective of what went right or wrong. By doing these two things regularly, you are naturally going to achieve more in your life.

I maintain a *triple* plan for myself, that looks more or less like this:

#### 1) **5-years plan** 

This should be a rough sketch of what my ideal life might be in 5 years, across all the axes: work, family, health, relationships, personal stuff, etc. I tend to be optimistic about this, because 5 years is a lot of time and we often underestimate what we can achieve over a long time.

Recently, I also learned about the [*Odissey Planning*](https://medium.com/@belinda.rowse/odyssey-planning-re-framing-the-five-year-plan-7a0948707615) method, and I tried to incorporate it 👇

> *Odissey Plan template:*

#### 2) **1-year plan**

This is a more practical plan that advances the 5-years one in a meaningful way. I write down a few main themes (2-3 max) and both some qualitative and quantitative goals.

I describe the what and almost never the *how.*

#### 3) 3-months plan

This is like personal OKRs. Three to five main things to accomplish for the current quarter. I started doing them in Q2 2020 👇

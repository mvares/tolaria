# Laura Tacho – Engineering Productivity and Developer Experience

Here is the agenda for today:

1. 🗺️ **Engineering metrics frameworks** — differences and how to pick the right onw

1. 🗳️ **The importance of human feedback** — because metrics can only go so far.

1. 🔧 **How to create a developer experience program **— a step-by-step guide to setup a virtuous process to improve your dx.

Let’s dive in!

---

### 1) 🗺️ Engineering Metrics Frameworks ([video](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=ylOXt85zsjo&t=358s))

If you are new to engineering metrics and you try to educate yourself, the first thing you will probably meet is *frameworks, *the most popular of which are: DORA, SPACE, and DevEX.

They are all born out of *research* into how engineering teams work, but they are about very different things.

1. 🚚 **DORA** — is about DevOps metrics. It’s four KPIs you should take into account to measure your delivery process.

1. 💫 **SPACE** — draws a holistic picture of how engineering teams work, along five axes: Satisfaction & Wellbeing, Performance, Activity, Communication & Collaboration, Efficiency & Flow. Each item is evaluated on individual, team, and system levels, and features a number of recommended metrics.

1. 🔧 **DevEx** — organizes developer experience into three key dimensions: *feedback loops*, *cognitive load*, and *flow state*.

Laura argues that the three frameworks are useful for different things. 

DORA is specific for DevOps, not dev productivity in general. This makes it useful in those cases where your bottleneck is around delivery, which may or may not be the case.

SPACE is sophisticated and non-prescriptive: it gives you all the ingredients but you have to put them together yourself in a recipe that works for your team.

DevEx is simpler and the most actionable for most teams. Here is more about its three dimensions:

- 🔄 **Feedback loops** — is quickly developers receive feedback on their actions, including the quality of that feedback.

- 🧠 **Cognitive load** — is the mental effort and processing capacity required by developers to code effectively. It emphasizes the need to minimize interruptions and streamline processes.

- 🌊 **Flow state** — it is the optimal mental state where developers experience high motivation, creativity, and productivity.

### 2) 🗳️ The Importance of Human Feedback ([video](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=ylOXt85zsjo&t=1005s))

As engineers, we are used to deal with *software* *systems*. We instrument them and debug them looking at logs and numbers.

So, a problem with engineering metrics is that we may be tempted to treat our *teams* like software, and fix their problems only looking at *telemetry* data. No opinions involved.

The difference is: **systems can’t talk to us, but our teams can**, and should.

So, to evaluate developer experience, the first thing you should do is to have good conversations with your teammates:

- 💬 **1:1s** — ask what is the one thing that makes it harder to do their job.

- 📮 **Surveys** — collect opinions and ideas about making things better.

- 🍻 **Retrospectives **— discuss ideas and improvements with the team.

### 3) 🔧 How to create a dev ex program ([video](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=ylOXt85zsjo&t=1678s))

A big chunk of our chat went into understanding how you can use this information to improve the actual dev experience of your team. Laura proposes a four-step process:

#### 1) Gather data 📑

- **Collect opinions and information **— through conversations, surveys, and of course tools.

- **Create a shortlist of action items** — that are the best candidates to have the highest impact.

- **Build a business case** — figure out what matters the most to the business (is it speed? revenues? growth?) and how those initiatives can help.

#### 2) Gather people 🍻

Laura says you need two people to start a program:

- 🏦 **Sponsor** — the person who is signing the budget (e.g. the CTO)

- 👑 **Champion** — the person who is the DRI and is leading this project. Might be an EM who is also trying to influence other EMs

You will also need a **charter** — that is, a plan of what you are going to do, and what you need for it.

Finally, create a map of your **allies & enemies **— the people who are happy to help vs those you need to change their mind.

#### 3) Start small 🤏

Pick a team to run a pilot with. It should be a small team, with some issues you think you can fix, made by people who are well-disposed towards the initiative.

Identify issues, fix a couple, and **demonstrate improvements**. E.g. last quarter it took 3 hours to resolve an incident, this quarter it’s 1 hour.

Create a **success story** that can be showcased to increase trust and unlock a wider mandate. 

#### 4) Land and expand 📈

Make developer experience a staple of the engineering teams’ work. Not all work is created equal, though:

- 🔴 **Big items **— large dx projects should be negotiated like normal product items. If you have an “engineering roadmap”, it is probably destined to fail. To get things done, you need to think like a PM, and you can even *partner* with them to get help. Ask them: what should you demonstrate to make this project viable?

- 🟢 **Normal maintenance** — set out a *normal* allocation for dx. It can be around 15% of dev work. Give developers slack and autonomy to go for things that take ~half a day.

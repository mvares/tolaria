Welcome back! This is the second part of a [two-part article](https://refactoring.fm/p/good-hiring-process-1?s=w) on how to create a good hiring process.

In the first part we focused on how to plan for growth and source candidates. This time, we will cover the last two major areas: 

- 🔍 **Selecting candidates** — how to have effective interviews.

- 🤝 **Converting candidates** — how to eventually win them over.

Again, this topic sparked the biggest debate *ever* on the [Refactoring community](https://refactoring.substack.com/p/community). I am grateful to have CTOs, EMs, VPs and Engineers all weighing in on what a good hiring process should look like.

![Thirty-one comments on this one is seriously great 🙏](../assets/f4175b4947374839.png)

Let’s dive in 👇

### 🔍 Selecting Candidates

Also known as: the interview process. This is the beefiest and most complex part of any hiring process. It involves the most people and takes the most time.

You want two things out of your interview process:

1. 🥇 **Select the** **right people**

1. ⚡ **Be fast**

These goals are, of course, at odds. By spending more time on selection, you will reduce uncertainty and will get more confident about good candidates. However, you will also 1) make your hiring cycle longer, 2) put more work on your team, and 3) lose candidates who might get other opportunities in the process.

So, how do you navigate this tradeoff? I use two rules:

- ⚖️ **The “hell yeah or no” rule**

- ⏱️ **The “two weeks” rule**

#### ⚖️ Hell yeah or no

In 2009, Derek Sivers wrote this super short and super influential [blog post](https://sive.rs/hellyeah) where he advices people to commit only to activities for which they are absolutely excited (*hell yeah*). A simple *yes*, or *maybe,* should turn into a *no*.

This advice can be applied verbatim to other areas of life too, like [dating](https://markmanson.net/fuck-yes), and hiring.

In other words, you want to optimize your process to **avoid false positives**, rather than false negatives. With hiring, it is most often ok to pass on a good candidate, but it can be an absolute disaster to bring in a bad one.

For many, the natural response to this is to make more interviews. This isn’t necessarily effective and brings diminishing returns. You may rather just raise the bar of **how excited you should be** about someone in order to bring them in — whatever your interview process is.

#### 🗓️ The “Two Weeks” rule

Disclaimer here: I have seen and worked directly mostly with startups and companies between 10 and 200 employees. So, what I am about to say may not apply to the FAANGs of the world and orgs with heavy HR and compliance processes.

I believe that, in normal conditions, you should keep your **time-to-offer within two weeks**. That means the amount of time from initial application to job offer.

If this seems brutal to you, consider that:

1. [Top candidates are gone in 10 days](https://officevibe.com/blog/12-recruiting-stats#:~:text=The%20best%20candidates%20are%20off%20the%20market%20within%2010%20days) — that is the time it takes for top 10% talent to get a good offer.

1. In hot markets, companies are even *faster* than this — Yurii Mykytyn, Director of Engineering at Rebbix, shares his experience with the Eastern Europe market 👇

> *Before the war, Eastern Europe and Ukraine in particular was a boiling hiring market. Salaries almost doubled over the course of two years and bigger software companies were settings KPI's for themselves like ****an offer in 24h****.

Such things as a home assignment or multitude of interviews were not an option, because during the same time, the candidate could get offers from 5 companies with zero effort.

So in general, our recruitment process looks like:
– 15 min screening
– 1.5h tech + team interview
– 1h final culture interview+offer.
*
*SLA would be ****7 working days**** from pre-screen to final decision (hire/no hire) per candidate*

So, how do you design an interview process that allows you to be fast *and *accurate?

Over the past month I have reviewed tens of selection processes. The first thing to notice is there are basically three type of interviews:

1. 📑 **Screening Interview**

1. 🔨 **Technical Interview**

1. 🍻 **Cultural Interview**

Let’s see what’s their goal and how you can organize them:

### 📑 Screening Interview

The screening interview is the first touchpoint with the candidate after their application.

It has three major goals:

1. **Cut down** —** **the number of applicants

1. **Provide context **— about the process / position

1. **Build rapport **— with the candidate

It should be run by recruiters / hiring managers, who will stay the point of reference of the candidate throughout the process.

Good screenings are just chats between two humans rather than a first round interview. Candidates tell more about themselves, their previous achievements and what they are looking for in their next role. The recruiter tells them more about the role and the team.

Here are a few topics to cover in the screening 👇

- **About the position** — responsibilities, vision, team.

- **Interview process** — how it will unfold and what you need to prepare about.

- **Salary range** — and/or salary expectations from the candidate.

- **Interest in the job** — why are you interested in the position?

- **When you can start** — notice period from current company, personal needs, etc.

- **Can / should you relocate?** — is the position location-based? Is the candidate willing to relocate?

These questions help both parties figure out if it is worthy to go on with the interview process.

Most of this alignment, though, can be built before-hand via the job post, with massive saving of time and energy by everyone. 

A **good job post** that is informative on these points 1) reduces the number of candidates who are unfit, and 2) simplifies the negotiation later on.

- Do you help with relocation? Great! Let them know in the job post.

- Do you have a streamlined, no-stress interview process? Awesome, describe how it works in advance.

- Same goes for the salary range. Include it in the post to 1) get more applications, and 2) make negotiation simpler.

If you want to learn more about creating good job posts, check out this previous Refactoring article:

- 🌀 [**How to Write a Great Job Post**](https://refactoring.fm/p/job-posts)

### 🔨 Technical interview

The technical interview is [...]

Let me start by noting that this is often incredibly stressful for engineers, and it doesn’t need to be so.

A dear friend of mine recently got hired by a FAANG — I followed the process closely as we often chatted for advice and/or just personal support. He went through 10+ interviews over more than 2 months. To prepare for them, he studied on a dedicated book and furiously did tens of [LeetCodes](https://leetcode.com/) and other exercises which, for the most part, resembled nothing like he had ever done at work, nor anything he will ever do.

Now, the very idea that people need ad-hoc preparation for interviews is borderline ridiculous. Even more than that — it is counterproductive. It scares many talented folks away as they are not willing to put in the effort or endure this kind of stress.

Let’s review the most common styles of technical interviews, with pros and cons. There are four main ones:

#### **LeetCode-like exercises**

Popularized by the eponymous platform, and focused on algorithmic + data structures challenges. 

They are extremely popular in large companies, and extremely despised by engineers. 

I can’t recommend anyone to use them, as they are hardly relevant for actual work, and people can get good at them just by doing many.

#### **Home assignments**

Tasks assigned to the candidate to be done at home. They usually require a few hours of work — more than that is unfeasible, less than that you would just do it in an interview.

Home assignments are good for several reasons:

- They can be designed to test something that resembles a **real-world task**.

- They can be done **asynchronously** — removing **stress** from the candidate and freeing up time to your team.

- They serve as a great basis for a **following technical interview** where you discuss the task itself.

The main drawback of such tests is that they are **high churn **— they have a high share of non-completions — and they also make your **hiring cycle longer**, as you have to give some days to the candidate to complete the test.

[Julien Evano](https://www.linkedin.com/in/julienevano/?originalSubdomain=au), Director of Engineering at IXUP, commented on this 👇

> *In a high demand market, we received a lot of feedback to remove the take-home test. However, we still see a lot of value to assess a candidate on the ability to code on their own and on several occasions helped us to filter candidates early in the process.*

Personally, I am a strong supporter of *take-homes*. Upsides are strong, while downsides are dubious. Candidates who churn have probably low conviction or would have failed the test anyway. 

#### System design

This is an interview where the candidate is tasked with the design of a sizeable piece of a (web) application.

Based on the role and company, such use cases might be large-scale, require a distributed system, and resemble a piece of the actual product architecture.

For mid-to-senior roles, this interview is the real deal.

Here you can go through the engineering mental process of the candidate, how they approach business requirements, circumvent constraints, and more. You can also design use cases based on real problems you had to solve in the past, to figure out how the candidate would approach them.

To learn more about system design interviews, the best resource I know is [ByteByteGo](https://bytebytego.com/) by [Alex Xu](https://twitter.com/alexxubyte).

#### Pair Programming

In a pair programming interview, the candidate goes through some coding task together with the interviewer. Here are a couple of common choices:

- **Follow up to the home assignment** — a new requirement is added on top of the previous excercise.

- **Code reading + small task **— the candidate reads and interprets some code from a real service, and is requested to add a small tweak.

How is this different from your regular LeetCode? In two ways:

- **Context** — the exercise, rather than being abstract, carries relevant context, either from the assignment or from a real use case. 

- **Pairing** — the candidate collaborates with the interviewer, who may even take control to show things and write some bits.

### 🍻 Cultural interview

The cultural interview is in many ways similar to the screening one, but is held by a manager rather than HR. This might be the future manager of the new hire, or, in small companies, a founder / leadership role.

Such interview covers values, future plans by the candidate, what motivates them, career growth, and answers any further question the candidate may have about the company.

It is not a very *selective* step, but it is important nonetheless. This is where managers have their say about their prospective new reports, or CTOs have the final word on engineering positions.

### 🤝 Converting Candidates

A couple of months ago I spoke with a hiring manager at a FAANG-league company everyone knows. He told me that only 1 every 6 offers they send out are accepted. 

One out of six.

Converting candidates happens at the end but is the **result of the whole process**.

So, what are the most important factors for this? Let me start with the one that shouldn’t: **the offer**.

If you fail to convert a candidate because your offer doesn’t meet their expectations, it is a massive fail of your process. You have two easy entry points to cover compensation ranges early on: the job post and the screening interview. These are where you need to make sure that mutual expectations can be met. Conversely, you don’t want to invest tens of combined hours by recruiters, engineers, and managers, just to shoot yourself in the foot a few steps before the finish line.

In my experience, here is what matters most, instead:

- **Speed **— by now you already know the *two-weeks* rule and that top candidates are gone in ~~60 seconds~~ 10 days. Speed always wins deals. A process that looks like 1) screening, 2) 2x technical interviews, 3) cultural interview, and 4) offer, works for most companies and you can squeeze everything in two weeks.

- **Context** — candidates form an opinion about what it is like to work at your company based on the interview process. So you want to make this relevant. Have interviews held by their future manager and team members. Create tasks that resemble the work on the actual product.

### 💬 Hiring processes from the Community

Finally, here are some examples of real-world hiring processes from folks in the [Refactoring Community](https://refactoring.substack.com/p/community) 👇

#### [Ivan Aponte](https://www.linkedin.com/in/apontedev/?originalSubdomain=co), Team Lead at IPCom

> *What we have done in the past year is a “hire fast” approach. We just do 4 steps: 
– 15 min screening with the recruiter
– small kind easy take-home homework
– 1 hour interview with one of the leads
– If hired a compliance check (medical, legal etc).
We usually have an answer within 3 days of the interview and the person starts as soon as compliance checks come in (this usually takes 3-5 days).
The whole process from the first screening to hired takes three weeks at most.*

#### [Luca Marchi](https://www.linkedin.com/in/luca-marchi-16111038/), Software Engineer at Picnic

> *This is the hiring process in my current company
– CV screening by recruiters
– LeetCode exercise
– Home assignment
– Pair programming session, followed by a whiteboard session (for these two we usually present problems that we faced, describing them from a business prospective)
– Lastly, there is a final feedback session with the recruiters and the engineers.*

#### [Yurii Mykytyn](https://www.linkedin.com/in/meako/), Director of Engineering at Rebbix

> *Our recruitment process looks like:
– 15 min screening
– 1.5h tech+team interview
– 1h final culture interview+offer.*

#### [Pamela Gotti](https://www.linkedin.com/in/pamelagotti/), CTO at Credimi

> *The hiring process looks like this:
– active candidates get a message from hr for a 30 min intro call
– coding challenge - that is more an architecture exercise, it is a very open questione that is the base for the technical interview
– 1h coding interview with 2 engineers
– 1h interview with 2 other people from the team (usually product manager and eng manager). 
– Hr jumps in here too to sort out any topic that might be still open like salary expectations or personal requests.*

#### [Julien Evano](https://www.linkedin.com/in/julienevano/?originalSubdomain=au), Head of Engineering at IXUP

> *Interview Process - taking 3-4 weeks on average
– Screening - CV review + phone screen to verify if the candidate matches the job description and overall base competencies.
– Take-Home test - ~2 hours coding/scenario challenge that is optional and depends on the role and hiring manager (e.g. start from scratch a mini frontend project to calculate tax from gross salary)
– Functional interview - situation/scenario-based interview often done on a whiteboard (e.g. system design interview)
– Cultural interview - to assess the base competencies and the culture match between the candidate and the company.
– Offer - to close the process as quick as possible.*

#### [Dulce Zavaleta](https://www.linkedin.com/in/dulcezavaleta/), Engineering Manager at AppSmiths

> *In our company [...] mostly we value being fast, if we find out FAST that this person is not a good fit for our company, we sadly part ways with them.
Basically we do the following:
– We always look for talent, but if talent comes to us, we take a look at their CV first. Even when CV is not flashy enough, we test culture fit and willingness to learn.
– If the person does not have CS career, we test them with some technical challenge first. We get to see how the candidate thinks and the level of thinking.
– We want people who are willing to listen and exchange ideas. [...] Me as the manager and the most experienced people in the team get to meet the new candidate, so we get to know his / her values and mindset.
– If CV is good enough and values seem a good fit, the candidate stays a 3 month period. If they get to deliver and they have good progress, the candidate stays. If not, we can part ways any time from month 0 to 3.*

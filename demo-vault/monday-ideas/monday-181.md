# Monday #181

### 1) 🔖 The Engineering Manager Quadrants

In summer we published one of our deeper articles ever, about [engineering manager archetypes](https://refactoring.fm/p/the-engineering-manager-archetypes). It was the result of months of work by my friend [Thiago Ghisi](https://www.linkedin.com/in/thiagoghisi).

The main idea is that managers naturally fall along two axes: how they handle **Stability vs Change**, and whether they default to **Execution vs People**. 

Putting them on a 2×2 matrix, you can identify four leadership archetypes:

1. ** **🧰 **Operator (*****Stability + Execution*****) **— focused on predictability, process discipline, and operational control. Think robust project plans, clear milestones, and minimal surprises.

1. 🪴 **Guide (*****Stability + People*****) **— prioritizes nurturing, safety, and team culture. This is the mentor figure, ensuring strong relationships, skill development, and psychological safety above all.

1. 🏁 **Driver (*****Change + Execution*****)** — embodies velocity, ambition, and technical or product transformation. These managers thrive on pushing the envelope, delivering new features or bold initiatives quickly.

1. 🔋 **Catalyst (*****Change + People*****)** — focuses on storytelling, cultural shifts, and strategic influence. Catalysts excel at inspiring teams to embrace new directions and adapt to evolving org priorities.

![image](../assets/2a2bdf02815c80fc.png)

By identifying where you land, you can see how you naturally handle the flux that defines engineering management—and consciously tweak your style when circumstances demand it.

- ↔️ **The Horizontal Axis** — reveals whether you rely on execution and *hard power*, or if you scale by uplifting people, culture, and *soft power*. 

- ↕️ **The Vertical Axis** — measures how you approach the system: do you optimize it for steadiness or keep pushing it to evolve?

These aren't rigid labels, but rather your *center of gravity.* Great EMs can shift between quadrants as situations demand, but knowing your **home quadrant** helps you 1) leverage your strengths and 2) partner with others whose archetypes complement yours.

### 2) 🔄 The Four Stages of Habits

There is a small handful of books that were not only enjoyable the first time I read them, but over time grew into *reference books* that I go back to for advice, guidance, or just a refresher.

One of these is Atomic Habits by James Clear. I think about Atomic Habits every time I feel some of my routines is falling through the tracks, or I am not able to follow through some commitment.

One of the most useful ideas in the book is that habits are made up of four stages:

1. 🟣 **Cue** — the trigger that makes the brain recognize an opportunity for reward.

1. 🔴 **Craving** — the desire to achieve the reward. Occurs once the cue indicates the opportunity.

1. 🟡 **Response** — the action you take to get the reward.

1. 🟢 **Reward** — the resulting benefit gained from the response.

For a habit to stick, you need all four. You can also organize these stages into two phases: problem + solution:

- **Create the problem** → Cue + Craving

- **Create the solution** → Response + Reward

So, the key to building good habits is to act on those stages:

1. Cue → make it **obvious**

1. Craving → make it **attractive**

1. Response → make it **easy**

1. Reward → make it **satisfying**

Likewise, if you want to break bad habits you do the reverse:

1. Cue → make it **invisible**

1. Craving → make it **unattractive**

1. Response → make it **difficult**

1. Reward → make it **unsatisfying**

### 3) 🎙️ Junior devs turn into senior devs

At the beginning of the year [I interviewed one of my heroes, Martin Fowler](https://refactoring.fm/p/growing-the-development-forest-with?utm_source=publication-search), on the podcast.

We discussed, among other things, the way AI may affect how we grow developers, and Martin said a simple thing that I keep thinking about:

> ***“One of the most important properties of a junior developer is the fact that you can turn them into a senior developer.”***

For as trivial as this observation might seem, it feels to me that many companies don’t fully grasp the reality of it.

Having a *pipeline* that reliably turns junior engineers into seniors over a few years is an incredible asset, that single-handedly solves 80% of your hiring problems. And given that hiring is, quite often, the #1 problem of engineering teams, I would say this is a pretty important topic.

Junior engineers are cheaper and faster to hire than seniors, you can create reliable pipelines to do so (e.g. from universities, bootcamps, and more), and you can grow them *exactly* into what you need them to be. 

Creating an environment where Junior engineers grow and thrive may require some initial investment, but it leads your team to *automatically* spawn seniors on a regular basis, to the point where you may get away with hiring *mostly* junior engineers.

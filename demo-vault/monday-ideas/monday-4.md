### 1) 🎽 The Iron Triangle of Planning

Consider the [Iron Triangle of Planning](https://www.atlassian.com/agile/agile-at-scale/agile-iron-triangle), proposed by Martin Barnes in 1969.

It is based on the assumption that **Scope = Time * Resources**, and supposedly you can change one dimension to impact the others.

![image](../assets/2b1713ed84834a18.png)

However, trading between these constraints is not always possible.

In particular, **resources** (or budget) do not often have an immediate impact on the other two.

In fact, you can't usually say "*I want to pay twice as much so you can deliver the app in half of the time*", because: 

1. In most projects this is straight impossible (9 girls cannot make a baby in 1 month)

1. Resources are not responsive (new people on the project need onboarding, coordination, etc.)

To deliver a project faster, your best bet is to act on one of the two other dimensions: 1) **reducing the scope**, or 2) **increasing the time for delivery**

### 2) 🔨 The three foundations of tech strategy

A good technical strategy is a *superpower.* You can design a good one by reflecting on three elements:

**1) **🏢 **Your Company **— what can engineering do to support your **company mission**? What should you invest in to create a **core asset **for your company, generating a hard-to-copy, **competitive advantage**?

*Example: Zoom built a competitive advantage with proprietary streaming tech.*

**2) **📱 **Your Product **— how can engineering support your **product strategy** to deliver a superior experience that drives the company mission forward? What kind of products can be **enabled** by a strong technical strategy?

*Example: Uber built additional product lines (Eats, Freight) by leveraging their fleet management tech.*

**3) **👕 **Your Team **— How can you leverage your engineering **team's strengths** to create something that benefits your company long term?

*Example: *[*Airbnb failed the React Native transition*](https://medium.com/airbnb-engineering/sunsetting-react-native-1868ba28e30a)* because (among other things) their mobile team skills were more invested in native tech.*

More about designing technical strategies 👇

### 3) 🎒 Keep your time-to-offer within two weeks

You should strive to keep your **time-to-offer within two weeks**. That is the amount of time from the initial application to the job offer.

If this seems brutal to you, consider that:

1. [Top candidates are gone in 10 days](https://officevibe.com/blog/12-recruiting-stats#:~:text=The%20best%20candidates%20are%20off%20the%20market%20within%2010%20days) — that is the time it takes for the top 10% talent to get a good offer.

1. In hot markets, companies are even *faster* than this — [Yurii Mykytyn](https://www.linkedin.com/in/meako/), Director of Engineering, shares his experience with the Eastern European market 👇

> Before the war, Eastern Europe and Ukraine in particular was a boiling hiring market. Salaries almost doubled over the course of two years and bigger software companies were settings KPI's for themselves like an offer in 24h.
